Write two emails: (a) a polite request, then (b) a gentle reminder for the same ask. Reveal the model to compare. Modal-question frame in (a); face-saving nudge in (b).

Model (a) — modal-question frame + explicit deadline:

Subject: Q3 sales report — could you send by Friday?

Hi Mai,

Could you send the Q3 sales report by Friday? modal "could" + soft deadline — neutral, professional

I'd appreciate it if you could include the regional breakdown. formal; thanks built into the ask

Thanks,
Quan

Model (b) — remind the inbox, not the person (no "you didn't"):

Subject: Re: Q3 sales report — following up

Hi Mai,

Just following up on the Q3 sales report. present participle, no "you" — neutral nudge

Just a gentle reminder that we need it by Friday. face-saving; re-states the deadline, not the failure

Let me know if anything's blocking you.
Thanks,
Quan

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
No modal politeness → "send me the file" reads as a commandPrepend a modal-question frame: Could you / Would you be able to / Would you mind.
Face-saving culture → never follow up (reminding = causing "mất mặt")Use the gentle-nudge formula — remind the inbox, not the person: Just following up / a gentle reminder.
Blunt follow-up → "you didn't do it" (literal "bạn chưa làm")Never put "you didn't". Use a present-participle opener with no "you" object: I'm following up.
Drops the deadline (VN relies on shared context, not dates)State the date explicitly: Could you … by Friday? Explicit deadlines are professional, not rude.
Over-apologises → "Sorry, sorry, could you sorry…" (translating "phiền anh/chị")One softener is enough. Just a gentle reminder OR I'd appreciate it if — not both stacked.
Pro-drop → "Is good. Will send Friday." (subject omitted)Supply full subject + modal: I will send it on Friday.
"Kindly do the needful" (dated calque)Drop kindly. Use please once, or let the modal carry the politeness.
"I remind you that…" (sounds like a scolding teacher)Always qualify: Just a gentle / quick / friendly reminder. The adjective does the softening.
No past tense on the original request → "I send last week, you not reply"Mark the tense: I sent the request last week; I haven't heard back.
