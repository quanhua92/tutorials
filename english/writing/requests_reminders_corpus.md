## A. Polite-request openers (the "ask")

The first half of the bundle: how to ask for something — a document, an action, a
reply — **without sounding like an order**. The mechanism is *indirectness*:
English softens a request by burying the imperative under a modal (*could*,
*would*) and by making the hearer the subject (*you*). Confirmed in Cambridge
Grammar — *Requests*: "We usually ask for something in a polite and indirect
way, for example, using *can*, *could*, *would you mind if* and *may*."

## B. Gentle-reminder / follow-up openers (the "nudge")

The second half: the message you send **when the first request got no reply**.
The skill is reminding someone without accusing them. Every opener here frames
the silence as *the email getting lost* rather than *the person failing* — a
face-saving move. These are the high-frequency phrases of the modern inbox.

## C. The politeness ladder (escalating firmness, same ask)

Same request, five rungs. Rung 1 = a favour you're almost apologising for; rung 5
= a deadline you're enforcing. A fluent writer climbs the ladder deliberately —
they start low and only escalate if silence continues. **Vietnamese L1 learners
typically skip the middle rungs**: either they never follow up (too polite → lose
the outcome) or they jump straight to a blunt "you didn't do it" (too direct →
damages the relationship).

| When you get a chance, could you …? | low pressure, no deadline (see §A) | /wen juː ɡet ə ˈtʃɑːns kʊd juː .../ UK | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/chance | ≈#450 (of *chance*) | US/UK |
| Could you … by Friday? | neutral + soft deadline (see §A) | /ˈkʊd juː ... baɪ ˈfraɪ.deɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/requests | ≈#40 (of *could*) | US/UK |

> ladder concept (indirectness → modal → deadline → reminder → firm request)
> reflects the politeness-strategy literature summarised in business-email
> corpora (HubSpot, CNBC, British Council *LearnEnglish* — C1 email request).
