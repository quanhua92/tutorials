## A. Vague nouns & substitutes — refer to a category when the exact item is not needed

These are the *general nouns* and *placeholders* a speaker reaches for when naming
every member of a set would be pedantic or impossible. Cambridge's own grammar
note on `stuff` states the case plainly: *"Stuff is one of the most common nouns
in speaking. It is more informal than thing. It is not at all common in writing."*

## B. Vague extenders — tack onto a list/claim to mean "and other similar things"

Vague extenders (also called **general extenders** in the pragmatics literature)
are tags added to the end of a list or an example so the speaker does **not** have
to enumerate everything. Channell (1994) and Jucker, Smith & Lüge (2003) show
their primary job is **social/interactive**, not informational: they mark shared
knowledge ("you know what I mean"), signal informality, and let the speaker stay
loosely committed. Register matters — the casual ones are *spoken* and must not
leak into formal writing.

| or something (like that) | used to show that what you just said is only an example or you are not certain (informal) | /ɔː ˈsʌmθɪŋ/ UK · /ɔːr ˈsʌmθɪŋ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/or-something | ≈#700 (chunk) | US/UK |

| and that kind of thing | and other things of that sort (neutral-to-casual) | /ən ðət ˌkaɪnd əv ˈθɪŋ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/and | ≈#1800 (chunk) | US/UK |

## C. The register ladder (the bundle's core L1 trap)

These are not separate chunks but a **ranking** of the same job — "and other
similar things" — from most casual to most formal. A Vietnamese learner's two
characteristic errors are (1) *never* using the casual rungs (sounds rigid /
textbook) and (2) using a casual rung in formal writing. Each row is anchored to
a corpus entry above.

| Job: "and other similar things" | Most casual (spoken only) → Most formal (written) | source URL |
|---|---|---|
| list extender | and stuff → or something → or whatever → and so on → and so forth → etc. (et cetera) | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/stuff |
| general noun | stuff → things → items / matters | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/stuff |

> **The pragmatic pay-off (why this exists):** Jucker, Smith & Lüge (2003) show
> that extenders like `or something`, `and stuff`, and `or whatever` are
> **interactive** — they *invite* the listener to infer the rest ("you know what
> I mean"), which is exactly the shared-understanding warmth that makes speech
> sound native. Overstreet (2011:308) stresses the function is *"interpreted
> locally in context."* Channell (1994) groups them as vague **additive**
> categories.
