## A. Proposing a time (the "Does X work? / How about X? / Shall we say X?" set)

Scheduling a meeting in English runs on a **small set of proposing chunks**, not
on a literal translation of Vietnamese ("thŠĽ© Ba c√≥ ńĎ∆įŠĽ£c kh√īng?" / "ńĎŠĽēi sang 3 giŠĽĚ
ńĎ∆įŠĽ£c kh√īng?"). Cambridge Grammar (*English Grammar Today*) groups these as the
**"making suggestions"** family: `How about‚Ä¶?` ("how about + noun phrase and how
about + -ing form when we make suggestions"); `Shall we‚Ä¶?` ("We use *shall I*
and *shall we* to make offers and suggestions"); and the verb `work` in the
sense "to be effective or have an acceptable result" ‚ÄĒ the source of the
collocation **"Does [time] work (for you)?"** that dominates spoken scheduling.
A learner who proposes a time without one of these chunks either sounds blunt
("We meet Tuesday?") or over-formal ("Is Tuesday convenient for your
consideration?").

## B. Confirming a time (That works / I'm free then / Perfect / Sounds good)

Once a time is proposed, the other side **confirms** with a short, positive
chunk. These are the accept-responses ‚ÄĒ they close the proposing turn and lock
the time. Vietnamese learners tend to over-confirm literally ("Yes, I agree")
instead of using the native chunk set, which sounds stiff. Cambridge documents
`work` ("to be effective or have an acceptable result"), `free` ("not busy or
doing planned things"), and the discourse-marker uses of `perfect` / `sounds
good` as brief positive assessments.

## C. Rescheduling (Can we push it to / move it to / reschedule / push it back)

The highest-stakes move: **asking to change a time already agreed**. English has
a dedicated, soft chunk set for this ‚ÄĒ `push it to/back`, `move it to`,
`reschedule` ‚ÄĒ all of which **preserve politeness** by framing the change as a
small, negotiable relocation. Vietnamese learners often translate literally
("can change time?") which sounds blunt, or over-apologise ("I am so sorry, I
cannot, please forgive me") which overweights a routine adjustment. Cambridge
documents the phrasal verb `push back` ("to delay something; postpone"),
`reschedule` ("to agree on a new and later date for something to happen"), and
the relocation verb `move` ("to change the time of").
