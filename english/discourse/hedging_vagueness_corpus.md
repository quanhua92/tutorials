## A. Approximators ("rounders" — Prince et al. 1980)

Rounders mark a number, time, or quantity as *inexact*. The speaker knows the
true value but deliberately gives a fuzzy band — the politeness/finance move of
"close enough without over-committing." Oxford's *approximately* entry names the
core set explicitly in its "Ways of saying approximately" bank: **about is the
most common and approximately the most formal** (Oxford, *approximately*).

| about | approximately (the most common approximator) | /əˈbaʊt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/about_1 | ≈#50 | US/UK |
| roughly | approximately but not exactly | /ˈrʌfli/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/roughly | ≈#1500 | US/UK |
| approximately | almost, but not completely, accurate (most formal) | /əˈprɒksɪmətli/ UK · /əˈprɑːksɪmətli/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/approximately | ≈#1100 | US/UK |
| or so | …and a bit more / about that (postposed) | /ər ˈsəʊ/ UK · /ər ˈsoʊ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/approximately | ≈#800 (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `around` / `about`: "We left **around/about** 8 o'clock." — Oxford,

> - `approximately`: "The journey took **approximately** seven hours." — Oxford,
>   *approximately*.
> - `or so`: "We are expecting thirty **or so** people to come." — Oxford,
>   *approximately* ("Ways of saying approximately" bank).

> **Verification note:** `around` /əˈraʊnd/, `about` /əˈbaʊt/, `roughly` /ˈrʌfli/,
> `approximately` /əˈprɒksɪmətli/ (UK) · /əˈprɑːksɪmətli/ (US) confirmed verbatim

> *approximately* bank and corroborated in Oxford *about* ("We wandered about the
> town for an hour or so").

## B. The `-ish` suffix (the informal approximator) — PINNED

The `-ish` suffix does the work of *around/about* but as a bound morpheme
clipped onto a number, time, colour, or adjective: **six-ish, forty-ish,
reddish, tired-ish**. Oxford lists "fairly; approximately" as one of its three
senses, with `thirtyish` and `reddish` as examples. The standalone adverb *ish*
("I've finished. Ish.") is the same word freed to stand alone — a 2010s
innovation now in the major learner's dictionaries.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| -ish (suffix) | fairly; approximately (also "having the nature of") | /ɪʃ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/ish_2 | n/a (suffix) | US/UK |
| ish (adverb) | used after a statement to make it less definite | /ɪʃ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/ish_1 | n/a (informal) | UK |
| six-ish | at approximately six o'clock | /sɪks ɪʃ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/ish_2 | n/a (phrase) | US/UK |
| thirtyish | approximately thirty (years old) | /ˈθɜːtiɪʃ/ UK · /ˈθɜːrtiɪʃ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/ish_2 | n/a (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `-ish` suffix: "fairly; approximately — **reddish**, **thirtyish**." — Oxford,
>   *-ish* suffix, sense 3.
> - `ish` adverb: "I've finished. **Ish**. I still need to make the sauce." —
>   Oxford, *ish* adverb (British English, informal).
> - `six-ish` / `thirtyish`: derived productively from the `-ish` "fairly;
>   approximately" sense (Oxford *-ish*); `six-ish` ("around six") and the
>   productive number+ish pattern are documented across the WordReference /
>   Reddit / Quora usage notes that cite the same Oxford/Cambridge senses.

> **Verification note:** `/ɪʃ/` confirmed in both the Oxford *-ish* suffix entry
> and the Oxford *ish* adverb entry (identical transcription). The "≈
> approximately" sense is Oxford sense 3 of the suffix, cross-checked against the
> Cambridge *-ish* entry (web search) and the usage-note literature. The
> productive `six-ish` / `tired-ish` extensions follow directly from this sense;
> they are not separate lemmas.

## C. Softeners ("adaptors" — Prince et al. 1980) — PINNED "kind of"

Softeners downgrade the force of the word that follows — *a bit tight* is less
than *tight*, *kind of strange* is less than *strange*. They protect the
speaker from over-claiming and protect the hearer's face. `kind of` and
`sort of` are the two most frequent downtoners in spoken English (Jucker, Smith
& Lüdge 2003; Channel 1994 calls them "the most stereotypical" vague-language
items).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| kind of | used when you cannot be exact; slightly (informal) | /ˌkaɪnd əv/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/kind-of | ≈#90 (spoken) | US/UK |
| sort of | to some extent, but in a way you can't easily describe (informal) | /ˈsɔːt əv/ UK · /ˈsɔːrt əv/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/sort_1 | ≈#150 (spoken) | US/UK |
| a bit | rather; to some extent (esp. BrE) | /ə bɪt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/bit_1 | ≈#200 (spoken) | UK |
| a little | slightly; to a small extent (esp. NAmE) | /ə ˈlɪtl/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/little_2 | ≈#180 (spoken) | US/UK |
| somewhat | to some degree (rather formal) | /ˈsʌmwɒt/ UK · /ˈsʌmwʌt/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/somewhat | ≈#1200 | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `kind of`: "It was **kind of** strange to see him again." — Cambridge,
>   *kind of* ("used when you are trying to explain or describe something, but
>   you cannot be exact").
> - `sort of`: "She **sort of** pretends that she doesn't really care." / "'Do
>   you understand?' '**Sort of**.'" — Oxford, *sort of* idiom (informal).
> - `a bit`: "These trousers are **a bit** tight." / "I was **a bit**
>   disappointed by the film." — Oxford, *bit* noun ("a bit … rather; to some
>   extent").
> - `a little`: "It costs a **little** bit more than I wanted to spend." —
>   Oxford, *bit* noun (the "a little / a little bit" NAmE variant note).
> - `somewhat`: "I was **somewhat** surprised to see him." — Oxford, *somewhat*
>   ("to some degree synonym rather"; "(rather formal)").

> **Verification note:** `kind of` /ˌkaɪnd əv/ is the standard Cambridge
> transcription (confirmed in the Cambridge *kind of* entry via web search and in
> the International-Scientific-Publications paper that cites the same Cambridge
> senses); `sort of` /ˈsɔːt əv/ derived from Oxford *sort* /sɔːt/ UK · /sɔːrt/
> US + weak *of* /əv/; `a bit` /ə bɪt/ from Oxford *bit* /bɪt/; `somewhat`
> /ˈsʌmwɒt/ (UK) · /ˈsʌmwʌt/ (US) confirmed verbatim in Oxford. The British vs
> American split for `a bit` (BrE) vs `a little` / `a little bit` (NAmE) is
> stated explicitly in Oxford *bit*'s "British/American a bit / a little" box.

## D. Shields ("plausibility shields" — Prince et al. 1980)

Shields hedge the speaker's *commitment to the truth* of the proposition rather
than the precision of a number. *More or less* doubles as both shield and
approximator (Oxford lists it under both "almost" and "approximately");
*something like* approximates an unnamed referent.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| more or less | (1) almost; (2) approximately | /ˌmɔːr ər ˈles/ UK · /ˌmɔːr ər ˈles/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/less_3 | ≈#600 (phrase) | US/UK |
| something like | something approaching or approximating | /ˈsʌmθɪŋ laɪk/ | https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/like | ≈#700 (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `more or less`: "I've **more or less** finished the book." (almost) /
>   "She could earn $200 a night, **more or less**." (approximately) — Oxford,
>   *less* adverb, "more or less" idiom (senses 1 & 2).
> - `something like`: "something like, something approaching or approximating."
>   — Collins / Dictionary.com, *like* ("See something like").

> **Verification note:** `more or less` is the Oxford *less* idiom
> (sense 1 "almost", sense 2 "approximately"); `more` /mɔː(r)/ UK · /mɔːr/ US and
> `less` /les/ are the standard Oxford transcriptions. `something like` is the
> Collins *like* sub-entry "6. See something like" (= *about*, approximating),
> corroborated in Dictionary.com's *like* entry.
