## Why this is bundle #23 (read this first)

Giving advice is the speech act a Vietnamese learner most often performs
**too directly** ‚ÄĒ not because the chunks are missing, but because the L1 and
the target disagree on what counts as *polite* advice. Vietnamese advice-giving
runs on **hierarchy + directness**: a senior (parent, teacher, older friend)
advises a junior with a bare imperative ‚ÄĒ *"ńĎi kh√°m ńĎi"* ("go see the doctor") ‚ÄĒ
or the modal *n√™n* ("should") ‚ÄĒ *"em n√™n nghŠĽČ ng∆°i"* ("you should rest"). Both
are normal, even caring, in Vietnamese. Both **transfer verbatim** into English
as *You should‚Ä¶* / *Go rest* ‚ÄĒ and to an Anglo ear, both sound like a **lecture,
an order, or a parent talking to a child**.

English runs on the **opposite assumption** for advice between equals. An
unsolicited *You should‚Ä¶* reads as presumptuous (who appointed you the expert on
my life?); even a **solicited** *You should‚Ä¶* can land as bossy. The native
repair is a **softening layer**: modals (*might, could*), conditionals
(*I'd‚Ä¶ = I would‚Ä¶*, *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*), and question-frames (*Why don't
you‚Ä¶?* / *Have you tried‚Ä¶?*). The softener is not weakness ‚ÄĒ it is the politeness
that lets the listener **keep ownership of the decision**. Drop it and you sound
like you are giving orders; keep it and you sound like a friend.

This bundle is built around the **soft‚Üístrong ladder** ‚ÄĒ five rungs, from the
softest (*You might want to‚Ä¶*) to the strongest still-polite (*If I were you,
I'd‚Ä¶*). Own the ladder and you own the act: pick the rung that fits the
relationship, the stakes, and whether the advice was asked for.

## 1. The soft‚Üístrong ladder (one picture)

Advice is not a single act ‚ÄĒ it is a **gradient of commitment**. The bottom
rungs *float an idea* and let the listener dismiss it without losing face; the
top rungs *commit the adviser* but still leave the final call to the listener.
The rung you pick encodes **how much authority you claim** ‚ÄĒ get it wrong and
the advice misfires (too soft = the listener misses it; too strong = the
listener resents it).

    ASK{"Advice asked for?<br/>(unsolicited advice is risky)"}

    ASK -->|"NO ‚ÄĒ wait, or preface"| HOLD["<b>Hold the advice.</b><br/>Ask 'Do you want a suggestion?' first"]

**The Vietnamese trap:** there is no ladder in L1 advice ‚ÄĒ there is direct
(*n√™n* = "should") and more-direct (bare imperative). So the learner reaches for
*You should‚Ä¶* as the **default** and uses it in every register, from a text to a
friend to an email to a client. But *You should‚Ä¶* is **rung 8** ‚ÄĒ above the
entire ladder, in plain-order territory. It claims authority the speaker may not
have, and it removes the listener's ownership of the decision. Drill the seven
rungs until *You should‚Ä¶* feels as heavy to you as it does to a native ear.

## 2. The soft rungs (R1‚ÄďR3 ‚ÄĒ plant the idea)

The safest tier. The adviser does not tell the listener what to do ‚ÄĒ they
**float a possibility** and let the listener pick it up or let it pass. The
softeners are mechanical: *might* (a modal of possibility, not certainty),
*tried* (a past-tense question that frames the advice as something the listener
may already have considered), and *be worth* (an adjective that judges the
**action**, not the listener). Use these rungs when the advice is **unsolicited**,
when the stakes are **low**, or when the listener is **sensitive**.

| Chunk | When it fits |
|---|---|
| **You might want to‚Ä¶** /juňź ma…™t ňąw…ínt t…ô/ UK ¬∑ /juňź ma…™t ňąw…Ďňźnt t…ô/ US | the gentlest possible flag ‚ÄĒ use when the listener might bristle at any stronger form; the *might* makes it easy to ignore |
| **Have you tried‚Ä¶?** /h…ôv juňź tra…™d/ | advice dressed as curiosity ‚ÄĒ implies "you've probably already thought of this", which protects the listener's competence |
| **It might be worth + -ing** /…™t ma…™t biňź w…úňźőł/ UK ¬∑ /…™t ma…™t biňź w…úňźrőł/ US | recommends an **action** without recommending it for **this person** ‚ÄĒ the most face-safe of the three |

> From `advising_corpus.md`:
>
> | You might want to‚Ä¶ | Have you tried‚Ä¶? |
> |---|---|
> | /juňź ma…™t ňąw…ínt t…ô/ UK ¬∑ /juňź ma…™t ňąw…Ďňźnt t…ô/ US | /h…ôv juňź tra…™d/ |
>
> Cambridge's *might* modal entry defines it as *"used to express the
> possibility that something will happen or be done."* That **possibility** is
> the politeness: *You might want to‚Ä¶* never claims the advice is right, only
> that it is *possible* the listener would want it. *Have you tried‚Ä¶?* uses the
> Oxford *try* entry (verb sense "to use, do or test something in order to see
> if it works") ‚ÄĒ the present-perfect question form is what makes it advice
> rather than a literal inquiry; the listener hears *"consider this option"*.
>
> - **It might be worth + -ing** is the Oxford *worth* adjective (sense 2:
>   *"used to recommend the action mentioned because you think it may be
>   useful"*), with the verbatim examples *"It's worth making an appointment
>   before you go"* and *"This idea is well worth considering."* The *worth +
>   -ing* frame is the face-safe recommendation: it judges the **action**,
>   not the person.
> - **You could always‚Ä¶** is the Oxford *always* adverb (sense 5, explicitly
>   labelled *"can/could always‚Ä¶ used to suggest a possible course of action"*),
>   verbatim example *"If it doesn't fit, you can always take it back."* The
>   *always* signals *"this option is still on the table if the others fail"* ‚ÄĒ
>   a fallback, not a first choice.

**The Vietnamese trap:** the learner who wants to say *"em n√™n thŠĽ≠‚Ä¶"* ("you
should try‚Ä¶") reaches for *You should try‚Ä¶* ‚ÄĒ rung 8. The native equivalent at
this gentleness is *You might want to try‚Ä¶* (rung 1) or *Have you tried‚Ä¶?* (rung
2). The *might* and the *Have you* are not decoration ‚ÄĒ they are the difference
between a friend and a lecturer.

## 3. The strong rungs (R6‚ÄďR7 ‚ÄĒ commit, but stay kind)

When the advice is **asked for**, the stakes are **real**, or the listener is
**floundering**, the soft rungs can feel evasive ‚ÄĒ the listener wants a real
recommendation, not a hedge. The strong rungs **commit** ("this is what I would
do") but still soften with two devices: (1) **`I'd`** (the contracted *I would*
‚ÄĒ a **conditional**, not *I do*; it signals "this is my hypothetical
recommendation, not a command"), and (2) **If I were you** (the second
conditional, which puts the adviser **inside the listener's situation** rather
than above it).

| Chunk | What the softener does |
|---|---|
| **I'd suggest (that)‚Ä¶** /a…™d s…ôňąd íest/ | the `I'd` (conditional) + *suggest* (the helpful verb ‚ÄĒ see Oxford's synonym note below) = committed but not bossy |
| **I'd recommend + -ing / that‚Ä¶** /a…™d ňĆrek…ôňąmend/ | slightly more authoritative than *suggest*; *recommend* claims expertise but the `I'd` keeps it advisory |
| **If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶** /…™f a…™ w…úňź juňź a…™d/ UK ¬∑ /…™f a…™ w…ôr juňź a…™d/ US | the strongest still-polite form ‚ÄĒ "in your shoes" framing makes it empathy + advice, not authority |

> From `advising_corpus.md`:
>
> - **I'd suggest‚Ä¶** uses the Oxford *suggest* verb sense 1, with the verbatim
>   Express Yourself example *"I suggest you have another look at the house
>   before you make a decision."* The *I'd* (I would) is the conditional softener
>   ‚ÄĒ bare *"I suggest you‚Ä¶"* is noticeably more direct.
> - **I'd recommend‚Ä¶** uses the Oxford *recommend* verb sense 2 ("to advise a
>   particular course of action"), verbatim Express Yourself example *"I'd
>   recommend waiting a few months."* Oxford's own synonym note is the key
>   attestation:
>
> > *"Advise is a stronger word than recommend‚Ä¶ I advise you‚Ä¶ can suggest that
> > you know better than the person you are advising: this may cause offence if
> > they are your equal or senior to you. I recommend‚Ä¶ mainly suggests that you
> > are trying to be helpful and is less likely to cause offence."*
>
> That note is the whole bundle in one paragraph: **advise/recommend/suggest**
> form their own commitment ladder, and *should* sits above all of them.
> - **If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶** is the **second conditional**, documented in the
>   Cambridge Grammar *Conditionals: if* entry (structure: *If + past simple, ‚Ä¶
>   would/wouldn't + infinitive*). Cambridge's verbatim example: *"I wouldn't
>   ask my parents for a loan if I were you."* The **were** (not *was*) is the
>   prescribed advice form ‚ÄĒ *"If I was you"* is heard in casual speech but
>   marked as non-standard; in writing and in careful advice, use *were*.

**The Vietnamese trap:** *n√™n* ("should") is the L1 word for advice, and it
maps cleanly onto *should* ‚ÄĒ which is exactly the trap. *You should‚Ä¶* claims
**moral authority** in English that *n√™n* does not in Vietnamese. The repair is
to swap *should* for a **conditional**: *I'd suggest‚Ä¶* / *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*.
The conditional does the same advice work without the authority claim. Drill the
swap until *You should‚Ä¶* feels like a last resort, not a default.

## 4. The question rungs (R4‚ÄďR5 ‚ÄĒ advice dressed as inquiry)

A native speaker's favourite move: **ask a question whose answer is the
advice**. *Why don't you‚Ä¶?* is not a real question ‚ÄĒ it is a recommendation
phrased as inquiry, which is why it never sounds like a lecture. The
question-frame hands the decision back to the listener even as it points them at
the answer. Vietnamese has the exact same device (*sao em kh√īng‚Ä¶?* = "why don't
you‚Ä¶?"), so this is the **cleanest L1‚ÜíEnglish transfer** in the bundle ‚ÄĒ drill
it first.

> From `advising_corpus.md`:
>
> - **Why don't you‚Ä¶?** /wa…™ ňąd…ô änt juňź/ UK ¬∑ /wa…™ ňądo änt juňź/ US ‚ÄĒ the Oxford
>   *suggest* entry's **Making suggestions** block attests it verbatim: *"Why
>   don't you try calling him again?"*
> - **Why not + verb‚Ä¶?** /wa…™ n…ít/ UK ¬∑ /wa…™ n…Ďňźt/ US ‚ÄĒ the compressed form,
>   same block: *"Why not just wait until they come back?"*
> - **How about + -ing / noun?** /ňąha ä …ôňąba ät/ ‚ÄĒ same block: *"How about going
>   out for a walk on Saturday?"*

**The Vietnamese trap:** *sao em kh√īng‚Ä¶?* transfers so cleanly that learners
often stop here and never learn the soft/strong rungs. That is fine for casual
chat, but *Why don't you‚Ä¶?* alone sounds **too breezy for high-stakes advice**
(a friend deciding whether to quit a job wants *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*, not *Why
don't you just quit?*). Own the question rungs **and** the commitment rungs;
mix them by register.

## 5. The unsolicited-advice rule (the move learners skip)

The single biggest advice-pragmatics failure is **giving advice that was not
asked for**. In Vietnamese culture, offering advice unsolicited is often a
**caring act** (an older friend *"khuy√™n"* you = they care); in English, it is
frequently read as **presumption or criticism** ("you think I can't figure this
out myself?"). The native repair is a **preflight check**: ask permission before
advising, then pick a soft rung.

> From `advising_corpus.md` + sibling bundles:
>
> - **Do you want a suggestion?** / **Want some advice?** ‚ÄĒ the preflight. If
>   the listener says *no*, **stop**. If *yes*, you have been invited and the
>   advice lands as help, not intrusion.
> - Preface with empathy first: *"That sounds really tough."* (ūüĒó
>   [SYMPATHY](./SYMPATHY.md) #24) ‚Üí *then* the advice. Empathy-without-advice
>   feels warm; advice-without-empathy feels cold.

**The Vietnamese trap:** the learner jumps straight to *You should‚Ä¶* the moment
they hear a problem ‚ÄĒ the L1 caring reflex. The English-speaking listener hears
it as *"here is what you are doing wrong."* Train a **two-beat pause**: empathy
first (*"That sounds rough"*), then a **preflight** (*"Do you want a
suggestion?"*), then the advice on a soft rung. The two beats are the difference
between a friend and a fixer.

## 7. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits on giving advice ‚ÄĒ extend, don't replace, the seed rows from the
spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Transfers *n√™n* ("should") as *You should‚Ä¶*** ‚ÄĒ the default L1 advice word, used in every register from text to client | Swap *should* for a **conditional**: *I'd suggest‚Ä¶* / *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*. The conditional does the same advice work without the authority claim. *You should‚Ä¶* is rung 8 ‚ÄĒ reserve it for genuine authority (a doctor, a parent, a manager) or genuine urgency. |
| **Uses the bare imperative** (*"ńĎi kh√°m ńĎi"* = "go see the doctor") ‚ÄĒ caring in Vietnamese, an **order** in English | Soften with a modal or question: *You might want to see a doctor.* / *Have you seen a doctor?* / *Why don't you see a doctor?* The bare imperative (*Go see a doctor.*) is only acceptable from a very close relation or in an emergency. |
| **Gives unsolicited advice** ‚ÄĒ the L1 *"khuy√™n"* (to advise) caring reflex; offers *You should‚Ä¶* the moment a problem is mentioned | **Preflight first**: *Do you want a suggestion?* / *Want some advice?* If yes ‚Üí soft rung. If no ‚Üí empathy only (*"That sounds rough"*). Unsolicited *You should‚Ä¶* reads as *"you can't figure this out yourself."* ūüĒó See [SYMPATHY](./SYMPATHY.md). |
| **Skips the empathy step** ‚ÄĒ goes straight from *"I hear your problem"* to *"You should‚Ä¶"*, which lands as cold/fixer energy | Two-beat pause: **empathy first** (*"That sounds really tough"*) ‚Üí then the advice on a soft rung. Advice-without-empathy = criticism; empathy-then-advice = care. |
| **Uses *should* for high-stakes advice** (quitting a job, ending a relationship) ‚ÄĒ too blunt for the weight of the decision | Step up to **If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ the second conditional. It commits the adviser (*"I would do X"*) while keeping the listener's ownership (*"in your shoes"*). *You should quit* sounds like a command; *If I were you, I'd quit* sounds like a friend. |
| **Says *If I was you*** (transferring the regular past) instead of *If I were you* | Use **were** for the second conditional in advice: *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*. *Was* is heard in casual speech but marked non-standard; in writing and careful advice, *were* is the prescribed form (Cambridge Grammar). |
| **Drops the *-ing* after *worth*** ‚Üí *"It might be worth try"* / *"It's worth consider"* | *worth* takes the **-ing form**: *It might be worth try**ing**.* / *It's worth consider**ing**.* (Oxford *worth* sense 2). No exceptions ‚ÄĒ *worth + -ing* is the frame. |
| **Drops the *-ing* / uses *to* after *suggest* and *recommend*** ‚Üí *"I suggest to go"* / *"I recommend to read it"* | *suggest/recommend* take **-ing** or a **that-clause**, never *to*: *I'd suggest go**ing**.* / *I'd suggest **that** we go.* / *I'd recommend read**ing** it.* (Oxford *suggest/recommend* entries.) |
| **Drops finals on the advice itself** ‚Üí *"You migh' want to"*, *"I'd sugges'"*, *"Why don' you"*, *"If I were you, I'"* | Release every final. The /t/ in *might*, the /t É/‚Üí/st/ in *suggest*, the /t/ in *don't*, the /d/ in *I'd* carry the chunk's meaning. ūüĒó Drill [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md). |
| **Vietnamese /őł/ ‚Üí /t/, /√į/ ‚Üí /z/** in *worth* /w…úňźőł/ ‚Üí "wut", *that* ‚Üí "dat" | *worth* ends in /őł/ ‚ÄĒ tongue between teeth. The /t/-substitution flips *worth* toward *word*/*wart*. ūüĒó See [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md). |
| **Stresses *re-COM-mend* on the wrong syllable** (transfer of Vietnamese even-syllable rhythm) | *recommend* is stressed on the **final** syllable: /ňĆrek…ôňą**mend***/ (Oxford). Drill it against the YouGlish clip; wrong stress is more audible than wrong sounds. |
| **Uses *advise* where *suggest/recommend* fits** ‚ÄĒ *advise* claims authority and *"may cause offence if [the listener] is your equal or senior"* (Oxford synonym note) | Default to *suggest* (helpful, low authority) or *recommend* (helpful, mild expertise). ## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚ÄĒ this guide, ¬ß1‚Äď¬ß5 (the ladder, the soft rungs, the strong
   rungs, the question rungs, the unsolicited-advice rule).
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) ‚ÄĒ open `advising.html`, drill the 8 flip cards + the
   two-register role-play **aloud**. Pay attention to the **rung**: when you're
   Person A (adviser), start on a soft rung in Dialog 1 (casual), then step up
   to a strong rung in Dialog 2 (committed). Feel the *should*-to-*conditional*
   swap.
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) ‚ÄĒ the writing task: write **one advice email line**
   starting with *You might want to‚Ä¶* (the softest rung). Then rewrite it three
   more times, stepping up the ladder: *Have you tried‚Ä¶?* ‚Üí *Why don't you‚Ä¶?* ‚Üí
   *If I were you, I'd‚Ä¶*. Same advice, four rungs ‚ÄĒ feel the commitment climb.
