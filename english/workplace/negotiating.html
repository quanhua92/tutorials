5Writing task

Write a conditional proposal. Use both "If you can…, we could…" and a compromise idiom. Then reveal the model answer.

Model (conditional offer → condition → compromise fallback):

If you can commit to a Friday delivery, we could agree to the full price. [offer + condition; modal "could" keeps it tentative]

We'd be willing to waive the rush fee, provided that you pay net-15. [concession-for-concession; formal conditional]

If that's too tight — how about we meet halfway? Monday delivery at a 10% discount? [compromise idiom when the first trade is refused]

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Concedes too much to preserve harmony (giữ thể diện) — accepts the first offerNever accept unconditionally. Always attach a condition: "We'd be willing to…, provided that…" A concession earns a concession.
Holds rigid to save face — refuses to move a number/term because moving "loses face"Reframe moving as a trade: "If you can do X, we could agree to Y." You're not giving in — you're trading.
Misses the concession-for-concession logic — positional ("this is my price"), not conditionalDrill the conditional template until it's automatic. Every offer should have an if. No free concessions.
States a position as a declarative: "We want $10,000" (sounds blunt/demanding)Soften with a modal + condition: "We could accept $10,000, if you can…" The could keeps it negotiable.
Cannot say "no" politely — direct refusal feels rude, so says "yes" or stays silentUse the polite-refusal formula: "I'm afraid we can't go that far." Refuses without aggression — drill it as a fixed chunk.
Silence is misread — a Vietnamese pause (thinking/harmony) reads to Westerners as rejection/stallingFill the gap with a fluency move: "Let me think about that" — then the counter. Silence in English negotiation is a weapon, not a courtesy.
Under-uses the compromise idioms — translates literally ("chia đôi") instead of the shared idiomUse the set: "meet halfway" / "split the difference" / "middle ground." Cambridge lists all three as compromise synonyms.
Overuses deal-breaker early as a threat, which kills the negotiationReserve "That's a deal-breaker" for the genuine walk-away term. Use it once; mean it.
Drops the that in "provided that / on condition that" under stress → loses the formal registerIn formal/contractual tone, keep the that: "provided that you can deliver by Friday."
Confuses reasonable with cheap/low → "That's reasonable" about a low price sounds like approval of cheapnessreasonable = "fair and practical" (CALD). "That sounds reasonable" = "I judge this fair," not "this is small."
