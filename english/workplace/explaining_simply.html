5Writing task

Explain a concept from your field using an analogy. Lead with "Think of it like…" / "Basically…". Then reveal the model answer.

Model (analogy-first — familiar thing, then the technical layer):

Think of it like a restaurant. /ˈθɪŋk əv ɪt laɪk/ — the canonical analogy opener; maps the unknown onto the known

You're the customer, the waiter is the API, and the kitchen is the server. you place an order (request) → waiter carries it to the kitchen → brings back your food (response)

Basically, the API is the middleman that takes your request and brings back the answer — you never talk to the kitchen directly. /ˈbeɪ.sɪ.kəl.i/ — reduce to the essence

Does that make sense? /dʌz ðæt meɪk sens/ — always end with the comprehension check

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Jargon-dumps to show expertise — stacks terminology ("the API gateway throttles the idempotent payload")Lead with an analogy first, technical second. Use Think of it like… Expertise in English = accessible, not dense.
Assumes shared context — drops the listener into the middle of a technical idea with no on-rampOpen with the analogy or simplification frame: To put it simply… / The gist is…
Dumps acronyms unparsed — "so the SSO uses JWT over OIDC" with no expansionSpell it out on first use, then abbreviate: single sign-on — SSO — Think of it like one key that unlocks every door.
Skips the "does that make sense?" check — explains in one long turn and moves onEnd with a comprehension check: Does that make sense? Checking is politeness, not condescension.
Condescends when simplifying — "Let me dumb this down" / talks downKeep the frame about the language, not the listener: To put it simply…, never Let me dumb this down for you.
Drops the final consonant on like /laɪk/ → "lye", and on gist /dʒɪst/ → "jis"Release the final /k/ on like and the /st/ on gist. The analogy connector is useless if mis-heard.
Mis-stresses basically → "ba-SI-cal-ly" or rushes all syllables equallyStress the first syllable: BA-si-cal-ly /ˈbeɪ.sɪ.kəl.i/. The weak syllables after it reduce.
Flattens the /ɜːr/ in works /wɜːrks/ → "wooks" or "vorks"Drill the NURSE vowel: round lips, tongue mid-central, add the /r/. works is high-frequency in every process explanation.
Repeats the same sentence louder when the listener doesn't get itUse the reframe move: In other words… / What I mean is… A second different shape beats a louder copy.
