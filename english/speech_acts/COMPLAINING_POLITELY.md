## Why this is bundle #29 (read this first)

A complaint is, by definition, **a face-threatening act**. In Brown & Levinson's
politeness theory, complaining imposes on the hearer's **negative face** ‚ÄĒ their
"want to have their freedom of action unhindered." Unhedged, a complaint reads
as an attack. Vietnamese culture handles this threat two ways, and **both fail
in English**:

1. **Suppression to save face.** Vietnamese face-saving culture (*thŠĽÉ diŠĽán*)
   often **suppresses** a complaint entirely ‚ÄĒ swallow it, say nothing, avoid
   confrontation. The waiter never hears the food was cold; the seller never
   hears the product was wrong. In English service culture, **silence is not
   politeness** ‚ÄĒ the problem goes unfixed, the relationship stays stuck.
2. **Literal translation when forced.** When a Vietnamese learner *does*
   complain, the instinct is to translate *c√°i n√†y hŠĽŹng rŠĽďi, sŠĽ≠a ńĎi* almost
   word-for-word: **"this is broken, fix it."** That imperative, with no hedge,
   maximises the imposition on the hearer's negative face. It sounds blunt,
   aggressive, or childish to a native ear ‚ÄĒ even though the *intent* is neutral.

English threads this needle with a **three-move arc**: a **soft hedge opener**
(*I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶* / *There seems to be a problem with‚Ä¶*) ‚Üí a
**factual statement of the problem** (*it isn't working / I was charged twice*) ‚Üí
a **hedged request for resolution** (*Could you look into‚Ä¶?* / *I'd appreciate
it if you could‚Ä¶*). The hedge does the softening; the statement does the
reporting; the ask is a modal question, never an imperative. This bundle teaches
that arc as **three retrievable chunks**, not a feeling.

## 1. The three-move arc: hedge ‚Üí fact ‚Üí ask

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    H["1. SOFT OPENER (hedge)<br/>protects negative face"] --> F["2. FACTUAL STATEMENT<br/>no emotion, just the fact"]
    F --> A["3. HEDGED ASK<br/>modal question, not imperative"]
    H2["I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶"] -.-> H
    F2["it isn't working /<br/>I was charged twice"] -.-> F
    A2["Could you look into‚Ä¶?<br/>I'd appreciate it if you could‚Ä¶"] -.-> A
    style H fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style F fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
    style A fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
```

The arc is the whole bundle in one picture. The **same complaint** ("my laptop
is broken") is delivered by three different chunks at three different moments.
Skip the opener ‚Üí you sound aggressive. Skip the ask ‚Üí you sound like you're
just venting. The arc is what makes the complaint **actionable and polite at
once**.

> From `complaining_politely_corpus.md` (the three moves, verbatim):
>
> - **Opener** ‚Üí *I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶* / *There seems to be a
>   problem with‚Ä¶* / *I hate to bring this up, but‚Ä¶* / *I'm not entirely
>   satisfied with‚Ä¶*
> - **Fact** ‚Üí *it isn't working* / *it hasn't arrived yet* / *I was charged
>   twice* / *there's a mistake on the invoice*
> - **Ask** ‚Üí *Could you look into‚Ä¶?* / *Could you sort this out?* / *I'd
>   appreciate it if you could‚Ä¶*

**The Vietnamese trap:** learners collapse the arc into one move ‚ÄĒ the **bare
fact** ("this is broken, fix it") or the **bare ask** ("give me refund"). Both
skip the hedge, so both read as attacks. The fix is to **practise the arc as
three slots** you fill in sequence, every time.

## 2. Why *afraid* doesn't mean "scared" here

The single highest-value word in the bundle is **afraid**, and it is *not* the
emotion word. In *I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶*, **afraid** is a **hedge
marker** ‚ÄĒ a conventional signal meaning *"I'm sorry to have to say this, but‚Ä¶"*
Cambridge lists it separately from the emotion sense:

> From `complaining_politely_corpus.md`:
>
> | afraid (emotion) | I'm afraid‚Ä¶ (hedge) |
> |---|---|
> | /…ôňąfre…™d/ ‚ÄĒ "feeling fear" | /…ôňąfre…™d/ ‚ÄĒ "used to politely introduce bad news or disagreement" |
> | *Don't be afraid.* | *There are no tickets left, I'm afraid.* |

The two share a spelling and an IPA; only the **function** differs. A Vietnamese
learner who knows only the emotion sense will hear *"I'm afraid there's an
issue‚Ä¶"* as *"I am terrified there is an issue"* ‚ÄĒ and will be afraid (emotion)
to use it. It is, in fact, the **most polite complaint opener in the language**,
and it is not scary at all.

ūüĒó The same hedge family ‚ÄĒ *afraid / seems to be / appears to be / I'd say* ‚ÄĒ
is drilled in [OPINIONS, HEDGED](./OPINIONS_HEDGED.md) (#20). Once you feel the
family, one member unlocks the rest.

## 3. The request half: modal questions, never imperatives

The third move is where Vietnamese learners most often break the arc. The L1
instinct (*sŠĽ≠a ńĎi / ńĎŠĽēi ńĎi / trŠļ£ tiŠĽĀn lŠļ°i ńĎi*) is **imperative** ‚ÄĒ a direct
command. English service complaints almost never use an imperative; they use a
**modal question** (*Could you‚Ä¶?*) or **conditional gratitude** (*I'd appreciate
it if you could‚Ä¶*). The reason is negative face again: an imperative *maximises*
the imposition; a modal question *minimises* it.

| L1 instinct (VN imperative) | English (hedged modal) | Why the difference |
|---|---|---|
| *SŠĽ≠a ńĎi.* ‚Üí "Fix it." | **Could you fix it?** / **Could you look into it?** | imperative = max imposition; modal Q = redressive |
| *ńźŠĽēi c√°i kh√°c.* ‚Üí "Give me another one." | **Could you replace it?** | same ‚ÄĒ modal Q protects the hearer's autonomy |
| *TrŠļ£ tiŠĽĀn lŠļ°i.* ‚Üí "Refund me." | **Could I get a refund?** / **I'd appreciate a refund.** | noun-stress shift: *refund* (n) /ňąriňźf Ćnd/ vs (v) /r…™ňąf Ćnd/ |
| *GiŠļ£i quyŠļŅt nhanh l√™n.* ‚Üí "Sort it out fast." | **Could you sort this out when you get a chance?** | + "when you get a chance" minimises the imposition further |

> From `complaining_politely_corpus.md`:
>
> | Could you look into‚Ä¶? | I'd appreciate it if you could‚Ä¶ |
> |---|---|
> | /k äd juňź l äk ňą…™nt…ô/ UK ¬∑ /k äd juňź l äk ňą…™ntuňź/ US | /a…™d …ôňąpriňź Éie…™t …™t …™f juňź k äd/ |
> | the standard soft ask (Cambridge *look into*) | conditional-gratitude hedge ‚ÄĒ strong but soft (Cambridge *appreciate*) |

**The Vietnamese trap:** the imperative feels *efficient* and *honest* in
Vietnamese. In English it feels *rude*. The modal question (*Could you‚Ä¶?*) feels
*roundabout* to a Vietnamese learner ‚ÄĒ but it is the **single most important
redressive move** in the bundle. Drill *Could you‚Ä¶?* until it is automatic.

## 4. The two failure modes: blunt vs. over-apologetic

Vietnamese learners of English swing between **two opposite errors** on
complaints. Naming them is the first step to fixing them:

```mermaid
flowchart TD
    L1["Vietnamese instinct on a complaint"] --> BL{"Which way do you swing?"}
    BL -->|"BLUNT (literal translation)"| B["\"This is bad / broken. Fix it.\"\n(no hedge, imperative)"]
    BL -->|"OVER-APOLOGETIC (face-saving leak)"| O["\"I'm so sorry, so sorry, but‚Ä¶ the thing‚Ä¶ is‚Ä¶ maybe‚Ä¶ a little‚Ä¶ broken?\"\n(hedge over-applied, ask never lands)"]
    B --> FIX["The arc: hedge ‚Üí fact ‚Üí ask"]
    O --> FIX
    style B fill:#fdecea,stroke:#c0392b,stroke-width:2px
    style O fill:#fdecea,stroke:#c0392b,stroke-width:2px
    style FIX fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
```

1. **Blunt.** *C√°i n√†y hŠĽŹng rŠĽďi, sŠĽ≠a ńĎi* ‚Üí *"This is broken, fix it."* No hedge,
   imperative ask. The hearer's negative face takes the full hit. Reads as
   aggressive or childish. **Fix:** prepend *I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶*
   and convert the ask to *Could you‚Ä¶?*
2. **Over-apologetic / passive-aggressive.** Face-saving culture leaks out as
   **excessive apology on the wrong side** ‚ÄĒ *"I'm so sorry to bother you, I
   really am, but the thing is, it might possibly be, you know, a little bit not
   working?"* The complaint never lands; the ask is buried. Reads as
   passive-aggressive (the hearer feels blamed *through* the apology). **Fix:**
   cut the apology (it isn't yours to make), state the fact plainly, make the
   ask explicitly.

> From `complaining_politely_corpus.md`:
>
> The middle path is the arc: **"I'm afraid there's an issue with [the laptop] ‚ÄĒ
> it isn't working. Could you look into it?"** One hedge, one fact, one modal
> ask. No emotion adjective, no apology for a fault that isn't yours.

ūüĒó The over-apologetic failure is the mirror of
[APOLOGIZING](./APOLOGIZING.md) #14 ‚ÄĒ there, the apology is *yours* because the
fault is yours. Here, the fault is *the seller's*, so the apology is theirs
(*I'm so sorry about that. I'll look into it.*). Don't steal their line.

## 6. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits on complaining politely ‚ÄĒ extend, don't replace, the seed rows from
the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Suppresses the complaint to save face** (VN *thŠĽÉ diŠĽán* culture ‚Üí say nothing, swallow it) ‚Üí the problem never gets fixed | In English service culture, **silence is not politeness**. Use the arc: hedge ‚Üí fact ‚Üí ask. A polite complaint is *expected*, not rude. |
| **Literal translation of the imperative** ‚Üí *"This is broken, fix it"* / *"Give me refund"* (from *c√°i n√†y hŠĽŹng rŠĽďi, sŠĽ≠a ńĎi*) | Convert to the modal question: *"Could you fix it?"* / *"Could I get a refund?"* The imperative maximises the imposition; the modal minimises it. |
| **Swings blunt OR over-apologetic** ‚ÄĒ no middle gear | Practise the **arc** as three slots (¬ß1, ¬ß4): one hedge, one fact, one modal ask. Never bare fact; never apology-buried ask. |
| **Over-apologises for a fault that isn't theirs** ‚Üí *"I'm so sorry, so sorry, but‚Ä¶ maybe‚Ä¶ it is a little broken?"* (face-saving leak) ‚Üí reads passive-aggressive | Cut the apology (the fault is the seller's). State the fact plainly: *"I'm afraid there's an issue with‚Ä¶ it isn't working."* Let the *agent* apologise. |
| **Drops the hedge entirely** ‚Üí *"There is a problem with this."* (sounds like an accusation) | Keep the hedge slot filled: *"There **seems to be** a problem‚Ä¶"* / *"**I'm afraid** there's an issue‚Ä¶"* The hedge is what makes it a complaint, not an attack. |
| **/őł/ ‚Üí /t/ or /d/** on *there's* ‚Üí *"dere's an issue"* | Tongue-between-teeth for /√į/ in *there's* /√įe…ôz/. ūüĒó Drill [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md). |

| **Drops final /t/ in *sort this out*** ‚Üí *"sort this ou"* | Release the final /t/ in *out*. ūüĒó Drill [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md) ‚ÄĒ the /t/ in *ou**t*** closes the request. |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚ÄĒ this guide, ¬ß1‚Äď¬ß4 (the arc, *afraid* as hedge, modal asks,
