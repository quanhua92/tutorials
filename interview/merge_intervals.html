The pattern behind every "intervals / meetings / calendar" problem: sort by start, then a single left-to-right scan where each interval is compared only with the previous one. Three variants share that spine — merge (extend the last end), insert (three-phase scan, no sort), meeting rooms (sweep-line peak). ② The boundary convention — closed vs half-open (the #1 off-by-one source)

Are [1,2] and [2,3] overlapping? It depends on whether intervals are closed [s,e] (share point 2 → overlap) or half-open [s,e) (room recycles at 2 → disjoint). P056 Merge uses closed; P253 Meeting Rooms treats the boundary as a free point (start >= ends[ptr]), so back-to-back meetings share a room.
In the sweep line, the tie-break (end, -1) before (start, +1) at the same timestamp encodes the "room recycles first" rule directly — get it backwards and you over-count rooms by 1.

③ Complexity + the wider interval-problem family

All three variants share the same spine: an O(n log n) sort then an O(n) scan. P057 Insert is the lone exception — its input is already sorted, so it's O(n) end-to-end.
Killer gotcha: nested intervals need max(last.end, end), never last.end = end — [1,10]+[2,3] must stay [1,10].
