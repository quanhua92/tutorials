## A. Casual thanks (informal register)

The everyday, low-effort ways to thank. Used with friends, family, colleagues you
know. Vietnamese learners often under-use these because L1 "cảm ơn" feels
complete on its own — but in English the casual thank is a **social lubricant**,
fired off many times a day.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thanks | the everyday, informal thank-you | /ˈθæŋks/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thanks | ≈#100 | US/UK |
| Thanks a lot | thanks + emphasised amount (casual; ⚠ also used sarcastically) | /ˈθæŋks ə ˈlɒt/ UK · /ˈθæŋks ə ˈlɑːt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thanks | ≈#1500 (phrase) | US/UK |
| Thanks so much | warm informal thanks | /ˈθæŋks soʊ mʌtʃ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thanks | ≈#2000 (phrase) | US/UK |
| Cheers | (mainly UK/Aus/NZ) thanks / bye — very casual | /tʃɪəz/ UK · /tʃɪrz/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/cheers | ≈#1500 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `thanks` /ˈθæŋks/, `cheers` UK /tʃɪəz/ · US /tʃɪrz/,
> `a lot` UK /ə ˈlɒt/ · US /ə ˈlɑːt/ are the standard Cambridge transcriptions
> (Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary entries for *thanks*, *cheers*,
> *lot*), cross-checked in the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary. ⚠
> `Thanks a lot` is a **register trap**: with flat/falling intonation it is a
> sincere thank, but with a rising/lengthened tone it is sarcastic
> ("Well, *thanks a lot* for nothing."). Cambridge flags this usage.

## B. Standard / formal thanks (neutral → professional register)

The set that travels from a neutral workplace "thank you" to a formal written
note. These are the chunks a Vietnamese learner needs for email, meetings, and
service interactions — where a bare "thanks" can read as curt.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thank you | the neutral / formal thank (the full form of *thanks*) | /ˌθæŋk ˈjuː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thank-you | ≈#120 | US/UK |
| Thank you so much | warm standard thanks | /ˌθæŋk juː soʊ mʌtʃ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thank-you | ≈#800 (phrase) | US/UK |
| That's so kind of you | warm, slightly formal thanks for a personal kindness | /ˌðæts soʊ ˈkaɪnd əv juː/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/grateful | ≈#3000 (phrase) | US/UK |
| I really appreciate it | standard sincere thanks for help/effort | /aɪ ˈrɪli əˈpriː.ʃi.eɪt ɪt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/appreciate | ≈#600 (phrase) | US/UK |
| I'm very grateful | formal thanks; common in writing / professional contexts | /aɪm ˈveri ˈɡreɪt.fəl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/grateful | ≈#1800 (phrase) | US/UK |
| I appreciate your help | workplace thanks for assistance | /aɪ əˈpriː.ʃi.eɪt jɔːr help/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/appreciate | ≈#1200 (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `appreciate` /əˈpriː.ʃi.eɪt/ (UK & US identical)
> verified directly in the Cambridge entry this session; Cambridge's own
> "I/we appreciate…" section attests *"We really appreciate all the help you

> this session; the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary "Express Yourself:
> Thanking somebody" panel attests verbatim: *"I'm very grateful"* and

> `your` UK /jɔː/ · US /jɔːr/ are standard Cambridge transcriptions.

## C. Responding to thanks (the thank-you response set)

The other half of the speech act. Vietnamese conversation often leaves a
"thank you" **unanswered** (a smile or a small nod is enough in L1); in English
**silence after "thank you" reads as cold or rude**. These are the stock
responses, climbing from casual to warm-formal.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| You're welcome | the standard, neutral response to *thank you* | /jɔːr ˈwel.kəm/ UK · /jɔːr ˈwel.kəm/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/you-re-welcome | ≈#300 (phrase) | US/UK |
| No worries | (orig. Aus/NZ; now widespread) casual response — "it was no trouble" | /noʊ ˈwʌr.iz/ US · /nəʊ ˈwʌr.iz/ UK | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/no-worries | ≈#1200 (phrase) | US/UK |
| No problem | casual response to thanks or a request — "it's fine" | /noʊ ˈprɒb.ləm/ UK · /noʊ ˈprɑː.bləm/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/no-problem | ≈#700 (phrase) | US/UK |
| My pleasure | warm, slightly formal response — "I was glad to do it" | /maɪ ˈpleʒ.ər/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/pleasure | ≈#1500 (phrase) | US/UK |
| Anytime | very casual response — "I'd help you at any time" | /ˈen.i.taɪm/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/anytime | ≈#2200 (phrase) | US/UK |
| Don't mention it | warm-formal response — "no need to thank me" | /doʊnt ˈmen.ʃən ɪt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/grateful | ≈#2500 (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned real example):** the Cambridge entry for
> **`no worries`** (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/no-worries)
> attests the exact exchange *"Thanks for the advice." — "No worries."* as
> "a friendly answer when someone thanks you for something you have done,"
> with synonyms *no problem* and *you're welcome*. This is the pinned
> sanity-check attestation for this bundle. `pleasure` UK /ˈpleʒ.ər/ ·
> US /ˈpleʒ.ɚ/ and `anytime` /ˈen.i.taɪm/ verified directly in Cambridge this
> session. `welcome` /ˈwel.kəm/, `worry` UK /ˈwʌr.i/ · US /ˈwɝː.i/,
> `mention` /ˈmen.ʃən/, `problem` UK /ˈprɒb.ləm/ · US /ˈprɑː.bləm/ are standard
> Cambridge transcriptions. The Oxford "Express Yourself: Thanking somebody"
> panel attests verbatim responses: *"That's all right." · "Don't mention it." ·
> "No problem." · "My pleasure." · "I'm glad I could help."*
