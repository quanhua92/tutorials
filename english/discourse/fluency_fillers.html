5Writing task

A tough interview question lands. Respond with a buying-time filler + your answer. Then reveal the model answer.

Question:

"Why do you want to leave your current job?"

Model (lead with a filler, hold the floor, then answer):

That's a good question. — stalling compliment, flatters + buys 2 sec

Let me think… — pure stall, level tone, don't trail off

I'd say I've grown a lot in my current role, but I'm ready for a new challenge where I can lead a small team.

Pattern: filler → hold → answer. Never filler → more silence.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Freezes in dead silence when thinking — stares, looks stuck/unpreparedDeploy a buying-time phrase reflexively: "Let me think…" / "That's a good question." Silence sounds frozen; a phrase sounds composed.
Overuses um… ơ… à… (Vietnamese ơ/à particles) carried into EnglishSwap in the lexicalized versions: "Let me see…", "Well…". Phrase fillers are rated more fluent than um/uh.
No filler reflex at all — VN signals thinking with a flat silence/nodPre-load 2–3 fillers so they fire automatically. The goal is reflex, not recall — drill until they precede thought.
Trailed-off, falling-tone filler — "let me think..." said like a sighSay it with level/rising tone + a stretched vowel (Weeell…). It signals "I'm holding the floor," not "I surrender."
Filler with no follow-through — "Let me think…" then another long silenceA filler is a bridge, not an ending. Always queue the answer: "Let me think… I'd say…"
Calques a Vietnamese hedge — "à để xem" → "ah let me see" (keeps the à)Drop the L1 particle; lead with the English filler: "Let me see…" (no leading à).
Apologizes for thinking — "Sorry, I need to think" (over-polite, insecure)Don't apologize for a normal cognitive act. "That's a good question. Let me think…" is confident; "Sorry" undercuts you.
Uses "How to say…" (Chinglish/VN calque)Use the native chunk: "How should I put it…" (Cambridge-attested), never "How to say."
