5Writing task

Write a contribution line that builds on a colleague's point. Use "Building on what [name] said…". Then reveal the model answer.

Model (a build-on contribution — credits, then extends):

Building on what Mai said, I'd add that the prototype already solves the onboarding step, so we could ship that piece first. /ˈbɪldɪŋ ɒn wɒt maɪ sɛd/ — the collaborative build + the polite add

That's a good point, and if we pair it with a short demo video, the drop-off should drop further. /ðæts ə ɡʊd ˈpɔɪnt ænd/ — validate, then extend

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Waits to be called by a senior — hierarchy says juniors speak only when invitedClaim the floor yourself: If I could jump in… / Can I come in here? Silence reads as disengagement, not respect.
Fears a "wrong" idea will lose face (thể diện) → under-contributesHedge it: For what it's worth… / I'd just add that… A half-formed idea said aloud beats a perfect idea unsaid.
Missing the "build on" move — favours independent positions, not chainingReach for Building on what X said… / That's a good point, and… — the highest-value move in an English meeting.
Drops the final consonant on point, add, build → "poin'", "ad'", "bil'"Release every final: point /pɔɪnt/, add /æd/, build /bɪld/. The /t/ and /d/ carry the meaning.
Reduces I'd like to to "I like" — drops the /d/ contractionDrill I'd /aɪd/ + like to /ˈlaɪk tə/. The /d/ is the politeness marker; losing it sounds blunt.
Silent disagreement instead of a diplomatic alternativeBuild then pivot: That's a good point, and… followed by your alternative. Silence is invisible; a hedged alternative is visible.
Mistakes chime in for rude interruptionChime in is the friendly entry ("usually to agree"). The rude cousins are butt in / barge in — avoid those.
Stresses the wrong syllable in contribute → "CON-tri-bute"Stress the second syllable: con-TRIB-ute /kənˈtrɪbjuːt/.
