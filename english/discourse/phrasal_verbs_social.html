6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Vietnamese has no verb+particle structure → treats "hang out" as two words, translates only "hang"Learn the chunk as one lexical unit. Drill hang out like a single word; never split the meaning.
Reaches for the formal one-word synonym — "I will accompany my friends" / "let us contribute"Swap to the casual chunk in social settings: tag along, chip in. The Latinate verb sounds like a textbook among friends.
Translates the parts literally — thinks catch up = "grab upward", chip in = "break inward"Memorize the figurative meaning: catch up = update each other; chip in = contribute. The particle carries no spatial sense.
Drops the particle entirely — "we will meet this weekend" (loses casual meet up)Always produce the full chunk. The particle IS the casual register — without it, meet is neutral/formal.
Mis-stresses the verb — HANG out, CHIP in (syllable-timed like Vietnamese)Stress the particle: hang OUT, chip IN, meet UP. English phrasal verbs are not equal-beat.
No linking → pronounces hang out as two words with a glottal breakLink consonant-to-vowel: /hæŋ‿ˈaʊt/, /tʃɪp‿ˈɪn/. (See LINKING.)
Drops the verb's final consonant → "han-out" for hang out, "chi-in" for chip inRelease the verb's final consonant — it's the launch for the particle. (See FINAL CONSONANTS.)
Uses these in formal writing — "I will hang out with the client" in an emailRegister check: these are informal/spoken. In workplace writing use the single verb. (See REGISTER SWITCHING.)
