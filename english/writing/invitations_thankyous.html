5Writing task

Write an invitation + a specific thank-you note. Rule: warm · specific · timely. Then reveal the model answers.

Part 1 — invitation: You're hosting a small dinner at your place next Saturday. Write the invite — a warm opener, the specifics (when/where), an RSVP-by date, and a warm close.

Part 2 — thank-you note: A friend gave you a hand-knitted scarf. Write a thank-you that names the exact gift — not "thanks for everything".

Model (warm · specific · timely):

[invitation] Hi everyone — you're invited to a small dinner at our place next Saturday the 12th, 7pm. We'd love for you to join us! Address: 12 Lê Lợi. Please RSVP by Thursday so we can cook the right amount. Hope you can make it! — warm opener + specific when/where + reply-by + warm close

[thank-you note] Dear Hằng, thank you so much for the beautiful hand-knitted scarf — the deep red is exactly the colour I'd have picked, and it's already keeping me warm on the ride to work. I can't thank you enough for the hours you put into it. It meant a lot that you made it by hand. Love, … — names the EXACT gift (hand-knitted scarf, deep red), never "everything"

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Translates kính mời as "Respectfully invited" / "Cordially invite you" for a casual birthday dinnerDrop the formal template for casual events. Use the first-person warm form: We'd love for you to join us. In English the warmth lives in we/us, not a title.
Writes the generic "Thank you for everything" / "Cảm ơn vì mọi thứ" for every recipientBe specific. Name the exact gift or act: Thank you so much for the handmade scarf / for covering my shift Tuesday. The blank after for must be specific, never everything.
Relies on the pronoun (anh/em/chị) to carry warmth — so the English words themselves stay coldEnglish has no anh/em system. Carry the warmth in the words: We'd love for you to join us, It meant a lot that you came.
Deflects a thank-you with "It was nothing" / "có gì đâu" — carried into English as a response to being thankedAccept warmly: You're welcome — I'm glad I could help / It was my pleasure. "It was nothing" can read as brushing the gratitude off. 🔗 THANKING.
Omits the RSVP-by date because a firm deadline feels pushyA clear Please RSVP by [date] is courteous, not pushy. Cambridge attests "RSVP by October 9th." Pair it with Hope you can make it! so it's not a demand.
Sends the invitation / thank-you too late — logistics circulate informally in a group chatTimely. Save the date goes out early (6–8 months for weddings); the thank-you within ~2 weeks. Lateness reads as afterthought, not gratitude.
Forgets the warm close — ends the invitation on the logistics ("…RSVP by Friday.")Add the warm sign-off: Hope you can make it! Two extra words turn a logistics line into a welcome.
Mixes thank (verb) / thanks (noun) and drops the preposition — "Thank for everything" / "Thanks you"Drill the fixed chunks intact: Thank you so much for + [specific], I can't thank you enough for + [specific]. The for is not optional.
Translates cảm ơn as "thank" everywhere — over-using thank where appreciate is the professional choiceFor colleagues / clients / hosts, use I truly appreciate your… (Cambridge: "I appreciate your making the effort to come"). Reserve thank for personal notes.
/θ/ → /t/, final-cluster deletion in thank you /ˈθæŋk juː/, enough /ɪˈnʌf/, appreciate /əˈpriːʃieɪt/Drill the thank-you chunks aloud: tongue-between-teeth for /θ/ in thank, hold /ŋk/, release /f/ in enough. 🔗 FINAL_CONSONANTS.
