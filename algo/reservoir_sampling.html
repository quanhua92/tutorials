Reservoir Sampling (Algorithm R) — interactive RESERVOIR_SAMPLING.md reservoir_sampling.py

Items roll past on a stream of unknown length N. We keep only k slots (the reservoir) and must end up with a uniform sample — every item, early or late, lands in the reservoir with probability exactly k/N. Algorithm R: fill the first k, then for each later item i draw j = randint(0, i) and replace slot j if j < k. Step through the trace, then watch the Monte-Carlo proof that the selection rate is flat across the whole stream. The trace is recomputed in JS from the identical seeded j-sequence as reservoir_sampling.py.  |  quickselect: O(n) selection →

① The algorithm — items stream in, the reservoir self-balances to k/N

What to do: press play (or step). The gold cell is the item currently arriving. The first k items fill the slots. From item k on, we draw j = randint(0, i): if j lands on a slot (j < k) we evict it and put the new item in (green); otherwise the item is dropped. Early items get evicted often; late items rarely get in but are then safe — the two effects cancel to exactly k/N.

② The proof, empirically — selection rate is flat at k/N across the whole stream

What this proves: run Algorithm R many times over the 12-item stream and count how often each item lands in the reservoir. In words: item A (arrives first) and item L (arrives last) are selected equally often. That is the magic: deciding each item's fate before knowing N still yields a perfectly uniform sample.

③ The whole point — O(k) memory instead of O(N)

What to do: drag N (stream length). The naive sampler must store all N items before it can sample; reservoir sampling holds only k slots throughout. For a stream that doesn't fit in RAM, only reservoir works.
In words: reservoir memory is constant in N; naive memory is linear. Both are one pass and O(N) time — reservoir just does it with a tiny fixed window, which is why it works on infinite / unbounded streams.
