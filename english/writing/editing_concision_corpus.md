## A. Cut wordy filler → one lean word

The single biggest win in business English: a four- or five-word phrase that
does the job of one word. Strunk & White's rule — "Omit needless words" — and
Zinsser's "clutter" target exactly these. Every pair below is a listed
transformation in a plain-style guide; the IPA is for the **lean target word**.

## B. De-nominalize → turn a noun phrase back into a verb

The "smothered verb": a heavy noun (`decision`, `discussion`, `consideration`)
propped up by a weak verb (`make`, `have`, `give`). The fix is to **let the verb
do the work**. This is Zinsser's clutter and the plain-language "de-nominalize"
move — the target verbs are dictionary headwords with verified IPA.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| make a decision → **decide** | noun phrase → verb (3 words → 1) | **decide** /dɪˈsaɪd/ | https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/general_writing/academic_writing/conciseness/index.html | ≈#250 (of *decide*) | US/UK |
| have a discussion → **discuss** | noun phrase → verb (3 words → 1) | **discuss** /dɪˈskʌs/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/discuss | ≈#350 (of *discuss*) | US/UK |
| give consideration to → **consider** | noun phrase → verb (4 words → 1) | **consider** /kənˈsɪdə(r)/ UK · /kənˈsɪdər/ US | https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/general_writing/academic_writing/conciseness/index.html | ≈#150 (of *consider*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Purdue OWL "Conciseness" demonstrates the
> de-nominalization move with `making a decision → determine/decide` ("Our Web
> site has made available many of the things you can use for making a decision on
> the best dentist" → "Our website presents criteria for **determining** the best
> dentist"). The Oxford Learner's entry for `discuss` explicitly contrasts
> `discuss` with the wordier `have a discussion about`, calling the latter the
> non-preferred alternative. IPA: `decide` /dɪˈsaɪd/, `discuss` /dɪˈskʌs/,
> `consider` /kənˈsɪdə(r)/–/kənˈsɪdər/ are the standard Oxford/Cambridge forms.

## C. Passive → active (subject–verb–object power)

English rewards **SVO**: the subject *does* the verb to the object. The passive
(`be` + past participle) hides the actor and drains energy — "Mistakes were made"
names nobody. In business writing the active voice is shorter, clearer, and more
accountable. This is Strunk & White's "The active voice is usually more direct
and vigorous than the passive" and the plain-language default.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| mistakes were made → **we made mistakes** | passive → active (actor revealed) | **make** /meɪk/ · **made** /meɪd/ | https://www.grammarly.com/blog/grammar/passive-voice/ | ≈#50 (of *make*) | US/UK |
| a decision was made → **I decided** | passive → active (first-person owner) | **decide** /dɪˈsaɪd/ | https://advice.writing.utoronto.ca/revising/passive-voice/ | ≈#250 (of *decide*) | US/UK |
| it is recommended → **I recommend** | impersonal passive → active owner | **recommend** /ˌrekəˈmend/ | https://advice.writing.utoronto.ca/revising/passive-voice/ | ≈#300 (of *recommend*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Grammarly's "Passive Voice" article uses the canonical
> pair "Passive: **Mistakes were made.** / Active: **We made mistakes.**" The
> University of Toronto Writing Advice ("Passive Voice: When to Use It and When
> to Avoid It") notes the passive is used "to be vague about who is responsible:
> **Mistakes were made**" — i.e. the active restores accountability. The
> impersonal "it is recommended" → "I recommend" is the plain-language move from
> the same tradition. IPA: `make` /meɪk/, `decide` /dɪˈsaɪd/, `recommend`
> /ˌrekəˈmend/ are the standard Cambridge forms.
