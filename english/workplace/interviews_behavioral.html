5Writing task

Write a 4-line STAR answer (Situation / Task / Action / Result) for "Tell me about a time you handled a conflict." Then reveal the model answer.

Model (4-line STAR — note "I" in the Action + a quantified Result):

Situation: The situation was that two teammates disagreed on the launch date, and it was blocking the whole sprint.

Task: My task was to get us unblocked before the deadline.

Action: What I did was I scheduled a 30-minute meeting, let each person state their concern, and proposed a compromise that split the launch into two phases.

Result: The result was that we shipped phase one on time, and team morale recovered within a week.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Under-sells / false modesty — "I just did my job", "chỉ là việc nhỏ"Claim your contribution explicitly. Lead the Action step with "What I did was…" and name the specific decision you made.
Uses "we" instead of "I" in the Action step — "We launched the project"The interviewer is hiring you. Switch to "I" for your personal actions: "What I did was I redesigned…".
No quantified results — "the result was good"Quantify every Result with a number: %, $, time saved. "The result was a 20% increase" beats "positive".
Answers too briefly or rambles — 2 sentences or 5 minutesUse the four STAR signposts as a timer: ~1 (S) + 1 (T) + 3–4 (A) + 1 (R) + 1 (reflection).
Self-promotion discomfort = face (khiêm tốn, sợ "khoe khoang")Reframe: specificity is not bragging — it is doing the interviewer's work. A number is a fact, not a boast.
Drops final clusters under pressure — "resul" for result, "tas" for taskDrill result /rɪˈzʌlt/, task /tɑːsk/–/tæsk/ until the /lt/ and /sk/ are automatic.
Wrong word stress — "RE-sult", "SIT-uation"English stress is meaning-bearing. result = re-SULT, situation = si-tu-A-tion.
Vague generalities — "Yes, I am good at conflict"Answer "Tell me about a time…" with one specific story in STAR order, not a self-assessment.
Vietnamese hesitation fillers — "ờ…", "um…", long silencesUse English fillers to buy time: "That's a great question", "Let me think of a good example".
