## The one structure: Hook → Value → CTA

LinkedIn posts that get read follow one skeleton — the **hook** stops the scroll
(only the first 2–3 lines show before the "see more" cut-off), the **value**
delivers on the hook's promise, and the **CTA** gives the reader a clear next
step. Confirmed across multiple LinkedIn-writing sources (Contentio's
"Hook → Value → Proof → CTA"; Meet-Lea's "Hook → Story → Lesson → Question";
the SocialSnippet "Hook, Story, Lesson, CTA" formula).

```text
HOOK (1–3 lines, stops the scroll)  →  VALUE (3–10 lines, delivers)  →  CTA (1–2 lines, invites)
```

The corpus below is mined into the three parts of that skeleton, plus the
**humble-brag trap** that Vietnamese learners (and many native speakers) fall
into and that every LinkedIn-writing source tells you to cut.

## A. Hooks — the scroll-stopping opener

The first line decides whether the rest gets read. The "announcement" openers
(*I'm excited to share…*, *Thrilled to announce…*) are **real and common** but
now flagged as *generic* by most writing guides — they work for genuine news
(new job, launch) but signal nothing on their own. The *authentic* openers
(*Hot take:*, *Unpopular opinion:*, *I'll be honest*) trade on a pattern-break.

| Thrilled to announce… | a more energetic "good news" opener (funding, product, hire) | /ˈθrɪld tu əˈnaʊns/ | https://magicpost.in/blog/how-to-announce-new-job-on-linkedin-(-5-pre-made-templates) | *thrilled* ≈#6000 | US/UK |

signpost chunks that carry the reader from hook to takeaway. They are read
**aloud** when a creator narrates a post or a video, so the IPA matters.

## C. CTA / engagement — give the reader a next step

A post with no ending leaves momentum dead. The strongest LinkedIn CTAs are
**questions** (they earn comments, which feed the algorithm). These are read
aloud with **rising intonation** when a creator closes a video.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thoughts? | the shortest, most open engagement question (rising tone) | /θɔːts/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/thought | *thought* ≈#300 | US/UK |
| Agree? | a yes/no engagement question (polarises the comments) | /əˈɡriː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/agree | *agree* ≈#500 | US/UK |
| What would you add? | an open question that invites the reader's expertise | /wɒt wʊd juː æd/ UK · /hwɑːt wʊd juː æd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/add | *add* ≈#120 | US/UK |
| Drop a comment | a direct instruction-CTA to engage | /drɒp ə ˈkɒment/ UK · /drɑːp ə ˈkɑːment/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/comment | *comment* ≈#800 | US/UK |
| DM me | "direct-message me" — a soft-offer CTA that moves chat to private | /ˌdiːˈem miː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/dm | (*DM* initialism) | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned anchor #2):** `thought` (noun) confirmed in
> Wiktionary — RP /θɔːt/, GenAm /θɔt/ (without the cot–caught merger); the
> plural **thoughts** adds /s/ after the voiceless /t/ → /θɔːts/ UK · /θɔːts/
> US (the /-ts/ final cluster is exactly what a Vietnamese learner risks
> dropping — see the L1 pitfalls table). YouGlish lists 1,000+ native clips for
> *thoughts*. As a one-word CTA, *Thoughts?* carries **rising intonation**.

## D. The humble-brag trap — the opener to cut

This is the single most-mocked LinkedIn cliché. Vietnamese professional culture
leans on modesty and "face," so learners either (a) overshoot into the boastful
generic humble-brag, or (b) undersell entirely. Every writing source below names
this phrase as the one to avoid.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| humbled and honored | a stock humble-brag pile-up ("so incredibly humbled and honored to announce…") — read by most readers as a flex, not humility | /ˈhʌmbəld ənd ˈɒnəd/ UK · /ˈhʌmbəld ənd ˈɑːnərd/ US | https://magicpost.in/blog/how-to-announce-new-job-on-linkedin-(-5-pre-made-templates) | (*humbled* ≈#7000) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `honoured` /ˈɒnəd/ UK confirmed in Wiktionary (RP
> /ˈɒnəd/, GenAm /ˈɑnɚd/) + YouGlish Modern IPA `ˈɒnəd` + Reverso `ˈɒnərd`/`ˈɒnəd`;
> `humbled` /ˈhʌmbəld/ is the standard Cambridge past participle of *humble*.
> MagicPost names "I'm so incredibly humbled and honored to announce…" as the
> "humbled trap" everyone now reads as a flex; the Corporette and
> LinkedInLunatics sources confirm the phrase is a widely-mocked cliché.

drops a hook + value, Person B (a connection) reacts with a question, A closes
