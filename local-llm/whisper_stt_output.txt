whisper_stt.py - reference impl. All numbers feed WHISPER_STT.md.
pure Python stdlib (no torch, no numpy). Real DFT + mel filterbank demo.
audio model: 16000 Hz mono, FFT=400 (25 ms), hop=160 (10 ms), 80 mel bands.

========================================================================
  SECTION A: audio -> log-mel spectrogram (30 s = 3000 frames)
========================================================================
Whisper is hard-coded to 16 kHz mono. The waveform is chopped into
25 ms windows (400 samples) every 10 ms (160 samples), FFT'd,
collapsed to 80 mel bands, then log'd. The encoder sees an
'image' of size 80 x <frames>.

frames/s = sample_rate / hop = 16000 / 160 = 100
30 s of audio -> 3000 mel frames (= 300 x 10)
mel grid for 30 s = 80 bands x 3000 frames = 240000 values

Tiny live demo (real DFT + mel filterbank, pure Python):
  synthetic 0.12s vowel signal, FFT=64, hop=128, mel bands=8

  mel band (high ^)            frames (time ->)
   7  |.. .  . ... ...
   6  |--::.::.:::::-:
   5  |####**+-=====*#
   4  |%%%%%%%####%%%%
   3  |**###%%%%%%@%%%
   2  |*#%%%%%%%#*##%%
   1  |%%%%%%#*++==-+*
   0  |%%##*+==-=-::==
      +---------------  (low v)

Energy rises from low to high mel bands over time -- the synthetic
formant transition. Real speech produces a far richer 80x3000 grid.

========================================================================
  SECTION B: encoder-decoder + special tokens (autoregressive decode)
========================================================================
The ENCODER reads the mel spectrogram -> hidden states (audio features).
The DECODER is autoregressive: it emits ONE text token per step, each
conditioned on the encoder output + all tokens generated so far.

Special tokens SELECT the behaviour of the SAME weights:
  <|startoftranscript|>    begin (also used for language detection)
  <|en|> / <|vi|> / ...        the detected/source language
  <|transcribe|>   transcribe in the source language
  <|translate|>    translate to English
  <|endoftranscript|>  done
  <|nospeech|>  no speech detected (skip the clip)

Autoregressive decode trace (phrase = 'the quick brown fox'):

| step | emitted token                | conditioned on (mel + tokens so far)        |
|------|------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| 1    | <|startoftranscript|>        | (mel context)                                |
| 2    | <|en|>                       | <|startoftranscript|>                        |
| 3    | <|transcribe|>               | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|>                 |
| 4    | the                          | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe|>  |
| 5    | quick                        | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe... |
| 6    | brown                        | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe... |
| 7    | fox                          | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe... |
| 8    | <|endoftranscript|>          | <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe... |

Full emitted sequence:
  <|startoftranscript|> <|en|> <|transcribe|> the quick brown fox <|endoftranscript|>

Each row is one decoder forward pass (KV-cached after the first). The
encoder runs ONCE; the decoder runs once per output token.

========================================================================
  SECTION C: the model size ladder (tiny -> large-v3)
========================================================================
Whisper ships as 5 sizes. Bigger = more accurate + slower + more VRAM.

| model    | params (M) | VRAM (GB) | rel speed | best for       |
|----------|------------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| tiny     | 39         | 1         | 32        | testing        |
| base     | 74         | 1         | 16        | quick draft    |
| small    | 244        | 2         | 6         | good quality   |
| medium   | 769        | 5         | 2         | high quality   |
| large-v3 | 1550       | 10        | 1         | best quality   |

All sizes share the SAME architecture (encoder-decoder) and mel front-
end; only the layer counts / widths differ. large-v3 is the current
best-quality checkpoint (supersedes large-v1/v2).

========================================================================
  SECTION D: faster-whisper (CTranslate2) -- 4x faster, 50% less VRAM
========================================================================
faster-whisper runs the SAME Whisper weights on CTranslate2: an
optimised C++ Transformer runtime (int8/FP16, fused kernels, no Python
per-token overhead). Default precision is int8 with FP16 compute.

Weight-memory comparison (FP16 OpenAI vs int8 faster-whisper):

| model    | params (M) | FP16 MB | int8 MB | memory of FP16 | reduction |
|----------|------------|---------|---------|----------------|-----------|
| tiny     | 39         | 78      | 39      | 0.50          | 50%       |
| base     | 74         | 148     | 74      | 0.50          | 50%       |
| small    | 244        | 488     | 244     | 0.50          | 50%       |
| medium   | 769        | 1538    | 769     | 0.50          | 50%       |
| large-v3 | 1550       | 3100    | 1550    | 0.50          | 50%       |

int8 weights are exactly HALF of FP16 -> ~50% less memory. Combined
with CTranslate2's fused kernels, the net effect is ~4x faster for the
SAME model and (for greedy decoding) bit-identical transcripts.

========================================================================
  SECTION E: distil-whisper (knowledge distillation)
========================================================================
distil-whisper trains a STUDENT with fewer decoder layers to mimic the
large teacher. distil-large-v3 keeps the teacher's encoder, distils the
32-layer decoder down to 2 layers.

distil-large-v3: 756M params (48.8% of large-v3)
reduction = 1 - 756/1550 = 51.2% fewer params (~49-51% smaller)
speed: ~6x faster than the teacher (fewer decoder layers per token)
quality: WER within ~1% of large-v3 on English (distillation loss
         keeps the student's distribution close to the teacher's)

Trade-off: distil is great for English transcription at speed, but it
trims multilingual coverage -- for non-English use the full model.

========================================================================
  SECTION F: real-time factor (RTF) + word error rate (WER)
========================================================================
RTF = processing_time / audio_duration.
  RTF < 1.0  => faster than real-time (can transcribe LIVE)
  RTF > 1.0  => slower than real-time

| setup                       | hardware       | RTF   | x real-time | 30s clip in |
|-----------------------------|----------------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| faster-whisper large-v3     | RTX 4090       | 0.03  | 33.3        | 0.9      s  |
| faster-whisper large-v3     | Apple M1 Pro   | 0.10  | 10.0        | 3.0      s  |
| faster-whisper medium       | Apple M1 Pro   | 0.15  | 6.7         | 4.5      s  |
| faster-whisper small        | Apple M1 Pro   | 0.35  | 2.9         | 10.5     s  |
| OpenAI whisper large        | CPU (8-core)   | 3.00  | 0.3         | 90.0     s  |

On an RTX 4090, faster-whisper large-v3 transcribes 30 s of audio in
~0.9 s -- 33x real-time. Even an M1 Pro laptop runs medium at 6x real-
time. The official OpenAI model on CPU (RTF ~2-4) CANNOT keep up live.

WER benchmarks (lower is better):
  large-v3 (English)       : ~5-8%  on clean speech
  large-v3 (multilingual)  : ~10-15%
  distil-large-v3          : ~6-9%  (within ~1% of teacher)

Worked WER example (word-level Levenshtein):
  ref : the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog
  hyp : the quick brown fox jump over a lazy dog
  S=2  D=0  I=0   WER = (2+0+0)/9 = 0.2222 (22.2%)
