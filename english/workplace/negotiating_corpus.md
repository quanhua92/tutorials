## A. Conditional propose ‚ÄĒ the "if you‚Ä¶, we could‚Ä¶" trade

The engine of English negotiation: a **concession-for-concession** trade, framed
as a conditional. Cambridge records `provided (that)` (= "if, or only if") as a
formal conditional conjunction with the pattern *"We'll be there at about 7.30,
provided that there's a suitable train"*, and `condition` as *"Agreement was
reached to repay part of the debt, on condition that the remaining debt be
cancelled."* `willing` (CALD: *"not opposed to doing something; ready or eager
to do something"*) carries the offer side; `agree to` carries the acceptance
side. The pattern is **offer + condition**: name what *you* will give, then make
it contingent on what *they* give. This is the trade logic Vietnamese learners
often miss (see L1 pitfalls).

## B. Compromise ‚ÄĒ "meet halfway" / "middle ground" / "split the difference"

The shared-vocabulary set for proposing a midpoint. Cambridge records all three
as idioms: `meet someone halfway` (*"to do some of the things that someone wants
you to do, in order to show that you want to reach an agreement"*; example
*"The buyers wanted to bring the price down from $15,000 to $10,000, so I
offered to meet them halfway at $12,500"*), `split the difference` (*"to accept
that you will reduce your demands or change your opinion in order to reach an
agreement"*; example *"I wanted $50 for the table and she offered $30, so we
agreed to split the difference"*), and `middle ground` (*"a position between two
opposite opinions in an argument"*). Cambridge's thesaurus lists all three as
synonyms of *compromise*. `How about‚Ä¶?` is Cambridge's suggestion-formula
(*"used to make a suggestion"*).

## C. Accept / decline ‚ÄĒ closing the trade (or walking away)

The closing moves. Cambridge records `reasonable` (*"based on or using good
judgment and therefore fair and practical"*), `afraid` in the polite-refusal
formula *"I'm afraid (that)‚Ä¶"* (= "I am sorry to say that‚Ä¶"), and `far` in the
idiomatic *"go that far / go too far"* (= to reach a particular limit or degree).
`deal-breaker` (Oxford Learner's; also CALD business) is *"an issue that makes
someone unable to agree to a deal or plan."* `work` in *"That works for us"* is
the CALD sense *"to be effective or acceptable."* These are the chunks that let
a learner **accept gracefully** or **decline politely without breaking the
relationship** ‚ÄĒ the hardest move for a Vietnamese speaker who has been taught
that direct refusal is rude.
