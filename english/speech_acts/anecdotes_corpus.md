## A. Setting the scene (opening a story)

A story needs a **launchpad** ‚ÄĒ a chunk that signals "I'm switching into
narrative mode now; here is where and when." These openings anchor the listener
in time and tell them to pay attention. Pragmatically they are **story-opening
frame markers** (Schiffrin, 1987; the Cambridge idiom dictionary lists
`So‚Ä¶` as a discourse-initiating marker). Without one, an anecdote lands cold.

## B. Building drama (the turning point)

Once the scene is set, a story needs **drama markers** ‚ÄĒ chunks that push the
action forward and signal the surprise/turn. These are the "and then‚Ä¶" links and
the sudden-event adverbials that make a listener lean in. Without them an
anecdote is a flat list of facts; with them it has tension.

## D. The three narrative tenses (grammar anchors with attested examples)

An anecdote is a **past narrative**, and English uses **three** past tenses to
layer it ‚ÄĒ a contrast Vietnamese has **no equivalent** for (see `ANECDOTES.md`
L1 pitfalls). Each row below is an attested example from a grammar reference,
not an invented sentence.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I was just dozing off when suddenly I heard a scream from outside. | past continuous (background, in-progress) + past simple (the interrupting event) | /a…™ w…ôz d í Ćst ňądo äz…™Ňč …Ďňźf wen ňąs Ćd.…ôn.li a…™ h…úňźrd …ô skriňźm/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/suddenly | grammar anchor | US/UK |
| When the police arrived, the thief had escaped. | past simple (later event) + past perfect (the earlier event) | /wen √į…ô p…ôňąliňźs …ôňąra…™vd √į…ô őłiňźf h…ôd …™ňąske…™pt/ | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/past-perfect | grammar anchor | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Sentence 1 is the **verbatim Cambridge "suddenly" entry
> example**: *"I was just dozing off when suddenly I heard a scream from
> outside."* ‚ÄĒ `was dozing` (past continuous) = the in-progress background,
> `heard` (past simple) = the interrupting main event. This is the canonical
> narrative pattern documented in the Cambridge grammar page *Past continuous or
> past simple?*. Sentence 2 is the **verbatim British Council past-perfect
> example**: *"When the police arrived, the thief had escaped."* ‚ÄĒ `had escaped`
> (past perfect) marks the event that happened **first**, `arrived` (past simple)
> the later one. Together these two sentences demonstrate all three narrative
> tenses and their roles.
