Geohashing — interactive GEOHASHING.md geohashing.py

Encode 2D lat/lng into a 1D base-32 string via bit-interleaving (the Z-order / Morton curve). Every 5 bits halve one axis and become one character; a shared prefix means geographic proximity, so a LIKE '9q8y%' rides an ordinary B-tree. Click the map to encode a point, drag precision to watch the grid subdivide, and open the 9-cell neighbour grid. The trace below is recomputed live in JS from the identical model as geohashing.py.

① Click the Earth → encode to a geohash

What to do: click anywhere on the equirectangular map (or type coordinates). The cyan box is the geohash cell at the chosen precision — every point inside it shares the same string.

② Grid subdivision — each char cuts area to 1/32

What to do: drag precision. A fixed window around San Francisco fills with the geohash grid at that precision. Notice how each +1 character makes the cells 32× smaller in area — precision 5 (~5 km) → precision 6 (~600 m) → precision 7 (~150 m).

③ The 9-cell query — centre + 8 neighbours

Why 9: points on a cell edge get a different prefix from their neighbour (the Z-order boundary problem), so a single LIKE 'prefix%' misses them. The fix is to read the centre cell plus all 8 neighbours. Below is the grid for the Panel-① point at its precision; the cell diverging earliest from the centre shows why you must enumerate, not prefix-match.
