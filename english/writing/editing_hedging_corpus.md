## A. Core modal hedges — may / might / could (the verb-level softeners)

The single most frequent hedge family in academic and professional English. A
modal verb between the subject and the claim is the cheapest, most reliable way
to say "this is possible, not certain." The Manchester Phrasebank "Being
cautious" section lists `may be / could be / might be` as the first explanation
frame: *"These frequent storms may be / could be / might be due to climate
change."*

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **may** (be / have) | used to say that something is possible (the core epistemic hedge) | /meɪ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/may | ≈#60 | US/UK |
| **might** (be / have) | the past of *may*; a slightly *more tentative* possibility than *may* | /maɪt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/may | ≈#120 | US/UK |
| **could** (be / have) | used to show that something is or might be possible | /kəd/ weak · /kʊd/ strong | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/could | ≈#90 | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned real example — modal hedge):** The Oxford Advanced
> Learner's Dictionary entry for **may** (sense 1, "used to say that something is
> possible", web-fetched) carries the verbatim examples *"That may or may not be
> true."* and *"There is a range of programs on the market which may be described
> as design aids."* The Manchester Phrasebank "Being cautious" section attests
> the *may be / could be / might be* frame verbatim. The **could** entry (sense 4,
> "used to show that something is or might be possible", web-fetched) carries
> *"Don't worry—they could have just forgotten to call."* The *may > might*
> tentativity scale (*may* = possible, *might* = a smaller possibility) is the
> calibration the brief pins.

## B. Epistemic phrase hedges — "It seems/appears that…" / "This suggests…"

When a bare modal is not enough, English uses a **distancing frame** — an
impersonal *It*-subject or a *reporting* verb that puts daylight between the
writer and the claim. These are the workhorses of hedged academic and
professional writing.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **It seems that…** | gives the impression; a soft claim that something is the case | /ɪt ˈsiːmz ðət/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/appear | ≈#400 (of *seem*) | US/UK |
| **It appears that…** | gives the impression of being / doing something (synonym of *seem*) | /ɪt əˈpɪəz ðət/ UK · /ɪt əˈpɪrz ðət/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/appear | ≈#500 (of *appear*) | US/UK |
| **This suggests that…** | to indicate that something exists or is true (the evidence → claim bridge) | /ðɪs səˈdʒests ðət/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/suggest | ≈#300 (of *suggest*) | US/UK |
| **The data suggests / indicates that…** | the evidence points toward a conclusion (reporting-verb hedge) | /ðə ˈdeɪtə səˈdʒests ðət/ UK · /ðə ˈdætə səˈdʒests ðət/ US | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **There is some evidence to suggest that…** | a hedged way to introduce a finding without over-claiming | /ðeər_ɪz_sʌm_ˈevɪdəns_tə_səˈdʒest_ðət/ | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned real example #1 — "It seems that…"):** The Oxford
> Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry for **appear** carries the **Language Bank

> the more personal data organizations collect, the more opportunity there is for

> theft easier."* and *"It is possible that the only way to protect ourselves
> against DNA identity theft is to avoid the creation of national DNA databases."*
> The appear entry's "look/seem" sense (B1) attests *"It appears that there has
> been a mistake."* and *"It now appears likely that the school will be closed

>
> **Verification note (pinned real example #2 — "The data suggests…"):** The
> Manchester Academic Phrasebank "Being cautious" → "Being cautious when
> discussing implications" section (web-fetched) lists verbatim: *"The findings
> of this study suggest that…"*, *"The evidence from this study suggests that…"*,
> and *"The data reported here appear to support the assumption that…"* The
> Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry for **suggest** defines the sense
> as *"to indicate that something exists or is true."* `seem` /siːm/,
> `suggest` /səˈdʒest/, and `appear` /əˈpɪə(r)/ UK · /əˈpɪr/ US are the verbatim
> Oxford transcriptions.

## C. Adverb & qualifier hedges — the scalar softeners

These are the **dial** hedges: a single word or short phrase that *turns down*
the strength of a claim by a measured amount. They sit on a spectrum from
near-certain (*arguably*) to heavily qualified (*to some extent*).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **arguably** | used when stating an opinion you believe you could give reasons to support | /ˈɑːɡjuəbli/ UK · /ˈɑːrɡjuəbli/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/arguably | ≈#3000 | US/UK |
| **to some extent** | partly; up to a certain degree (a measured qualifier) | /tə sʌm ɪkˈstent/ | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **in general** | usually; in most cases (avoids the over-generalisation trap) | /ɪn ˈdʒenrəl/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/tend | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **tends to** | to be likely to do something / happen in a particular way | /tendz tə/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/tend | ≈#450 (of *tend*) | US/UK |
| **It is likely that…** | there is a good chance; probable (a moderate-confidence hedge) | /ɪt_ɪz_ˈlaɪkli_ðət/ | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** **arguably** /ˈɑːɡjuəbli/ UK · /ˈɑːrɡjuəbli/ US verbatim
> from the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (web-fetched), which attests *"He
> is arguably the best actor of his generation."* **tend** /tend/ verbatim from
> Oxford (sense 1, "to be likely to do something"), which attests *"Women tend to
> live longer than men."* and *"I tend to agree with many of the points you
> make."* The Oxford **tend** entry's **Language Bank "generally"** (web-fetched)
> attests *"In general, women do not earn as much as men."*, *"Most senior
> management posts tend to be held by men."*, and *"Economic and social factors
> are, to a large extent, responsible for…"* — the *to some extent* family. The
> Manchester Phrasebank "Devices for avoiding over-generalisation" section lists
> *"In general…"*, *"generally"*, and *"tends to"* verbatim.

## D. Strengthening devices — clearly / definitely / the data shows (the confidence escalators)

Calibration is two-sided: a hedge softens, but sometimes the evidence is strong
and the claim **deserves** certainty. These are the escalators that *raise* the
epistemic force — the mirror image of §C. The expert skill is knowing when to
deploy §C vs §D.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **clearly** | used to emphasize that what you are saying is obvious and true | /ˈklɪəli/ UK · /ˈklɪrli/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/clearly | ≈#400 | US/UK |
| **definitely** | a way of emphasizing that something is true and there is no doubt about it | /ˈdefɪnətli/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/definitely | ≈#700 | US/UK |
| **The data shows / clearly shows that…** | the evidence demonstrates a conclusion (strong reporting verb) | /ðə ˈdeɪtə ˈklɪəli ʃəʊz ðət/ UK · /ðə ˈdætə ˈklɪrli ʃoʊz ðət/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/clearly | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **I'm confident that…** | feeling certain that something will happen in the way you expect | /aɪm ˈkɒnfɪdənt ðət/ UK · /aɪm ˈkɑːnfɪdənt ðət/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/confident | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** **clearly** /ˈklɪəli/ UK · /ˈklɪrli/ US verbatim from the
> Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (sense 2 + 3, web-fetched), which attests
> *"These results clearly show that the government's actions are unpopular."*,
> *"The numbers clearly indicate that the market is still strong."*, and
> *"Clearly, this will cost a lot more than we realized."* **definitely**
> /ˈdefɪnətli/ verbatim from Oxford (sense 1, web-fetched), which attests *"I
> definitely remember sending the letter."* **confident** /ˈkɒnfɪdənt/ UK ·
> /ˈkɑːnfɪdənt/ US verbatim from Oxford (sense 2, "feeling certain that something
> will happen"), which attests *"I'm confident that you will get the job."* and
> *"We feel confident that these results are accurate."* — the strengthening
> counterpart to the hedged *"suggests"*.

## E. Calibration spectrum anchors — under-hedged / just-right / over-hedged

These three sentence triples are the bundle's central teaching device: the same
underlying claim, calibrated three ways. Each row is composed from the verified
devices above (§A–§D); the calibration logic is the Manchester Phrasebank's
"strength of knowledge" principle.

### E1. Under-hedged → sounds unfounded / arrogant (claim outruns evidence)

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **This proves that the policy failed.** | states a cause-effect as certain when the evidence only *suggests* it | /ðɪs pruːvz ðət ðə ˈpɒləsi feɪld/ UK · /ðɪs pruːvz ðət ðə ˈpɑːləsi feɪld/ US | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **The results are wrong.** | a bald dismissal with no hedge — reads as aggressive | /ðə rɪˈzʌlts ɑː rɒŋ/ UK · /ðə rɪˈzʌlts ɑːr rɔːŋ/ US | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Why it fails:** The Manchester Phrasebank warns a writer must "avoid
> expressing absolute certainty, where there may be a small degree of
> uncertainty." `proves` and a bare `are wrong` grant no such margin — they read
> as unfounded or arrogant. This is the classic **under-hedge** failure mode.

### E2. Just-right → hedge matches the evidence (the calibration target)

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The data suggests that the policy may have reduced wait times.** | a claim calibrated to its evidence — *suggests* + *may have* | /ðə ˈdeɪtə səˈdʒests ðət ðə ˈpɒləsi meɪ hæv rɪˈdjuːst weɪt taɪmz/ UK · /ðə ˈdætə səˈdʒests ðət ðə ˈpɑːləsi meɪ hæv rɪˈduːst weɪt taɪmz/ US | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **It appears that these results may not generalise to all users.** | a hedged limitation — *appears* + *may* | /ɪt əˈpɪəz ðət ðiːz rɪˈzʌlts meɪ nɒt ˈdʒenrəlaɪz/ UK · /ɪt əˈpɪrz ðət ðiːz rɪˈzʌlts meɪ nɑːt ˈdʒenrəlaɪz/ US | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Why it works:** The Phrasebank's "Being cautious when discussing
> implications" section attests *"The findings of this study suggest that…"* and
> the *may / might / could* frame — exactly the two devices combined here. The
> claim is neither over-stated nor timid; it matches the evidence strength.

### E3. Over-hedged → sounds timid / unsure of everything (hedge drowns the claim)

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **It might perhaps arguably seem to possibly suggest that it could maybe be the case…** | so many hedges stacked that the claim vanishes | /ɪt maɪt pəˈhæps ˈɑːɡjuəbli siːm tə ˈpɒsəbli səˈdʒest ðət ɪt kʊd ˈmeɪbi biː ðə keɪs/ | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |
| **I think it kind of seems like maybe the data could perhaps indicate…** | colloquial over-hedging that erodes all authority | /aɪ θɪŋk ɪt kaɪnd əv siːmz laɪk ˈmeɪbi ðə ˈdeɪtə kʊd pəˈhæps ˈɪndɪkeɪt/ | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/using-cautious-language/ | — (phrase) | US/UK |

> **Why it fails:** Stacking four or more hedges (*might perhaps arguably seem*)
> does not add caution — it signals the writer lacks confidence in *anything*. The
> Manchester Phrasebank lists each device individually precisely because one is
> enough; doubling or tripling them is the **over-hedge** failure mode that
> Vietnamese learners fall into when they are unsure of their English and "hedge
> everything to be safe."
