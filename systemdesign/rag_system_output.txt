==========================================================================
  SECTION 1: Chunking strategies (fixed-size, sentence-aware, semantic)
==========================================================================
Chunking decides what the retriever can EVER return: a fact split across
two chunks is retrievable only as fragments; a chunk that merges two
topics dilutes both. Three strategies on the SAME demo document:

  fixed-size can SPLIT a sentence (chunk boundaries ignore grammar);
  sentence-aware never splits a sentence but may exceed the budget;

==========================================================================
  SECTION 2: TF-IDF embedding (bi-encoder proxy)
==========================================================================
A bi-encoder maps text -> dense vector. We proxy it with TF-IDF so the
whole pipeline runs in stdlib. Embedding = a sparse vector of
tf * idf for every vocabulary term, where idf = log(N/df) rewards terms
that appear in FEW chunks (discriminative) and zeroes terms in every
chunk (stop-word-like). Retrieval math is unchanged: vectors + cosine.

==========================================================================
  SECTION 3: Vector retrieval (cosine similarity, top-K)
==========================================================================
Embed the query, score every chunk by COSINE SIMILARITY (angle between
the two normalized vectors), take the top-K. Cosine is scale-invariant
-- a chunk with small tf magnitude but a matching term angle still scores
well. That is also its weakness: it rewards 'one strong shared term' over
'covers the whole query', which is what the cross-encoder fixes next.

==========================================================================
  SECTION 4: Cross-encoder reranking (re-score the top-K jointly)
==========================================================================
A cross-encoder reads [query, chunk] TOGETHER and outputs a relevance
score. Too slow for the full corpus (~100 docs/GPU-batch), so we only
run it on the top-K the retriever returned. We simulate its score with
IDF-WEIGHTED QUERY COVERAGE = sum(idf[t] for t in query AND chunk) /
sum(idf[t] for t in query). Coverage rewards chunks that touch MORE
distinct query terms -- a different signal than cosine magnitude.

Query: 'vector retrieval similarity search'  (terms: retrieval, search, similarity, vector)

  rank        cov    cosine  chunk
  1        0.7500    0.5029  c0: {search,similarity,vector}
  2        0.4167    0.2373  c2: {retrieval,similarity}
  3        0.2500    0.1411  c3: {vector}
  4        0.2500    0.1797  c10: {retrieval}
  5        0.1667    0.0793  c7: {similarity}

  cosine order : c0,c2,c10,c3,c7
  rerank  order: c0,c2,c3,c10,c7
  -> cross-encoder REORDERED the top-K (coverage != magnitude).
  The chunk covering the MOST query terms (vector + retrieval +
  similarity) is promoted to #1 even if its cosine was not the highest.

==========================================================================
  SECTION 5: Citation extraction (source tracing + relevance gate)
==========================================================================
Each reranked chunk becomes a CITATION: (doc_id, title, chunk_index,
span_text, score). The relevance gate DROPS chunks below threshold --
the RAG abstention mechanism ('I don't know' instead of hallucinating).

Relevance gate threshold = 0.5  (drop chunks below this coverage).

  rank  doc    title                   score  span
  1     doc1   (Vector Databases)     0.7500  "vector databases store dense embeddings..."

  Top citation: doc1, chunk #0 (score 0.7500). The generated answer is
               grounded in this span; the LLM is constrained to cite it.
  Relevance gate DROPPED 4 chunk(s) below 0.5 -> not cited.
  Faithfulness: every claim in the answer must trace to a cited span,
  checked by an NLI entailment model (DeBERTa) in production.
