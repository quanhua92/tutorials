## A. Welcome — opening the room (welcome / acknowledge attendance)

A meeting opening in English is brief and **egalitarian** — the chair thanks the
room and signals readiness in one or two chunks. The "welcome" move almost
always frames the *next* move (the start, §B).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thanks everyone for joining. | thanks to all present for attending | /ˈθæŋks ˈevriwʌn fə(r) ˈdʒɔɪnɪŋ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/join (join /dʒɔɪn/); attested phrase https://oxfordhousebcn.com/en/the-ultimate-guide-to-video-conferencing-in-english/ | ≈#1400 (of *join*) | US/UK |
| Thanks for making the time. | thanks for fitting this meeting into your schedule | /ˈθæŋks fə(r) ˈmeɪkɪŋ ðə ˈtaɪm/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make (make the time); attested phrase https://loughainstitute.com/en/resources/business-english-phrases | ≈#90 (of *make*) | US/UK |
| Good to see you all. | warm acknowledgment of the whole group | /ɡʊd tə ˈsiː juː ˈɔːl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/good (good /ɡʊd/); see /siː/ | ≈#100 (of *good*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `join` /dʒɔɪn/ confirmed in Cambridge, Oxford (OED) and
> Collins; `make` /meɪk/, `time` /taɪm/, `see` /siː/, `good` /ɡʊd/, `all` /ɔːl/
> are the standard Cambridge transcriptions. The full phrase forms are composed
> from these dictionary word-forms with weak `for` /fə(r)/ and `to` /tə/. The
> phrases "Thanks for joining" / "Thanks for making the time" are attested in the
> Oxford House Barcelona video-conferencing guide, the Lougha Institute business
> English phrase list, and The English Center (Amsterdam) spoken tips.

## B. Start — signalling the meeting is now under way

The "start" move transitions from social/welcome to business. The single
highest-frequency chunk in this set is **Let's get started** — it is the default
neutral opener for internal team meetings.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Let's get started. | let us begin (neutral, default) | /lets ɡet ˈstɑːtɪd/ UK · /lets ɡet ˈstɑːrtɪd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/start (start UK /stɑːt/ US /stɑːrt/; *get started* = "to begin") | ≈#130 (of *start*) | US/UK |
| Shall we begin? | a slightly more formal, consultative start | /ʃəl wi bɪˈɡɪn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/begin (begin /bɪˈɡɪn/); https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/begin | ≈#220 (of *begin*) | US/UK |
| Let's dive in. | an informal, energetic start (jump straight in) | /lets ˈdaɪv ˈɪn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/dive-in (dive in = "to start doing something enthusiastically"); dive /daɪv/ | ≈#2000 (of *dive*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `start` UK /stɑːt/ US /stɑːrt/ and the idiom *get

## C. Agenda — naming what the meeting is for

Once started, the chair names the first item. The word **agenda** /əˈdʒendə/ is
the hinge: it is a high-frequency business noun that Vietnamese learners
mis-stress as /əˈdʒen.də/ (wrong syllable) or insert a vowel after /dʒ/.

## D. Handover — passing the turn to a speaker

The "handover" invites a named person to speak first on an item. The two
highest-frequency handover chunks in English meetings are **over to you** and
**kick off** (the latter borrowed from football, now standard business idiom).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| X, would you like to kick us off? | invite X to open / speak first | /wʊd juː ˈlaɪk tə kɪk ʌs ˈɒf/ UK · /wʊd juː ˈlaɪk tə kɪk ʌs ˈɔːf/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/kick-off (kick off = "to start a discussion/activity"); https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/kick-off | ≈#1800 (of phrasal *kick off*) | US/UK |
| Over to you, X. | hand the turn to X | /ˈəʊvə tə juː/ UK · /ˈoʊvər tə juː/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/over (over to you = "it's your turn to take action") | ≈#45 (of *over*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `kick off` = "to start/commence a discussion, job, etc."
> confirmed in Cambridge (entry *kick off*) and Collins; `over to you` = "it's
> your turn to take action" is the example sentence in the Cambridge entry for
> *over*; `over` UK /ˈəʊvə/ US /ˈoʊvər/ and `off` UK /ɒf/ US /ɔːf/ are standard
> Cambridge transcriptions.
