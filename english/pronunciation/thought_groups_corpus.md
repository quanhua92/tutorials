## A. What a thought group is (the core definition + canonical attested examples)

A **thought group** is "a group of words that convey a message" / "groups of
words that form phrases, clauses, or sentences" — a chunk expressing **one
idea**, closed by a short pause and carrying a single **focus word** (the last
content word, which takes sentence stress). The three references below all give
the same definition; the canonical example phrases are quoted verbatim.

## B. The grammatical-boundary pause (the core skill — the pinned examples)

The single most useful rule: **pause at grammatical boundaries** — between
clauses, between a long subject and its verb, before/after a fronted adverbial,
and wherever **punctuation** (comma, semicolon, period) would fall in writing.
The two pinned examples below demonstrate exactly this: a fronted dependent
clause // the main clause, and a main clause // a time phrase // a conditional
tail clause. The constituent phrases are real native utterances (YouGlish clips,
verified HTTP 200); the `//` placement is licensed by the grammatical-boundary
rule documented in all three prosody references.

## C. Where NOT to pause — the 3 rules (San Diego Voice & Accent, verbatim)

Pausing is **not** "pause wherever you run out of breath." There are places a
pause **breaks** the group and sounds unnatural. San Diego Voice & Accent gives
three mechanical rules; each is paired below with the wrong vs. right pausing.

### C1. Rule #1 — no pause between article/preposition/possessive + its noun

| English chunk (pausing marked with `//`) | meaning | IPA (focus word) | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| the store / at school / my daughter | keep determiner+preposition+possessive glued to the noun — NO pause | /stɔːr/ US · /stɔː/ UK · /skuːl/ · /ˈdɔːtə/ UK · /ˈdɑːtər/ US | https://sandiegovoiceandaccent.com/american-english-intonation/3-rules-to-using-thought-groups-in-american-english | phrase | US/UK |

### C2. Rule #2 — keep infinitives together

| English chunk (pausing marked with `//`) | meaning | IPA (focus word) | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| to go / to eat / to drive | keep *to* + verb together — NO pause inside the infinitive | /ɡəʊ/ UK · /ɡoʊ/ US · /iːt/ · /draɪv/ | https://sandiegovoiceandaccent.com/american-english-intonation/3-rules-to-using-thought-groups-in-american-english | phrase | US/UK |

### C3. Rule #3 — a new phrase begins with the conjunction (not ends)

| English chunk (pausing marked with `//`) | meaning | IPA (focus word + pause) | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Do you want coffee // or tea? | ✅ right: the conjunction *or* starts the next group | /ˈkɒfi/ UK · /ˈkɑːfi/ US // /tiː/ | https://sandiegovoiceandaccent.com/american-english-intonation/3-rules-to-using-thought-groups-in-american-english | phrase | US/UK |
| Do you want coffee or // tea? | ❌ wrong: trailing the conjunction sounds broken | /tiː/ | https://sandiegovoiceandaccent.com/american-english-intonation/3-rules-to-using-thought-groups-in-american-english | phrase | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** all three rules are quoted verbatim from San Diego Voice
> & Accent ("3 Rules to Using Thought Groups in American English"). The
> disambiguation example *Maria said, "The student is asleep."* vs *"Maria," said
> the student, "is asleep."* — where pausing alone flips who is asleep — is from
> the same source.

## E. Why pausing helps (comprehension + breath) — the research anchor

A peer-reviewed source on **non-native pausing** confirms the L1 trap: learners
"split clauses unpredictably or skip" pauses, which **lowers** ease of
processing for the listener. Well-placed pauses — at grammatical boundaries —
do the opposite: they hand the listener pre-segmented meaning units and give the
speaker a place to breathe.

| English chunk (claim) | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| "non-native pausing behavior … ease of processing of L2 speech" | learners split clauses unpredictably or skip pauses → harder to process | — | https://www.iastatedigitalpress.com/psllt/article/id/13355/ | — | US/UK |
| "groups of words that make up a single idea" | a thought group = one idea, closed by a short pause (Grant, 2010) | — | https://tfcs.baruch.cuny.edu/thought-groups/ | — | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Phillips, Aguilar, Alt & Darcy (2022), *Pause for thought
> (groups): non-native pausing behavior and ease of processing of L2 speech*,
> Proceedings of the 12th PSLLT (DOI 10.31274/psllt.13355) — peer-reviewed, from
> Indiana University's Second Language Psycholinguistics Lab. The Grant (2010)
> definition is cited verbatim via CUNY Baruch (Tools for Clear Speech).
