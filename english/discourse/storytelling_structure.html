5Writing task

Structure a short anecdote using all four moves: setting + tension + turn + payoff. One chunk per stage.

Model (one chunk per arc-stage):

[Setting] Picture this: I was at the airport, two hours early for my flight.

[Tension] Everything was going fine, until I reached into my bag and my passport wasn't there.

[Turn] That's when I remembered — I'd left it in the taxi's back seat.

[Payoff] Long story short, the driver found it and turned around. I made the flight by four minutes.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls (narrative shape)

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Circular / indirect structure — context first, the point arrives late; you circle the centre before landing on itSwitch to linear arc: setting → tension → turn → payoff, in that order. The listener wants to feel the shape.
No tension build — a flat list of events ("I went, I saw, I came back") with no complicationInsert a tension chunk: "Everything was going fine, until…" / "The problem was…". Without tension it's a report, not a story.
No hook / no opening — starts narrating cold, mid-event, no signal a story is comingOpen with a setting chunk: "So, this one time…" / "Picture this…". One sentence that says "pay attention."
Buries the point — punchline / lesson arrives so late or soft the listener has disengagedFront or mark the payoff: "Long story short…" / "In the end…". The English listener expects a visible resolution.
Missing the turn — jumps from tension straight to resolution, no pivot, so the climax is invisibleUse a turn-marker: "That's when…" / "And then, out of nowhere…". The turn = Labov's evaluation ("so what?").
No tense anchoring — Vietnamese has no past morphology, so the story drifts between time framesAnchor in past simple (main events) + past continuous (background). 🔗 See ANEDCDOTES + NARRATIVE TENSES.
Over-explains the moral — states the lesson at length, or moralises where a native would let the story imply itEither compress ("Long story short…") or state the lesson in one line: "The moral of the story is: always call ahead."
Tells the whole backstory — loads the setting with so much context the tension never arrivesCap the setting at 1–2 sentences (who/where/when). The Story Spine: "Once upon a time…" is one line, not a paragraph.
