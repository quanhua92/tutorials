## A. Frame the talk — give the listener the "map"

A sustained monologue dies in the first 30 seconds if the listener cannot see the
shape of what is coming. The **framing move** previews the structure so the
audience can slot each point into a pre-announced skeleton. EAP Foundation
(*Language for presentations*) names this the "giving the structure" function:
*"This talk is divided into four main parts."* VirtualSpeech (*Speech
transitions*) gives the same move as a presentation outline: *"There are three
key points I'll be discussing…"* / *"This presentation is divided into four
parts…"*. These are the spoken capstone of the `workplace/short_presentations`
signposting chain, extended from a 2-minute talk to a 5-minute **sustained**
monologue where stamina is the added constraint.

>   divided into four parts…"*; *"break this into three parts"* is the same

## B. Signpost transitions — the audible spine between points

The core insight of sustained monologue: **the listener cannot see paragraph
breaks**, so the speaker must *announce* every transition. Newcastle University
Academic Skills Kit (*Signposting*) lists *"Moving on to…"* and *"Turning now
to…"* under "Transitioning between points" — *"Helps readers identify where they
are in the writing's overall structure."* EAP Foundation gives *"This leads/
brings me to my next point, which is…"* and *"Let's now move on to/turn to…"*
under "Transitions". These four chunks are the load-bearing transitions of any
5-minute talk.

## C. Recap + conclude — close the loop

A 5-minute monologue that trails off sounds unfinished and unmemorable. The
**recap** restates the points so they stick; the **conclusion** lands the
takeaway. Oxford, *recap* /ˈriːkæp/, prints the attested example *"Let me just
recap the main points."* Oxford, *conclusion*, glosses *"In conclusion (= finally),
I would like to thank…"* with the example *"In conclusion, I would like to thank
you all for your hard work."* Oxford, *summarize*, prints *"To summarize, the
main conclusions are as follows…"* EAP Foundation lists *"To recap…"*, *"So, to
sum up…"*, and *"In conclusion I'd like to say…"* under "Summing up" / "Concluding".

> - `So to summarize`: Oxford, *summarize* — *"To summarize, the main conclusions
>   are as follows…"*
> - `In conclusion`: Oxford, *conclusion* — *"In conclusion (= finally), I would
>   like to thank…"* / *"In conclusion, I would like to thank you all for your

> - `That covers everything I wanted to say`: builds on *cover* = "to include
>   something; to deal with something" (Oxford, *cover_1*); the *"That covers…"*

> /ˈevriθɪŋ/, `wanted` /ˈwɒntɪd/ UK · /ˈwɑːntɪd/ US, `say` /seɪ/ are the standard
> Cambridge forms.

## D. Stamina supports — refer back + hold the thread

The hidden skill of a 5-minute monologue is **stamina**: not running out of things
to say, not losing the thread, not speeding up. The **back-reference** chunk lets
you reuse material you already said — buying cognitive recovery time and binding
the talk together. Newcastle, *Signposting*, lists *"As indicated earlier,…"*
under "Referring backward" — *"Reminds readers of important information mentioned
earlier."*

| As I mentioned earlier… | back-reference: links back to an earlier point | /əz aɪ ˈmenʃnd ˈɜːliə/ UK · /əz aɪ ˈmenʃnd ˈɜːrliər/ US | https://www.ncl.ac.uk/academic-skills-kit/writing/academic-writing/signposting/ | ≈ high (back-ref) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the source above):**
> - `As I mentioned earlier`: Newcastle, *Signposting* — "Referring backward":
>   *"As indicated earlier,…"* / *"As discussed previously,…"* The *"As I
>   mentioned earlier…"* frame is the spoken equivalent (Cambridge, *mention*:
>   "to write or speak about something/someone, especially without giving much
>   information").

> **Verification note:** `mention` /ˈmenʃn/, `mentioned` /ˈmenʃnd/, `earlier`
> /ˈɜːliə/ UK · /ˈɜːrliər/ US are the standard Cambridge forms. The
> back-reference function is attested in Newcastle Academic Skills Kit.
