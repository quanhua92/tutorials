## Why this bundle (read this first)

Scheduling a meeting looks trivial ‚ÄĒ pick a time, say it. But in English it runs
on a **fixed three-move exchange** (propose ‚Üí confirm ‚Üí, if needed, reschedule)
carried by a **small set of native chunks**, not by a literal translation of
Vietnamese. A learner who skips the chunks produces one of two failures:

1. **Too blunt.** *"We meet Tuesday?"* / *"Can change time?"* ‚ÄĒ Vietnamese marks
   these politely by tone and context, but the English translation drops the
   softening grammar, so it reads as a demand.
2. **Too formal / over-apologetic.** *"Is Tuesday convenient for your
   consideration?"* / *"I am so sorry, I cannot, please forgive me."* ‚ÄĒ the
   learner overweights a routine adjustment and sounds stiff or anxious.

The native system is far lighter: **propose** with `Does Tuesday work?` /
`How about Monday?` / `Shall we say 3 o'clock?`, **confirm** with `That works
for me.` / `I'm free then.` / `Sounds good.`, and **reschedule** (if needed)
with `Can we push it to 3?` / `Can we move it to Wednesday?` /
`Can we reschedule?`. That skeleton carries any scheduling exchange from a
casual coffee to a board meeting.

## 1. The three-move exchange (the scheduling spine)

Every scheduling conversation is three moves. The proposing chunks *open*,
the confirming chunks *close*, and the rescheduling chunks *repair*.

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    P["PROPOSE<br/>Does Tuesday work?<br/>How about Monday?<br/>Shall we say 3 o'clock?"] --> C{"time OK?"}
    C -->|"YES"| CONF["CONFIRM<br/>That works for me.<br/>I'm free then.<br/>Sounds good."]
    C -->|"NO / CHANGE"| R["RESCHEDULE<br/>Can we push it to 3?<br/>Can we move it to Wednesday?<br/>Can we reschedule?"]
    R --> P
    style P fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style CONF fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
    style R fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
```

The single most common error a Vietnamese learner makes is **skipping the
proposing chunk** ‚ÄĒ asking *"Tuesday OK?"* instead of *"Does Tuesday work?"*.
The chunk isn't decoration; it is what makes the move polite. Without the
auxiliary `Does` + the verb `work`, the question reads as a demand for
confirmation rather than a genuine proposal.

>   "is Tuesday an acceptable time for the meeting?"
> - **How about Monday?** /ňĆha ä …ôňąba ät ňąm Ćnde…™/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge Grammar (*How
>   about‚Ä¶?*): "how about + noun phrase ‚Ä¶ when we make suggestions."
> - **Shall we say 3 o'clock?** / É√¶l wiňź se…™ őłriňź …ôňąkl…ík/ UK ¬∑ / É…ôl wiňź se…™
>   őłriňź …ôňąkl…Ďňźk/ US ‚ÄĒ Cambridge Grammar (*Shall*): "We use *shall I* and
>   *shall we* to make offers and suggestions."
> - **Does 3pm suit?** /d Ćz ňĆőłriňź piňź ňąem suňźt/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `suit` (BE
>   CONVENIENT): "whatever time **suits** you best."

## 2. Confirming ‚ÄĒ lock the time with one chunk

Once a time is proposed, the other side confirms with a **short positive
chunk**. The trap is to over-confirm: *"Yes, I agree, that is acceptable to
me."* A native speaker says it in three words.

| Chunk | Job | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| **That works (for me).** | the default accept | the most common confirmation; safe in any register |
| **I'm free then.** | confirm by stating availability | when you want to stress you have no conflict |
| **Sounds good.** | light, casual accept | casual / semi-casual; slightly informal for strict business |
| **Perfect.** | strong positive accept | when the proposed time is exactly what you wanted |
| **Great, see you then.** | confirm + close the exchange | the natural finisher ‚ÄĒ locks the time and ends the turn |

> From `scheduling_corpus.md`:
>
> - **That works for me.** /√į√¶t w…úňźks f…ô(r) miňź/ UK ¬∑ /√į√¶t w…úňźrks f…ôr miňź/ US
>   ‚ÄĒ `work` (Cambridge: "to be effective or have an acceptable result").
> - **I'm free then.** /a…™m friňź √įen/ ‚ÄĒ `free` /friňź/ (Cambridge: "not busy or
>   doing planned things") + `then` /√įen/ ("at that time").
> - **Sounds good.** /sa ändz …° äd/ ‚ÄĒ `sound` link verb (Cambridge: "to seem
>   good").
> - **Perfect.** /ňąp…úňźf…™kt/ UK ¬∑ /ňąp…úňźrf…™kt/ US (Cambridge: "complete and
>   correct in every way").

**`free` vs `available`:** both mean "not busy," but `free` is the spoken
default (`I'm free then.`); `available` is slightly more formal / written
(`I'm available at 3.`). In casual scheduling, `free` wins. Learners who say
only `available` sound like they are reading an email aloud.

## 3. Rescheduling ‚ÄĒ the soft relocation chunks

The highest-stakes move: **asking to change a time already agreed**. English has
a dedicated, soft chunk set for this ‚ÄĒ all of which frame the change as a small,
negotiable relocation, not a crisis. This is where a Vietnamese learner is most
likely to sound blunt ("can change time?") or over-apologetic.

| Chunk | Job | Register |
|---|---|---|
| **Can we push it to 3?** | move the meeting to a later time today | spoken-default |
| **Can we push it back an hour?** | delay it by one hour | spoken-default |
| **Can we move it to Wednesday?** | relocate it to another day | spoken / email |
| **Can we reschedule?** | ask for a brand-new time (vaguest) | any register |
| **Could we reschedule for next week?** | politely move it to next week | formal / email |

> From `scheduling_corpus.md` (the rescheduling set, verbatim):
>
> - **Can we push it to 3?** /k…ôn wiňź p ä É …™t t…ô őłriňź/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge phrasal verb
>   `push back`: "**push back** something ‚Ä¶ to delay something; postpone: The
>   target date for construction has been **pushed back** at least until fall."
>   The relocation form `push it to [time]` is the spoken variant.
> - **Can we reschedule?** /k…ôn wiňź ňĆriňźňą Éed í.uňźl/ UK ¬∑ /k…ôn wiňź
>   ňĆriňźňąsked í.uňźl/ US ‚ÄĒ Cambridge: "to agree on a new and later date for
>   something to happen: I **rescheduled** my doctor's appointment for later in
>   the week." Business-English attested: "Can we **reschedule** tomorrow's
>   meeting for some time next week?"
> - **Can we move it to Wednesday?** /k…ôn wiňź muňźv …™t t…ô ňąwenzde…™/ ‚ÄĒ `move`
>   (Cambridge: "to change the time of"); the collocation `move the meeting to
>   [day]` is attested across COCA + business-email corpora.

**`push back` vs `push it to`:** `push back` = delay (later); `bring forward` =
earlier. `push it to [specific time]` names the new time directly. When you do
not want to risk the *forward/back* ambiguity (see ¬ß6), use `move it to [time]`
or `reschedule` ‚ÄĒ both are unambiguous.

## 4. Prepositions: `at` for clock times, `on` for days

This is the rule a Vietnamese learner must internalise, because Vietnamese has
**no equivalent preposition morphology** ‚ÄĒ time is shown by the time word
itself ("3 giŠĽĚ", "thŠĽ© Ba"), not by a preposition. Carry that into English and
you get *"in Tuesday"*, *"on 3 o'clock"*, or no preposition at all.

| Time expression | Preposition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| clock time | **at** | **at** 3 o'clock / **at** 3pm |
| day of the week | **on** | **on** Tuesday / **on** Wednesday |
| date | **on** | **on** the 15th |
| part of day (general) | **in** | **in** the morning / **in** the afternoon |
| specific night | **on** + night | **on** Tuesday night |

> Cambridge Grammar (*At, on and in (time)*) ‚ÄĒ
> https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/at-on-and-in-time
> ‚ÄĒ `at` for clock times ("at 3 o'clock"), `on` for days ("on Tuesday"). The
> rule is mechanical: **clock ‚Üí at, day ‚Üí on**. Drill it until it is automatic.

## 6. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits when scheduling ‚ÄĒ extend, don't replace, the seed rows from the
spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Drops the proposing chunk** ‚ÄĒ *"Tuesday OK?"* / *"We meet Tuesday?"* (Vietnamese marks politeness by tone, so the bare translation reads as a demand) | Use the full chunk: **Does Tuesday work?** / **How about Monday?** / **Shall we say 3?**. The auxiliary (`Does`/`Shall`) + verb (`work`/`say`) is the politeness. |
| **Blunt rescheduling** ‚ÄĒ *"Can change time?"* / *"I want change time"* (Vietnamese "ńĎŠĽēi giŠĽĚ" has no softening auxiliary) | Use the soft relocation set: **Can we push it to 3?** / **Can we move it to Wednesday?** / **Can we reschedule?**. `Can we‚Ä¶?` frames it as negotiable, not a demand. |
| **Over-apologises on a routine reschedule** ‚ÄĒ *"I am so sorry, I cannot, please forgive me"* (overweights a small change) | One light sorry + the relocation chunk: *"Sorry, **can we push it to 3?**"* A routine reschedule is not a crisis in English ‚ÄĒ keep the apology short. |
| **Preposition errors** ‚ÄĒ *"in Tuesday"*, *"on 3 o'clock"*, or no preposition (Vietnamese has no time-preposition morphology) | Drill the mechanical rule: **clock ‚Üí at** (`at 3`), **day ‚Üí on** (`on Tuesday`). Cambridge Grammar (*At, on and in (time)*). |
| **12h / 24h ambiguity** ‚ÄĒ *"3 o'clock"* with no AM/PM (Vietnamese uses s√°ng/chiŠĽĀu/tŠĽĎi to disambiguate; English relies on AM/PM or "in the afternoon") | Always add **AM/PM** or **in the morning/afternoon** when the time is ambiguous: `3pm`, `3 in the afternoon`. `3 o'clock` alone is genuinely ambiguous in English. |
| **`free` vs `available` confusion** ‚ÄĒ uses only `available` (sounds formal/written) or translates "rŠļ£nh" as `free time` (a noun, not the adjective) | Use the adjective **free** for spoken scheduling: `I'm free then.` / `I'm free on Tuesday.` Reserve `available` for written/formal contexts. |
| **`Wednesday` /ňąwenzde…™/ ‚Üí "Wed-nes-day"** ‚ÄĒ spells it out (three syllables) or drops the /d/ ‚Üí "Wennessay" | The standard form is **two syllables**: /ňąwenzde…™/ (the first `d` is silent). Drill it as "WENZ-day," not "WED-nes-day." ūüĒó [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md). |
| **`Tuesday` /ňątjuňźzde…™/ UK ¬∑ /ňątuňźzde…™/ US ‚Üí "Toos-day" with the wrong vowel** ‚ÄĒ VN maps the /juňź/~/uňź/ to a single vowel | UK keeps the /j/: /ňątjuňźzde…™/ ("TYOOZ-day"); US drops it: /ňątuňźzde…™/ ("TOOZ-day"). Pick one accent and be consistent. |
| **`push back` vs `bring forward` ambiguity** ‚ÄĒ says *"move it earlier/later"* vaguely, or assumes `push back` = earlier | `push back` = **later** (delay); `bring forward` = **earlier**. To avoid all ambiguity, name the new time directly: `Can we move it to 3?` |
| **No subject + auxiliary** ‚ÄĒ *"Is good for me?"* (pro-drop, Vietnamese-style) | Supply the full structure: **That works for me.** / **That's good for me.** Never drop the subject in a scheduling confirmation. |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚ÄĒ this guide, ¬ß1‚Äď¬ß4.
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) ‚ÄĒ open `scheduling.html`, drill the 8 flip cards + the
   role-play **aloud**, marking every `Does`/`Can we`/`work` with a clear
   auxiliary.
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) ‚ÄĒ the writing task: write **a scheduling email line**
   (`Does Tuesday work?` / `Can we move it to 3?`). Then write a second one
   rescheduling it. Read them aloud, recording yourself; check every
   preposition (`at`/`on`) and every auxiliary (`Does`/`Can`).
