5Writing task

Structure a 60-second impromptu answer using PREP (Point-Reason-Example-Point) on a random topic. Hit all four moves, then reveal the model.

Topic: Should meetings be shorter?

Model (PREP — Point, Reason, Example, Point):

P — That's a great question. Off the top of my head, in my view, meetings should be shorter. /ˌðæts ə ˌɡreɪt ˈkwestʃən/ — buy-time opener + Point

R — The main reason is that long meetings drain focus. Reason

E — For example, my team cut meetings to 30 minutes and we made faster decisions. Example

P — So, to sum up, shorter meetings generally work better. /tə ˌsʌm ˈʌp/ — conclude + restate Point

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Freezes in dead silence when asked cold — face-saving (thể diện) instinct of not wanting to sound wrongDeploy a rehearsed buy-time phrase instantly: "That's a great question. Let me think…" A phrase sounds more fluent than silence.
Rambles unstructured — a stream of clauses with no "First / Second / Finally"Rehearse the signpost frame: "There are a couple of points. First… second… finally…" The listener must be able to map your structure.
Translates word-by-word from Vietnamese in real time — slow, halting, disfluentRetrieve chunks, not words. "That's a great question" is one unit, not six words. Drill the 8 chunks until they fire as blocks.
Pro-drop / omitted subject → "Is good question" instead of "That's a great question"Supply the subject + copula. "That's…" — the dummy subject is load-bearing here.
Drops the question acknowledgement — jumps straight into a hesitant answerAlways acknowledge before you answer: "That's a great question" buys ~2 sec and signals respect.
Over-apologizes — "Sorry, my English is bad" instead of structuring the answerNever apologize for your English. Replace the apology with the structure: opener → PREP → conclude.
Starts with a blunt "Yes/No" then cannot expandUse "If I had to choose…" or "On a personal level…" to buy a frame before committing.
No concluding signpost — the answer trails off into "…so, yeah"Land it: "To sum up…" or "So that's my take." A one-line conclude tells the listener the answer is finished.
/θ/ → /t/, /ð/ → /z/ in think → "tink", that's → "zat"Tongue-between-teeth for /θ ð/. Both appear in the very first chunk ("That's… think").
Drops the final consonant in great /ɡreɪt/ → "gray", first /fɜːst/ → "firs"Release every final: /t/ on great, first, that's; /k/ on think. A dropped final kills the signpost.
