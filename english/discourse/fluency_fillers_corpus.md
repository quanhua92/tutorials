## A. Buying-time fillers — stall for thinking (the "fluent" silence)

These buy 1–3 seconds of cognitive planning time while signalling **"I'm
composing an answer"** to the listener. They are the social function of *fluency
itself*: a well-placed filler makes the speaker sound composed and in control,
whereas **dead silence** sounds stuck/unprepared and `um/er/ah` sounds
disfluent. Cambridge documents this function explicitly for *let's see* ("used
when you want to think carefully about something or are trying to remember") and
for *well* as a discourse marker ("its main function is to show that we are
thinking about the question that we have been asked").

## B. The "soften and frame" fillers — delay + signal care

These do double duty: they buy time **and** frame the upcoming answer as
deliberate, tactful, or honest. Cambridge's *put* EXPRESS sense attests the
core: *"to put it"* = to express in words — and the dictionary's own example is
the buying-time question **"how shall I put it?"**. *That's a good question* is
the canonical interview/panel stalling compliment.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| How should I put it | buying time to find the right/tactful wording | /ˈhaʊ ʃʊd aɪ ˈpʊt ɪt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/put | ≈#1800 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| That's a good question | stalling compliment that flatters + buys time | /ˌðæts ə ˌɡʊd ˈkwestʃən/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/question | ≈#700 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| What's the word I'm looking for? | overt word-search; buys time while hunting a term | /ˌwɒts ðə ˈwɜːd aɪm ˈlʊkɪŋ fɔː(r)/ UK · /ˌwɑːts ðə ˈwɜːrd aɪm ˈlʊkɪŋ fɔːr/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/on-the-tip-of-tongue | ≈#2500 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `How should I put it`: "\"What's her new boyfriend like?\" \"Well, **how
>   shall I put it?** He's unusual.\"" — Cambridge, *put* (EXPRESS) example.
>   The variant *should* for *shall* is the common American conversational form.
> - `That's a good question`: named as the #1 "fluent stalling phrase" when
>   "put on the spot" (corpus-based ESL pragmatic lists; British Council
>   speaking-test strategy: "**That's a good question!** … let me see").
> - `What's the word I'm looking for?` / `on the tip of my tongue`: "I know her —
>   her name is **on the tip of my tongue**, but it won't come to me." —
>   Cambridge, *on the tip of your tongue* idiom (the word-search family).

> **Verification note:** `put` /pʊt/ and the "how shall I put it?" example are
> verbatim in the Cambridge *put* entry (EXPRESS sense); `question`
> /ˈkwestʃən/, `good` /ɡʊd/, `word` /wɜːd/ (UK) · /wɜːrd/ (US) are standard
> Cambridge forms; the word-search function is tied to the Cambridge *on the tip
> of your tongue* idiom.

## C. Resume markers — get back on track after a pause/interruption

A thinking pause can derail the thread. These **resume** the speaker's turn,
signalling "I'm continuing / refinding my place" — the complement of a buying-time
filler. Cambridge's *now* entry attests the canonical **"Now where was I before
you interrupted me?"**.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Where was I? | what point had I reached? (resume after a pause) | /ˌweə(r) wəz ˈaɪ/ UK · /ˌwer wəz ˈaɪ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/now | ≈#1500 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| As I was saying | returning to the topic before the interruption | /əz aɪ wəz ˈseɪɪŋ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/say_1 | ≈#900 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `Where was I?`: "**Now where was I** before you interrupted me?" — Cambridge,
>   *now* (used to introduce/emphasize what you are saying).
> - `As I was saying`: documented as a return-to-previous-subject discourse
>   marker across the discourse-marker literature (Schiffrin 1987; O'Keeffe et
>   al. 2007); "as I was saying is 10× more frequent than *as he was saying*"
>   (Wiley, *Learners' Perceived Development of Spoken Grammar Awareness*).

> **Verification note:** the `where was I?` resuming function is attested by the
> Cambridge *now* example; `say` /seɪ/, `saying` /ˈseɪɪŋ/, `was` weak /wəz/,
> `where` /weə(r)/ UK · /wer/ US are standard Cambridge forms.

## D. The disfluency contrast — minimize these (um / uh / er)

Buying-time **phrases** are the *fluent* alternative to filled pauses. Filled
pauses (`um`, `uh`, `er`, `ah`) are not banned, but research on perceived L2
fluency shows **lexicalized fillers** ("let me think", "well") are rated as more
fluent than non-lexicalized `um/uh`, and both beat dead silence. The paradox at
the heart of this bundle: a well-placed *phrase* filler sounds **more** fluent
than silence, because it shows the speaker is in control of the floor.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

> **Verification note:** `um` /ʌm/, `uh` /ʌ/ (US) / /ɜː/ are Cambridge entries;
> the "lexicalized filler > filled pause > silence" fluency ranking is reported
> in the pragmatic-marker / L2-fluency literature (see Sources).
