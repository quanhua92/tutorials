6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Translates the imperative + pronoun directly — "Give me…" / "You do it" (from anh cho em…) — sounds rude because English has no pronoun hierarchy.Climb the ladder: Could you…, please? The modal + please does the work anh/em used to do.
Under-hedges requests — defaults to Can you everywhere, sounds too casual with a client or senior.Match the rung to the distance: Can you (peer) → Could you (default) → Would you mind (favour). When in doubt, climb one rung.
Reaches for the to-infinitive after mind — "Would you mind to open the window?"Mind takes the -ing form, always: Would you mind opening…? Drill it as a fixed chunk.
Offers with the present simple — "I do it for you" / "I help you" (VN has no tense morphology).Use 'll for spontaneous offers: "I'll do it." Never the present simple to offer.
Drops the please / thanks uptake — accepts with a nod, declines with a bare "No" that reads as blunt.Always bookend: Yes, please / That'd be great, thanks to accept; No, thanks to decline.
Pronounces the modal in full /kʊd/ /wʊd/ — every syllable equal weight, sounds stiff.Reduce the modal: Could you /kədʒə/, Would you /wədʒə/. Content words strong, modals weak.
Uses Shall I in US contexts and sounds formal/literary to American ears.Shall I is everyday UK, "A2 formal in US". In US English prefer Should I…? / Do you want me to…?
Misses the closing thanks — "Could you send the file?" (silence) — the request hangs.Always close: "Could you send the file? Thanks." The thanks completes the speech act.
