## A. Positive / specific praise (open the feedback with what worked)

The professional-English way to open feedback: **name a specific thing that
worked**, not a generic "good job." Cambridge records `work` (verb, past
`worked`) with the sense "to be effective or have a good result"; `One thing
that worked well was…` is the canonical retrospective / debrief opener
(attested across meeting-facilitation and feedback guides). `I really liked how
you…` is the specific-praise frame (Cambridge `like` + `how` = the manner
compliment). These are **specificity anchors** — the opposite of the vague
praise that marks weak feedback.

## B. SBI constructive core (Situation-Behavior-Impact)

The engine of English constructive feedback: describe the **specific situation
and observable behavior**, then the **impact** it had — not a judgment of the
person. This is the **SBI™ model** originated by the Center for Creative
Leadership (CCL): *"Describe the Situation… Describe the actual, observable
Behavior… Describe the Impact that the person's behavior had"* (CCL, "Improve
Talent Development With Our SBI Feedback Model"). The canonical sentence frame
is `When you [behavior], the impact was [impact]` — attested across SBI
template sources (FeedbackPulse, Revolution Learning, et al.). `I noticed
that…` (Cambridge `notice`, past `noticed`) is the observation opener that
keeps feedback factual.

## C. Forward-looking suggestion + supporting lexis

The "forward" move: after the SBI observation, offer a **specific, hedged
suggestion** the person can act on — never a blunt "you should." `Next time,
you might want to…` is the polite-hedge suggestion (Cambridge `might` + `want
to`); `Have you considered…?` is the question-form suggestion Cambridge's
Dictionary Blog documents as a polite way to make suggestions sound nicer
(*"Have you considered/thought about speaking to James?"*). Vietnamese
learners under-use both — they default to "You should…" which sounds like an
order.
