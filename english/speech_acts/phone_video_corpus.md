## A. Phone-call openings ‚ÄĒ identifying yourself / asking for someone

On a phone call you cannot see the other person, so the first turn is always an
**identification**. English uses the dummy pronoun *"it"* (`Hi, it's X`) or the
demonstrative *"this is"* (`Hello, this is X`) ‚ÄĒ **never** *"I am X"*, which
sounds like a roll-call or a self-introduction at a meeting, not a call. Each
chunk below is glossed in a learner's dictionary under the *phone / speak to*
sense. **"Hi, it's X" is the pinned real example** (Cambridge `it` entry
documents *"it's me"* as the phone self-identification; Cambridge `speak to`
documents *"I'd like to speak to‚Ä¶"* as the canonical request).

## B. Phone transfer & hold ‚ÄĒ "I'll connect you"

## C. Video-call tech check ‚ÄĒ "can you hear/see me?"

business. These are the chunks a Vietnamese learner most often freezes on,

## D. Video-call connection problems ‚ÄĒ "you're frozen / breaking up / lost"

When the connection fails, English speakers **narrate the problem out loud**
rather than go silent. Vietnamese learners tend to freeze and wait; the fix is to
say the diagnostic chunk. Cambridge documents both `frozen` (Immobility: "not
moving at all") and `break up` ("STOP BEING HEARD: If someone who is talking on a
mobile phone is breaking up, their voice can no longer be heard clearly").
**"I think we lost them" is the pinned dropped-caller example** (Oxford `lose`:
"not understand/hear" sense ‚ÄĒ *"His words were lost (= could not be heard)"*).
