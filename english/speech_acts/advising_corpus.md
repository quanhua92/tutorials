## A. Soft advice — hedged, low-pressure (the "plant the idea" tier)

The safest register. The speaker does **not** tell the listener what to do; they
float a possibility and let the listener own the decision. This is the tier
Vietnamese learners most often **skip** — jumping straight to *You should…* —
because the L1 advice particles (*nên* "should", bare imperative *đi*/*lên*
"go"/"do it") have no soft equivalent. English softens with **modals** (*might,
could*) and **hedged verbs** (*might want to, might be worth*). The hedge is the
politeness; dropping it sounds like a lecture.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| You might want to… | soft suggestion — "it could be a good idea to" (no pressure) | /juː maɪt ˈwɒnt tə/ UK · /juː maɪt ˈwɑːnt tə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/might | ≈#120 (of *might*) | US/UK |
| Have you tried…? | soft advice as a question — "consider this option (you may already have)" | /həv juː traɪd/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/try_1 | ≈#180 (of *try*) | US/UK |
| It might be worth + -ing | hedged recommendation of an action — "this could pay off" | /ɪt maɪt biː wɜːθ/ UK · /ɪt maɪt biː wɜːrθ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/worth_1 | ≈#500 (of *worth*) | US/UK |
| It's worth + -ing | recommendation of an action — "this is worth doing" | /ɪts wɜːθ/ UK · /ɪts wɜːrθ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/worth_1 | ≈#500 (of *worth*) | US/UK |
| You could always… | soft fallback suggestion — "this option remains available" | /juː kʊd ˈɔːlweɪz/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/always | ≈#90 (of *always*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *might* + *want to* is the canonical soft-advice frame,
> built from the Cambridge *might* modal entry ("used to express the possibility
> that something will happen or be done"). *Have you tried…?* uses the Oxford
> *try* entry (verb sense "to use, do or test something in order to see if it
> works"). *It might be worth + -ing* and *It's worth + -ing* use the Oxford
> *worth* adjective entry sense 2 ("used to recommend the action mentioned
> because you think it may be useful"), verbatim examples *"It's worth making an
> appointment before you go"* and *"This idea is well worth considering."*
> *You could always…* is the Oxford *always* adverb entry sense 5 — explicitly
> labelled "can/could always… used to **suggest a possible course of action**",
> with the verbatim example *"If it doesn't fit, you can always take it back."*
> IPA: *might* /maɪt/, *want* UK /wɒnt/ · US /wɑːnt/, *try/tried* /traɪd/,
> *worth* UK /wɜːθ/ · US /wɜːrθ/, *always* /ˈɔːlweɪz/ — all pulled verbatim from
> Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary and cross-checked in Cambridge-based
> teaching sources.

## B. Stronger advice — committed, but still softened (the "I'm telling you, kindly" tier)

The speaker commits to a recommendation but still leaves the listener the final
call. The two softeners are: (1) **`I'd`** (the contracted *I would* — a
conditional, not a direct "I do"), and (2) the **second conditional** *If I were
you* frame, which puts the speaker **inside the listener's shoes** rather than
above them. This is the tier that replaces *You should…* in any context where
the listener is a peer, a friend, or a client — *should* without a modal reads
as authority or lecture.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'd suggest (that)… | committed recommendation — "my advice is" (softer than *should*) | /aɪd səˈdʒest/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/suggest | ≈#300 (of *suggest*) | US/UK |
| I'd recommend + -ing / that… | committed recommendation — slightly more authoritative than *suggest* | /aɪd ˌrekəˈmend/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/recommend_1 | ≈#700 (of *recommend*) | US/UK |
| If I were you, I'd… | second-conditional advice frame — "in your position, I would" | /ɪf aɪ wɜː juː aɪd/ UK · /ɪf aɪ wər juː aɪd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/conditionals-if | ≈#60 (of *if*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *I'd suggest (that)…* uses the Oxford *suggest* verb
> entry sense 1, with the verbatim Express Yourself example *"I suggest you have
> another look at the house before you make a decision."* and the collocation
> note *"I would suggest…"*. *I'd recommend + -ing* uses the Oxford *recommend*
> verb entry sense 2 ("to advise a particular course of action"), with the
> verbatim Express Yourself example *"I'd recommend waiting a few months"* and
> *"We'd recommend you to book your flight early."* Oxford's own synonym note
> states: *"Advise is a stronger word than recommend… I advise you… can suggest
> that you know better than the person you are advising: this may cause offence
> if they are your equal or senior to you. I recommend… mainly suggests that you
> are trying to be helpful and is less likely to cause offence."* *If I were
> you, I'd…* is the **second conditional**, documented in the Cambridge Grammar
> entry *Conditionals: if* (structure: *If + past simple, … would/wouldn't +
> infinitive*); the *were* form (not *was*) is the standard advice form.
> Cambridge's verbatim example: *"I wouldn't ask my parents for a loan if I were
> you."* IPA: *suggest* /səˈdʒest/ (Oxford, also /səɡˈdʒest/), *recommend*
> /ˌrekəˈmend/ (Oxford — note the stress on the final syllable), *were* UK
> /wɜː/ · US /wər/ — all Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary transcriptions,
> cross-checked in Cambridge.

## C. The suggestion question — advice dressed as a question (the "collaborative" tier)

A native speaker's favourite move: **ask a question whose answer is the advice**.
*Why don't you…?* is not a real question — it is a recommendation phrased as
inquiry, which is why it never sounds like a lecture. *How about + -ing?* and
*What about + -ing?* do the same with even less commitment. Vietnamese has the
exact same device (*sao em không…?* = "why don't you…?"), so the L1→English
transfer is clean here — this is the **easy win** of the bundle.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why don't you…? | advice as a question — "I recommend that you…" | /waɪ ˈdəʊnt juː/ UK · /waɪ ˈdoʊnt juː/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/suggest | ≈#10 (of *why*) | US/UK |
| Why not + verb…? | compressed form of the above — "consider just…" | /waɪ nɒt/ UK · /waɪ nɑːt/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/suggest | — | US/UK |
| How about + -ing / noun? | suggestion probe — "what do you think of…?" | /ˈhaʊ əˈbaʊt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/suggest | ≈#80 (of *about*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *Why don't you…?* / *Why not…?* / *How about + -ing?*

> transcriptions cross-checked in Cambridge.

## D-short. Dialog anchors (the role-play's advice focus chunks)

These lines anchor the two role-plays in `advising.html`. **Dialog 1** is casual
(a friend between friends) — Person B asks for advice, Person A answers on the
**soft** rungs. **Dialog 2** is the same situation in a **more committed**
register — Person A shifts to the **strong** rungs. Every bold chunk is a row in
§A–§C above.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| What do you think I should do? | request for advice — the opening line of every advice exchange | /ˈwɒt duː juː ˈθɪŋk aɪ ʃʊd duː/ UK · /ˈwɑːt duː juː ˈθɪŋk aɪ ʃʊd duː/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/should | ≈#40 (of *should*) | US/UK |
| That's a tough one. | empathy stall before advice — "this is genuinely hard" | /ˈðæts ə tʌf wʌn/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/tough_1 | ≈#1500 (of *tough*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the dialog assembles corpus rows from §A–§C verbatim;
> no line is invented. *What do you think I should do?* uses the Oxford *should*
> modal entry (sense "used to say or ask what is the right thing to do"). *That's
> a tough one* uses the Oxford *tough* adjective entry (sense "difficult") — a
> standard empathy-before-advice stall that lets the adviser buy thinking time
> and signals the problem is being taken seriously. *tough* /tʌf/ is exactly the
> /ʌf/ coda Vietnamese has no slot for (see
> [FINAL_CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md)); *should* /ʃʊd/
> carries the /ʃ/ + /ʊ/ + /d/ that learners often collapse.
