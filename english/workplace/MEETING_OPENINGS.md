## Why this is the gateway bundle for workplace speaking

A Vietnamese professional's first English meeting usually goes one of two ways ‚ÄĒ
both wrong. Either they **over-defer** ("Respectfully, I would like to humbly
wait‚Ä¶") or they **freeze** and wait to be called on, because that is exactly how
a Vietnamese meeting works: the most senior person opens, juniors stay silent
until invited, and elaborate formal openings signal respect. English meetings
invert almost all of that. The chair still opens, but the opening is **short,
efficient, and egalitarian** ‚ÄĒ a thank-you, a one-line agenda, a handover ‚ÄĒ and
everyone is expected to contribute from the first minute.

The single skill that unlocks the rest of Phase 2 is the **four-move opening**:
welcome ‚Üí start ‚Üí agenda ‚Üí handover. Get those four chunks out cleanly and the
meeting is yours to participate in. Stumble on the opening and you spend the
rest of the meeting recovering.

## 1. The four-move opening (the anatomy)

chunks vary by register (casual internal sync vs. formal client call), but the
**skeleton is constant**:

## 2. The welcome move ‚ÄĒ thank the room (briefly)

English openings are **short on ceremony**. A thank-you that runs more than one
clause sounds stiff. The three chunks below cover the whole register range:

> From `meeting_openings_corpus.md`:
>
> - **Thanks everyone for joining.** /ňąőł√¶Ňčks ňąevriw Ćn f…ô(r) ňąd í…Ē…™n…™Ňč/ ‚ÄĒ the
>   default external/cross-team welcome.
> - **Thanks for making the time.** /ňąőł√¶Ňčks f…ô(r) ňąme…™k…™Ňč √į…ô ňąta…™m/ ‚ÄĒ acknowledges
>   people carved a slot from a busy day; very common in client/vendor calls.
> - **Good to see you all.** /…° äd t…ô ňąsiňź juňź ňą…Ēňźl/ ‚ÄĒ warm, internal, recurring
>   team.

**Why "making the time" and not "giving your time"?** `make the time` =
proactively free up a slot (the neutral, expected framing); `give your time`
sounds like a favour or a sacrifice and reads as over-formal or even mildly
guilt-tripping in a peer meeting. The collocation `make the time` is attested
across the Oxford House, Lougha Institute and English Center business-English
sources (see Sources).

## 3. The start move ‚ÄĒ "Let's get started" is the default

> From `meeting_openings_corpus.md`:
>
> - **Let's get started.** /lets …°et ňąst…Ďňźt…™d/ UK ¬∑ /lets …°et ňąst…Ďňźrt…™d/ US ‚ÄĒ
>   neutral default; works in 90% of internal meetings.
> - **Shall we begin?** / É…ôl wi b…™ňą…°…™n/ ‚ÄĒ consultative, slightly formal; the
>   `shall we‚Ä¶?` frame politely invites agreement instead of asserting it.
> - **Let's dive in.** /lets ňąda…™v ňą…™n/ ‚ÄĒ energetic, informal; signals "no
>   preamble, straight to work".

**The pragmatic choice:** `Let's get started` is the safe default ‚ÄĒ it is
neither cold nor over-warm. `Shall we begin?` adds a hair of formality (useful
with senior stakeholders or when the room includes people you don't know well).
`Let's dive in` is for tight, trusted teams where everyone is already briefed.

**Pronunciation note (L1-relevant):** `started` ends in the cluster /t…™d/ (UK)
or /t…ôrd/ (US). Vietnamese has no consonant clusters and tends to drop the
final /d/ ‚Üí "Let's get start-it". Hold the cluster: drill
/ňąst…Ďňźt…™d/ ‚Üí release the final /d/ audibly. ūüĒó See
[FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md).

## 4. The agenda move ‚ÄĒ "agenda" is the hinge word

Once started, the chair names what the meeting is for. The word **agenda**
/…ôňąd íend…ô/ is the pivot ‚ÄĒ and it is a classic Vietnamese trap (mis-stressed or
given a parasitic vowel after /d í/).

> From `meeting_openings_corpus.md`:
>
> - **First item on the agenda.** /ňĆf…úňźst ňąa…™t…ôm …ín √į…ô …ôňąd íend…ô/ UK ¬∑
>   /ňĆf…úňźrst ňąa…™t…ôm …Ďňźn √į…ô …ôňąd íend…ô/ US ‚ÄĒ opens the first topic.
> - **We've got X on the agenda.** /wiňźv …°…ít ‚Ä¶ …ín √į…ô …ôňąd íend…ô/ UK ¬∑
>   /wiňźv …°…Ďňźt ‚Ä¶ …Ďňźn √į…ô …ôňąd íend…ô/ US ‚ÄĒ scopes the whole meeting.
> - **Moving on to‚Ä¶** /ňąmuňźv…™Ňč ňą…ín t…ô/ UK ¬∑ /ňąmuňźv…™Ňč ňą…Ďňźn t…ô/ US ‚ÄĒ the
>   transition cue between items.

**Stress is on the second syllable:** a-**GEN**-da, not **A**-gen-da. Vietnamese
learners often front-stress it (influenced by Vietnamese's sesquisyllabic
pattern) or break the /d í/ with a vowel ‚Üí /…ôňąd íen…ôd…ô/. Drill the stress with a
clap on the second syllable: a-**GEN**-da.

## 5. The handover move ‚ÄĒ pass the turn by name

The handover is where Vietnamese learners freeze ‚ÄĒ they expect to be *called*,
not to *be called on and start immediately*. In English, the handover is
explicit and the named person is expected to begin within a second or two.

> From `meeting_openings_corpus.md`:
>
> - **X, would you like to kick us off?** /w äd juňź ňąla…™k t…ô k…™k  Ćs ňą…íf/ UK ¬∑
>   /w äd juňź ňąla…™k t…ô k…™k  Ćs ňą…Ēňźf/ US ‚ÄĒ formal handover; `kick off` = "to
>   start/commence" (Cambridge + Collins).
> - **Over to you, X.** /ňą…ô äv…ô t…ô juňź/ UK ¬∑ /ňąo äv…ôr t…ô juňź/ US ‚ÄĒ the neutral
>   default; `over to you` = "it's your turn to take action" (Cambridge *over*).

**`kick off` is borrowed from football** (the opening kick) and is now standard
business idiom ‚ÄĒ Cambridge and Collins both list the "commence a discussion/job"
sense. It is **not** slang in a meeting context; it is the expected formal
handover verb.

## 7. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." Vietnamese meeting culture and English meeting culture
collide on **hierarchy, ceremony, and turn-taking** ‚ÄĒ and the opening is where
the collision is most visible. These are the specific interference traps a
Vietnamese speaker hits when opening or being handed the floor in an English
meeting.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Over-defers / waits to be called** ‚ÄĒ in VN meetings the most senior opens and juniors stay silent until invited; you freeze when the turn reaches you | Take the turn within ~1‚Äď2 seconds of a handover. Lead with the chunk, not an apology: *"Sure ‚ÄĒ thanks. So, on the Q3 numbers‚Ä¶"*. Silence reads as unprepared, not respectful. |
| **Overly elaborate formal openings** ‚ÄĒ translates the VN ceremonial opening word-for-word ("Respectfully, I would like to humbly begin by expressing‚Ä¶") | English openings are **one clause each**: welcome ‚Üí start ‚Üí agenda ‚Üí handover. Cut the ceremony. *"Thanks everyone for joining. Let's get started. First item‚Ä¶"* is complete and correct. |
| **Hedges the start with apology** ‚ÄĒ *"Sorry, can I start?"* / *"Maybe we can begin?"* | Don't ask permission to start a meeting you're chairing. *"Let's get started"* is a polite assertion, not a command. Save *"sorry"* for genuine interruptions (ūüĒó [INTERRUPTING](../speech_acts/INTERRUPTING.md)). |
| **Drops final consonants** ‚Üí *"Let's get start-it"* (drops /d/), *"thank"* (drops /s/), *"kic-off"* (drops the /k/ release) | Release every final: /ňąst…Ďňźt…™d/, /őł√¶Ňčks/, /k…™k …íf/. ūüĒó Drill [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md) first ‚ÄĒ every opening chunk ends in a sound VN omits. |
| **Mis-stresses `agenda`** ‚Üí /ňą√¶d íend…ô/ (front-stress) or /…ôňąd íen…ôd…ô/ (parasitic vowel after /d í/) | Stress the **second** syllable: a-**GEN**-da /…ôňąd íend…ô/. The /d í/ is one sound ‚ÄĒ no vowel inserted. |
| **`/őł/ ‚Üí /t/`** ‚Üí *"tanks everyone"* for *thanks* | Tongue between the teeth for /őł/: **th**anks /őł√¶Ňčks/. ūüĒó [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md). |
| **Translates "xin ph√©p" / "cho ph√©p" literally** ‚Üí *"With your permission, I would like to open‚Ä¶"* | There is no English equivalent at meeting-open. Permission-asking belongs to *interrupting*, not *opening*. Use *"Let's get started."* |
| **Names the agenda but never hands over** ‚ÄĒ lists items, then waits for someone to volunteer | Always end the opening with an explicit handover **by name**: *"Mai, would you like to kick us off?"* Silence is not an invitation in English. |
| **Weak /…ô/ vs full forms confused** ‚ÄĒ stresses grammar words (*for*, *to*, *the*) in "Thanks **for** making **the** time" | Keep **for/to/the** weak: /f…ô(r)/, /t…ô/, /√į…ô/. Stress only content words: **Thanks**, **making**, **time**. ūüĒó [SENTENCE STRESS](../pronunciation/SENTENCE_STRESS.md). |
| **No /r/ in US `start`, `started`** ‚Üí says UK form in a US room (or vice versa) | Match the room's accent. US `started` /ňąst…Ďňźrt…™d/ has a clear /r/; UK /ňąst…Ďňźt…™d/ does not. Flag your default and stay consistent. |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚ÄĒ this guide, ¬ß1‚Äď¬ß5.
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) ‚ÄĒ open `meeting_openings.html`, drill the 8 flip cards,
   then run the role-play **aloud** as Person A (the chair): welcome ‚Üí start ‚Üí
   agenda ‚Üí handover, no pauses, as one flowing sequence.
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) ‚ÄĒ the writing task: write a **3-line meeting opening**
   (welcome + agenda + handover) for a real meeting you have this week. Read it
   aloud, recording yourself; check every final consonant is audible and
   `agenda` is stressed on the second syllable.
