6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Freezes / never starts a conversation — introversion + face (mình không giỏi tiếng Anh, nói sai mất mặt)Walk up within 5 minutes and use the opener: "Hi — what brings you here?" Silence reads as uninterested, not modest.
Jumps straight to business — "I am developer, I want job"Do the three moves in order: opener → what-do-you-do → connect. Business surfaces after the connect, never before.
Refuses to answer "What do you do?" — "I just work at a company" (modesty / fear of boasting)Answer with a frame: "I'm in marketing" or "I'm a software engineer." Stating your job is the social lubricant, not boasting.
Re-words the question — "What is your work?" / "What job do you do?"Use the canonical chunk: "What do you do?" Don't reword it — it is the single expected question.
Treats "let's grab a coffee sometime" as a real appointment — chases for a date the next dayPer Oxford it's "often a polite gesture rather than a definite plan." Say it back, connect on LinkedIn, let the follow-up come through the channel.
Forgets to exchange contact — VN relationships run through the mutual introducer, so the card step feels transactionalAlways end with an explicit connect move: "Let's connect on LinkedIn" or "Here's my card." No exchange = the conversation didn't happen.
Over-formal self-intro — "My name is Nguyen. I am working at FPT Corporation as the position of…"English self-intro is one short clause: "I'm Mai — I'm a software engineer." The CV belongs on LinkedIn.
Drops the /ŋz/ cluster in brings → "What bring you here?"Hold the cluster: /brɪŋz/ — release the /z/ audibly. Drill Final Consonants.
Front-stresses engineer → /ˈendʒənɪə/ ("EN-gi-neer")Stress the third syllable: en-gi-NEER /ˌendʒəˈnɪr/. Drill with a clap on the last syllable.
/ʃ/ → /s/ in shoot → "soot you an email"Round the lips for /ʃ/: shoot /ʃuːt/. Minimal pair: shoot /ʃuːt/ vs suit /suːt/.
Says "linked-IN" with stress on the second syllableStress the first syllable, weaken the second: LINK-ed-in /ˈlɪnktɪn/. Keep the /kt/ cluster tight.
