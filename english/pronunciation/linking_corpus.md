## A. Consonant-to-vowel (C-V) linking ‚ÄĒ word-final C glides to the next vowel

The workhorse of connected speech: when a word **ends in a consonant** and the
next word **begins with a vowel**, that final consonant hops over to become the
**onset** of the next syllable. There is no gap, no glottal stop ‚ÄĒ the words
sound glued. This is why "an apple" is /…ô.ňąn√¶pl/, not /…ôn ‚Ä¶ ňą√¶p…ôl/.

## B. Consonant-to-consonant (C-C) linking ‚ÄĒ merger + assimilation

When two consonants meet at a word boundary, English simplifies rather than
double-articulating: **identical** consonants **merge** into one long consonant
(gemination), and **similar** consonants **assimilate** toward each other ‚ÄĒ the
big one being yod-coalescence (/t/+/j/‚Üí/t É/, /d/+/j/‚Üí/d í/).

## C. Linking /r/ and intrusive /w/ /j/ /r/ ‚ÄĒ smoothing a vowel-to-vowel gap

Two vowels back-to-back cause a **hiatus** (an awkward gap). English smooths it
three ways:
- **Linking /r/** ‚ÄĒ a word *spelled with a final 'r'* (far, for, more, four)
  pronounces that /r/ only when the next word begins with a vowel.
- **Intrusive /r/** ‚ÄĒ a word with *no 'r' in the spelling* but ending in /…ô …Ďňź …Ēňź
  …™…ô/ (idea, law, saw) inserts an /r/ before a vowel anyway.
- **Intrusive /w/ /j/** ‚ÄĒ after a closing diphthong, /w/ (after /…ô ä  ä/ ‚ÄĒ *go on*)
  or /j/ (after /iňź e…™ a…™ …Ē…™/ ‚ÄĒ *I am*) glides into the next vowel.
