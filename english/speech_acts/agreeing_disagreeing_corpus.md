## B. Full / maximal agreement ‚ÄĒ the multi-word confirmers

These carry *more* weight than a bare "yes" ‚ÄĒ they signal genuine, considered
agreement. **"I couldn't agree more" is the pinned real example** (OED-attested
since 1939): the negative-polarity idiom that means the *opposite* of its syntax.

## C. Soft / partial disagreement ‚ÄĒ the hedged pivots

The expert layer. English does **not** disagree by saying "You're wrong." It
disagrees by **agreeing first** (a partial concession), then pivoting on a
**but**. Learners who skip the concession and jump to the disagreement sound
abrupt or hostile. The hedge **actually** is the single most important softener.

## D. The polite direct disagreement ‚ÄĒ softened with "really" / "actually"

When the disagreement is stronger, English still does not say "You're wrong." It
adds **really** (downtoner) or **actually** (hedge) to mark it as a *personal
take*, not an attack.
