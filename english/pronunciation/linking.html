6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Inserts a glottal stop [ʔ] between words → every word starts crisply, speech sounds chopped/roboticDrop the glottal stop. Let the final consonant glide into the next vowel: an‿apple /əˈnæpl/, not /ən/ [ʔ] /ˈæpəl/.
Treats each syllable as self-contained (syllable-timed L1) → word boundaries are hard wallsResyllabify: the final consonant belongs to the next word. Say stop‿it as if spelled sto-pit.
Linking feels like "slurring" → deliberately separates words to "speak clearly"Reframe: linking is clear English. Unlinked speech sounds broken to a native ear. Link to sound fluent.
Holds each word's final consonant separately → good day as /ɡʊd … deɪ/ (double tap)Merge identical consonants into one long consonant: good day /ɡʊˈdeɪ/, black cat /ˌblækˈkæt/.
Never coalesces /t/+/j/ → says got you as /ɡɒt ju/ (stilted)Let /t/+/j/ fuse to /tʃ/: gotcha /ˈɡɒtʃə/, would you /ˈwʊdʒu/. Standard, not slang.
Leaves a gap between two vowels → I am /aɪ … æm/, go on /ɡəʊ … ɒn/Smooth with intrusive glides: I am /ˈaɪ.jæm/ (/j/), go on /ˈɡəʊ.wɒn/ (/w/).
Doesn't use linking/intrusive /r/ → idea of /aɪˈdɪə … ɒv/ (two gaps)In non-rhotic UK style, link with /r/: idea(r) of /aɪˈdɪə.rəv/.
Cannot parse fast linked speech → natives sound like they swallow wordsReverse-engineer: hear /wəˈdʒə/ as what do you. Shadow native clips to map sound→spelling. 🔗 REDUCTIONS
