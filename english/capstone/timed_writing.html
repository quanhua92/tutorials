The time-box — one rule, three stages

A timed essay is not free writing. Split the clock ~20% plan / ~60% draft / ~20% polish — and keep the stages separate.

1 · plan ~20%

Outline + thesis first

Intro / 2–3 points / conclusion. Write the thesis before sentences.

"First, I'll outline…"

2 · draft ~60%

Get ideas down — DON'T edit

Forward motion only. Signpost as you go.

"For example… / However… / On the other hand…"

3 · polish ~20%

Check thesis + topic sentences + conclusion

Fix small errors; improve, don't rewrite.

"In conclusion…"

5Writing task — timed mini-essay primary practice

Write a timed mini-essay through all 3 stages. Set a timer, then reveal the model answer (which shows the plan → draft → polished stages).

Prompt

"Remote work is better for most teams." Do you agree or disagree? Write a short timed essay.

Suggested split for a ~12-min mini-essay: ~2 min plan · ~8 min draft · ~2 min polish.

Model — all 3 stages shown:

① PLAN (outline + thesis)

Outline: intro / Point 1 (productivity) + example / Point 2 (balance) + counter / conclusion.
Thesis: Remote work improves most teams' output.

② DRAFT (signpost, don't edit)

First, my main argument is that remote work improves productivity. For example, studies show output rises when people work from home. On the other hand, some people lose focus without an office. However, the net effect is still positive for most teams.

③ POLISH (check thesis, topic sentences, conclusion)

In conclusion, the benefits of remote work outweigh the drawbacks.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Writes with no plan — starts "to save time," then rambles and runs out of clock before the conclusionPlan ~20% first. Outline (intro / 2–3 points / conclusion) + write the thesis before any sentences. "Planning is an investment."
Edits sentence-by-sentence WHILE drafting — writes, crosses out, polishes an adjective; 30 min later = one paragraph, no essayDon't edit while drafting. Draft stage = forward motion only. Keep a rough draft on time; leave all correction to polish.
Neglects the outline AND the conclusion — sacrifices both ends to the bodyProtect the outline + conclusion above all. "An essay without a conclusion cannot score above Band 5." If time runs out, stop the body and write the conclusion first.
No signposts — a wall of unconnected sentences; the reader can't follow the turnSignpost while writing: First… / For example… / However… / On the other hand… / In conclusion…. The signposts are the coherence.
No thesis — drifts into "I will discuss both sides" with no positionWrite the one-sentence thesis first (My main argument is…) and make every topic sentence point back to it.
Over-polishes the intro, under-writes the body — 10 min on a "hook" fillerSkip the hook. Paraphrase the prompt + state the thesis in 2–3 sentences, then move on. The intro is ~5% of the marks.
Adds new ideas in the conclusion — "In conclusion, and also I predict…"The conclusion restates the thesis and summarises the reasons — no new ideas.
Missing articles / plural -s / tense in the rushLeave these for the polish stage: know your hotspots (articles, -s, tense) and hunt only those in the last minutes.
Pro-drop / missing subject under pressure — "Is good idea to…"Supply the subject + copula even when rushed: "It is…" / "My main argument is…".
Can't pronounce the signposts aloud — "In conclusio," "On the othe hand"The signposts are spoken too. Release every final: conclusion /kənˈkluː.ʒn/, other /ˈʌðə(r)/, however /haʊˈevə(r)/.
