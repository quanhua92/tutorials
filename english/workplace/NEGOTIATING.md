## Why this is bundle #38 (read this first)

A negotiation in English is **not** two people stating positions and waiting.
It is a sequence of **conditional trades**: *"if you give X, we can give Y."*
The whole exchange runs on three small sets of chunks:

1. **Conditional propose** ‚ÄĒ name your offer *and* the condition it depends on.
2. **Compromise** ‚ÄĒ propose the midpoint when neither side will move fully.
3. **Accept / decline** ‚ÄĒ close the trade, or refuse it *without* breaking the
   relationship.

A Vietnamese learner who can produce these three sets fluently can negotiate a
price, a deadline, or a scope change in English. A learner who can't is left
either **conceding too much** (the L1 harmony reflex ‚ÄĒ *giŠĽĮ thŠĽÉ diŠĽán*) or
**holding rigid** (the other L1 reflex ‚ÄĒ refusing to move to save face). This
bundle is the cure for both.

## 1. The mechanism: the conditional trade

English negotiation is built on a **concession-for-concession** structure. You
never give something for nothing ‚ÄĒ you give it *on condition*. The conditional
is the grammatical engine:

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    OFFER["We'd be willing to<br/>do X"] -->|"made contingent on"| COND["provided that /<br/>on condition that<br/>you do Y"]
    COND -->|"if accepted"| DEAL["That works for us.<br/>That sounds reasonable."]
    COND -->|"if refused"| DECLINE["I'm afraid we can't<br/>go that far.<br/>That's a deal-breaker."]
    DECLINE -.->|"salvage"| MID["How about we meet<br/>halfway? Can we split<br/>the difference?"]
    MID --> DEAL
    style OFFER fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style COND fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
    style DEAL fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
    style DECLINE fill:#fdecea,stroke:#c0392b,stroke-width:2px
    style MID fill:#eaf2f8,stroke:#2980b9,stroke-width:2px
```

> From `negotiating_corpus.md`:
>
> - **If you can do X, we could agree to Y.** /…™f ju k√¶n duňź X, wi k äd …ôňą…°riňź
>   tuňź Y/ ‚ÄĒ the canonical concession-for-concession trade template.
> - **We'd be willing to‚Ä¶, provided that‚Ä¶** /wiňźd bi ňąw…™l…™Ňč tuňź, pr…ôňąva…™d…™d
>   √į√¶t/ ‚ÄĒ the formal conditional offer.
> - **On condition that‚Ä¶** /…ín k…ônňąd…™ Én √į√¶t/ ‚ÄĒ the strict, formal version
>   (legal/contractual tone).

**The pragmatic engine:** `provided (that)` and `on condition that` are
Cambridge-documented conditional conjunctions meaning *"if, or only if."* The
soft modal `could` /k äd/ (not *will*) keeps the offer **tentative** ‚ÄĒ it signals
"this is negotiable," which is exactly the face-saving move that lets the other
side counter without losing face. `willing` (CALD: *"ready or eager to do
something"*) names your offer without committing you to it unconditionally.

## 2. Compromise ‚ÄĒ when neither side will move fully

When two positions are far apart, English has a **shared idiom set** for
proposing the midpoint. Cambridge's thesaurus lists all three as synonyms of
*compromise*:

> From `negotiating_corpus.md`:
>
> - **How about we meet halfway?** /ňĆha ä …ôňąba ät wi miňźt ňĆh…Ďňźfňąwe…™/ UK ¬∑
>   /ňĆha ä …ôňąba ät wi miňźt ňĆh√¶fňąwe…™/ US ‚ÄĒ CALD idiom: *"to do some of the things
>   that someone wants you to do, in order to show that you want to reach an
>   agreement."*
> - **Can we split the difference?** /k√¶n wi spl…™t √į…ô ňąd…™fr…ôns/ ‚ÄĒ CALD idiom:
>   *"to accept only part of what you originally wanted when making an
>   agreement involving money"* (the price/deadline midpoint).
> - **Let's find some middle ground.** /lets fa…™nd s Ćm ňąm…™dl …°ra änd/ ‚ÄĒ CALD:
>   *"a position between two opposite opinions."*

These three are **interchangeable in function** but not in register:
`split the difference` is most concrete (money/numbers), `meet halfway` is the
all-purpose workhorse, `middle ground` is slightly more abstract (principles,
scope). Use all three ‚ÄĒ repetition with variation is how native negotiators
keep the conversation moving without sounding repetitive.

## 3. Accept / decline ‚ÄĒ closing without breaking the relationship

The hardest move for a Vietnamese speaker. Direct refusal feels rude in the L1;
English solves it with two down-toners and one walk-away phrase:

| Move | Chunk | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Accept (warm) | **That works for us.** | `work` = "be acceptable" (CALD) ‚ÄĒ short, decisive, positive. |
| Accept (judgement) | **That sounds reasonable.** | `reasonable` = "fair and practical" ‚ÄĒ validates the other side's offer. |
| Decline (polite) | **I'm afraid we can't go that far.** | `I'm afraid (that)` = "I'm sorry to say" (CALD polite formula) + `go that far` = "reach that limit." Refusal **without** aggression. |
| Walk away | **That's a deal-breaker.** | Names the non-negotiable term clearly ‚ÄĒ the formal way to signal "we cannot agree to this." |

**The pragmatic key:** `I'm afraid` is not "fear" here ‚ÄĒ it is the **polite
refusal formula** (CALD: *"used to politely tell someone something that may
annoy, upset, or disappoint them"*). Vietnamese has no exact equivalent; the
nearest is *"xin lŠĽói nh∆įng‚Ä¶"* but the English chunk is a fixed, expected
formula. Using it marks you as a fluent, professional negotiator.

## 4. The negotiation turn map

A full negotiation cycles through propose ‚Üí compromise ‚Üí accept/decline, often
more than once. The mermaid below maps a single cycle ‚ÄĒ a real deal may loop 2‚Äď3
times before landing:

```mermaid
sequenceDiagram
    participant A as Person A (seller)
    participant B as Person B (buyer)
    A->>B: We'd be willing to‚Ä¶, provided that‚Ä¶
    B->>A: I'm afraid we can't go that far.
    A->>B: How about we meet halfway?
    B->>A: Can we split the difference?
    A->>B: That sounds reasonable.
    B->>A: That works for us.
```

ūüĒó Contrast with [DIPLOMATIC DISAGREEMENT](./DIPLOMATIC_DISAGREEMENT.md) ‚ÄĒ there
the move was acknowledge ‚Üí counter; here the move is **offer ‚Üí counter-offer**,
and every offer is conditional. The grammar is different (conditional + modal)
but the face-saving instinct is the same.

## 6. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits in an English negotiation ‚ÄĒ the two failure modes are opposite
(concede too much vs. hold rigid), and the cure for both is the **conditional
trade**.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Concedes too much to preserve harmony** (*giŠĽĮ thŠĽÉ diŠĽán* / collectivist conflict-avoidance) ‚ÄĒ accepts the first offer to avoid conflict | Never accept unconditionally. Always attach a condition: **"We'd be willing to‚Ä¶, provided that‚Ä¶"** ‚ÄĒ a concession earns a concession. |
| **Holds rigid to save face** ‚ÄĒ refuses to move a number/term because moving "loses face" | Reframe moving as a **trade**, not a loss: **"If you can do X, we could agree to Y."** You aren't giving in ‚ÄĒ you're trading. |
| **Misses the concession-for-concession logic** ‚ÄĒ Vietnamese negotiation is often positional ("this is my price"), not conditional ("if‚Ä¶then") | Drill the conditional template until it's automatic. Every offer should have an *if* attached. No free concessions. |
| **States a position as a declarative** ‚ÄĒ "We want $10,000" (sounds blunt/demanding) | Soften with a modal + condition: **"We could accept $10,000, if you can‚Ä¶"** The `could` keeps it negotiable. |
| **Cannot say "no" politely** ‚ÄĒ direct refusal feels rude, so the learner either says "yes" (and resent) or stays silent | Use the polite-refusal formula: **"I'm afraid we can't go that far."** It refuses without aggression ‚ÄĒ the L1 has no exact equivalent, so drill it as a fixed chunk. |
| **Silence is misread** ‚ÄĒ a Vietnamese pause (thinking / harmony-keeping) reads to a Western negotiator as rejection or stalling | Fill the gap with a fluency move: **"Let me think about that"** / **"That's an interesting point"** ‚ÄĒ then the counter. Silence in English negotiation is a weapon, not a courtesy. |
| **Under-uses the compromise idioms** ‚ÄĒ translates literally ("chia ńĎ√īi" ‚Üí "divide in two") instead of using the shared idiom | Use the set: **"meet halfway" / "split the difference" / "middle ground."** Cambridge lists all three as *compromise* synonyms ‚ÄĒ these are the expected, native-sounding moves. |
| **Overuses "deal-breaker" as a threat** ‚ÄĒ uses it early to sound tough, which kills the negotiation | Reserve **"That's a deal-breaker"** for the genuine walk-away term (the one thing you cannot accept). Use it once; mean it. |
| **Drops the `that` in "provided that / on condition that"** under stress ‚Üí "provided you can‚Ä¶" (acceptable, but loses the formal register) | In formal/contractual tone, keep the **`that`**: "provided **that** you can deliver by Friday." |
| **Confuses `reasonable` with `cheap`/`low`** ‚Üí "That's reasonable" said about a low price sounds like approval of cheapness | `reasonable` = "fair and practical" (CALD) ‚ÄĒ it's a judgement of fairness, not size. **"That sounds reasonable"** = "I judge this fair." |
| **Stresses the wrong syllable in `negotiable`** ‚Üí /ňĆniňź…°…ô ä Éiňąe…™bl/ (wrong) | Stress the **second** syllable: /n…™ňą…°…ô ä Éi…ôbl/ UK ¬∑ /n…™ňą…°o ä Éi…ôbl/ US. Same for `negotiate` /n…™ňą…°…ô ä Éie…™t/. |
| **/őł/ missing in the conditional** ‚Üí drops the /őł/ in `both` / `worth`, or the final cluster in `halfway` ‚Üí "halfway" sounds like "haf-way" | Keep the final cluster tight: **halfway** /ňĆh…Ďňźfňąwe…™/. ūüĒó See [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md). |
