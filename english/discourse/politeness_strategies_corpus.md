## A. Negative-politeness strategies (face-saving indirectness)

Negative politeness, in Brown & Levinson's (1987) framework, is **redressive
action addressed to the addressee's *negative face*** — their "want to have his
freedom of action unhindered and his attention unimpeded" (Brown & Levinson 1987,
p. 62, via MPG.PuRe). The speaker *gives the hearer an out*: the request is
hedged, indirect, and easy to refuse without embarrassment. This is the strategy
Vietnamese L1 learners most under-use — they tend to be too direct.

## B. Positive-politeness strategies (solidarity, inclusion, "we")

Positive politeness is **redressive action addressed to the addressee's *positive
face*** — their "want... that his wants be desirable to at least some others"
(Brown & Levinson 1987, p. 62). The speaker shrinks the distance: uses inclusive
*we*, treats the hearer as an ally, frames the request as a shared project.
Vietnamese has a strong equivalent via kinship pronouns (*anh/em*) — but English
does it **syntactically** (Let's / We could), not lexically.

## C. Bald-on-record & off-record (the two poles)

Bald-on-record is the **maximally efficient, maximally clear** strategy — direct
imperative, no redressive action. Brown & Levinson predict it when **face-risk is
low** (intimates, emergencies, routine tasks, or task-oriented work where clarity
beats softness). Off-record is the opposite pole: a **hint** so indirect the
hearer can pretend it was never a request at all. Vietnamese learners default to
bald-on-record where English expects negative politeness — the core L1 error.
