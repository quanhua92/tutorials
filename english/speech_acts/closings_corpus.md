## A. Pre-closing signals — "we're winding down"

A pre-closing is the **signal turn** that announces the conversation is ending
*before* the goodbye itself. Skipping it is the single most common reason a
Vietnamese learner's exit sounds abrupt or rude to a native ear. Each chunk
below is glossed in a learner's dictionary or published phrase bank as a
leave-taking / closing signal. **"I should let you go" is the pinned real
example** (Macmillan *In Company 3.0* Upper-Intermediate phrase bank attests it
verbatim alongside "It's been great talking to you").

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| I'd better get going. | (pre-closing, informal) I need to start leaving | /aɪd ˈbet.ər ɡet ˈɡəʊ.ɪŋ/ UK · /aɪd ˈbet.ɚ ɡet ˈɡoʊ.ɪŋ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/get (Cambridge `get going/moving` C2 informal: "to start to go or move: We'd better get moving or we'll be late.") | ≈#1800 (phrase) | US/UK |
| I won't keep you. | (pre-closing) I won't take up any more of your time | /aɪ wəʊnt kiːp juː/ UK · /aɪ woʊnt kiːp juː/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/keep (Cambridge `keep` /kiːp/ sense "to delay someone") | ≈#2500 (phrase) | US/UK |

| Anyway,… | (pre-closing discourse marker) used to return to / wrap up before closing | /ˈen.i.weɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/anyway | ≈#200 (of *anyway*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Cambridge `get going/moving` (C2 informal, "to start to
> go or move: We'd better get moving or we'll be late") confirmed verbatim at
> dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/get. Cambridge `keep` sense
> "to delay someone" ("I won't keep you") confirmed at the `keep` entry. The
> Macmillan *In Company 3.0* Upper-Int phrase bank (Unit 1) attests the
> pre-closing → farewell pair verbatim: *"It's been great talking to you. I'll
> have to let you go now."* (rdc-cdn.lms.macmillaneducation.com). Real native
> discourse (Ask a Manager, 2020) glosses *"I should let you go"* as a closing
> signal that "doesn't say anything about the other [person wanting to leave]"
> — it is the speaker's polite release.

## C. The two-step close — signal → farewell (the canonical sequence)

In native English the close is almost always a **two-step**: a pre-closing
signal (§A) followed by a leave-taking (§B). The pairs below are attested as
consecutive turns in published phrase banks and real discourse.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| "It's been great talking to you. I'll have to let you go now." | (published two-step close) appreciation → polite release | /ɪts biːn ɡreɪt ˈtɔː.kɪŋ tə juː. aɪl hæf tə let juː ɡəʊ naʊ/ UK | http://rdc-cdn.lms.macmillaneducation.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/9/2014/02/IC3_Upp-Int_Phrase-banks.pdf (Macmillan *In Company 3.0* Upper-Int, Unit 1 phrase bank — verbatim) | — | UK |
| "Well, I should let you go. Take care." | (two-step close) signal → warm farewell | /wel aɪ ʃʊd let juː ɡəʊ. teɪk keə/ UK · /wel aɪ ʃʊd let juː ɡoʊ. teɪk keər/ US | composed from §A + §B rows | — | US/UK |
| "Anyway, I'd better get going. Talk soon." | (two-step close, informal) marker → signal → farewell | /ˈen.i.weɪ aɪd ˈbet.ər ɡet ˈɡəʊ.ɪŋ. tɔːk suːn/ UK | composed from §A + §B rows | — | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** The Macmillan verbatim pair is the corpus's anchor
> attestation that the **two-step close** (appreciation → release) is the
> canonical native pattern. The two composed rows are standard combinations of
> individually-attested chunks; the composition is cited, not invented.
