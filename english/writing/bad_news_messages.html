The 4-part spine (indirect / buffer approach — Cruthers, Business Writing For Everyone)

1. Buffer
neutral/positive cushion — no false hope
2. Reason
one brief, blame-free sentence
3. Bad news
clear but de-emphasised — never buried
4. Close
redirect / alternative + forward-looking

5Writing task primary mode

Write a 4-part bad-news message — buffer + reason + bad news + constructive close. Then reveal the model answer.

Model — the 4-part spine, each move labelled:

[buffer] Dear Linh, After careful consideration, we want to thank you for taking the time to apply for the Junior Designer role.

[reason] Unfortunately, due to the high number of applicants this round, we could only move a small shortlist forward.

[bad news] I regret to inform you that we are unable to offer you the position at this time.

[close] Your portfolio showed real promise, and we would welcome a future application from you. We wish you the very best in your search.

Sincerely, ____

Every chunk above is a corpus row: After careful consideration / We appreciate your (Cambridge consideration, appreciate) · Unfortunately / due to (Cambridge unfortunately, due to) · I regret to inform you / We're unable to (Cambridge regret, unable) · We wish you the best / welcome a future application (Cruthers indirect-approach close).

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Too blunt — "You're rejected." / "We can't accept." — discomfort makes you want it over fastNever the bare you + rejection verb. Frame with I regret to inform you / We're unable to.
Too vague / news buried — so much hedge the recipient can't tell if they got the jobState the news once, in one clear clause, mid-message. Softening must never cost clarity.
Over-long cushion / indirectness — a face-saving paragraph of storyKeep the reason to one sentence. The kindness in English is the clarity, not the length.
Passive-voice overuse to avoid agency — "a decision was made" for the whole messageUse the passive at most once to de-emphasise; the actor (we) must appear — ideally in the close.
Guilt/discomfort → avoidance — never sending it, or sending it so late the news is staleSend it promptly. A timely, well-structured "no" is more respectful than a late, apologetic one.
Opens with false hope — "I'm pleased to tell you…" then pivots to rejectionThe buffer cushions; it does not celebrate. Open with After careful consideration / Thank you for your application.
Drops the to — "I regret inform you" / "unable accept"Drill the fixed chunk intact: regret to inform, unable to accept, afraid that …
Mixes regret (verb) and regret (noun) — "I have a regret to inform you"Verb for the action: I regret to inform you. Noun phrase for the close: Please accept our regrets.
Closes on the bad news — ends with the rejection, no redirectEnd forward-looking: We wish you the best / We'd welcome a future application. Never re-state the bad news.
