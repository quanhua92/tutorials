5Writing task — calibrate the hedging

Rewrite each sentence three ways: under-hedged (claim too strong) → just-right (matches evidence) → over-hedged (claim drowns). Then reveal the model.

Claim 1: "The new layout increased sign-ups." (evidence: one A/B test, +12%)

Model — calibrate each line to its evidence:

Under-hedged: This proves the new layout increased sign-ups. — claim outruns one A/B test; reads as unfounded.

Just-right: The data suggests the new layout may have increased sign-ups. — suggests + may have = evidence-matched.

Over-hedged: It might perhaps seem to possibly suggest it could maybe have… — four hedges stacked; the claim vanishes.

Claim 2 (model): "These results clearly show the fix works." → evidence = replicated across 3 cohorts → just-right is the strengthener, not a hedge. Calibration works both ways.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
States opinion as bald fact — "This is wrong" / "This proves…" (VN context carries the politeness)Calibrate: if evidence only suggests it, write "This appears to be incorrect" / "The data suggests…". Match verb to evidence.
Over-hedges everything to be "polite" — "I think maybe it might possibly seem…"One hedge is enough. Pick one device (a modal or It seems or arguably) and stop. Stacking four signals you're unsure of anything.
Uses spoken hedges "kind of" / "like" / "sort of" in a formal reportWritten hedging uses lexical devices: It appears that / The data suggests / may / tends to. Reserve "kind of" for spoken casual.
Uses "I think" as the only hedge in every sentenceVary the attribution: It seems that… (impersonal), The data suggests… (evidence), arguably (defensible). Repeating "I think" sounds unprofessional.
Under-claims strong evidence — reports solid numbers with "I think it might be…"When data is clear, strengthen: "The results clearly show…" A hedge on strong evidence under-sells your work.
Translates VN modals directly — "can be" / "will be" where English needs "may be"Epistemic may/might/could ≠ ability can. For uncertainty use may/might: "This may be a problem," not "can be."
Confuses "prove" and "suggest" — writes "This proves…" for correlational evidenceProve = deductive certainty (rare). Suggest / indicate / appear to = the standard for empirical evidence. Default to suggest.
Mis-stresses "arguably" — "ar-GU-ab-ly" (wrong)AR-gyu-ab-ly /ˈɑːɡjuəbli/ — primary stress on the first syllable. Drill it aloud; wrong stress marks you instantly non-native.
