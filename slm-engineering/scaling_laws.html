📖 Read the full guide — the Kaplan → Chinchilla → overtraining lineage, decision tables, killer gotchas, cheat sheet. This page is the interactive companion.
For a fixed FLOP budget C, how do you split it between model size N and data D? Kaplan (2020) said "build big, starve data" (~1.7 tok/param); Chinchilla (2022) said "scale them together" (~20 tok/param); deployed SLMs overtrain 100-1000× past that because inference, not pretraining, dominates a served model's lifetime bill. Every number below is recomputed in JS from the identical formula in scaling_laws.py.  |  compare with vocab rationalization →

① The compute-optimal frontier: drag the FLOP budget C → watch N_opt and D_opt grow together (always 20:1)

How to read: Chinchilla says N_opt = √(C/120) params and D_opt = 20·N_opt tokens (because C ≈ 6·N·D and D=20N ⇒ C = 120·N²). Both bars grow as √C, so 4× the budget only doubles the model — and the D/N ratio is a flat 20.0 everywhere. That flatness is Chinchilla's whole point.
120 = 6 × 20 (the compute constant × the optimal ratio)
N_opt (parameters) — the model size D_opt (tokens) — always 20× N_opt Kaplan-style N at ~1.7 tok/param (big & undertrained)

② The overtraining trade: drag the overtrain factor k → find the break-even #queries

How to read: fix a small model N = 135M (SmolLM2-135M) and train it on D = k·20·N tokens — past the Chinchilla-optimal point. The overtrained model costs MORE to pretrain once, but matches a BIGGER Chinchilla model N′ on loss, so it is cheaper to serve (2N vs 2N′ FLOPs/token). The two lifetime-FLOP curves cross at the break-even #queries — beyond it, overtraining wins. For a deployed SLM queried billions of times, that crossing comes fast.
