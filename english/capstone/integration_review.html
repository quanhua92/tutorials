5Writing task

Write your 180-day self-assessment: what improved / what's still weak / your maintenance plan for the next month. Then reveal the model.

Model (look back → win → gap → goal → maintenance):

Look back — Looking back on the last six months, I've come a long way from Day 1. /ˈlʊkɪŋ ˈbæk/ — open the reflection (Collins COBUILD)

Name a win — One thing I've gotten better at is releasing my final consonants — "went" no longer sounds like "wen." /ˌwʌn ˈθɪŋ aɪv ˈɡɑːtn ˈbetər ət/ — present perfect win (Cambridge better)

Name a gap — Something I still need to work on is my past tenses when I speak under pressure. An area I want to focus on is my /θ/ sounds. /ˈsʌmθɪŋ aɪ stɪl ˈniːd tə ˈwɜːrk ˈɑːn/ — Oxford work on

Set a goal — My goal for the next month is to re-drill the pronunciation bundles and get every flip card to "knew it." /maɪ ɡoʊl fər ðə ˈnekst mʌnθ ɪz/ — Oxford goal

Plan maintenance — I'll keep practicing ten minutes a day so I don't lose it. Next I want to tackle the phrasal verbs I keep forgetting. There's still room for improvement — but I'm on track. /aɪl ˈkiːp ˈpræktɪsɪŋ/ + /ˈnekst aɪ ˈwɑːnt tə ˈtækl/ — Cambridge keep + tackle

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Skips self-assessment entirely (face: doesn't want to confront weaknesses) → weak chunks decay unseenRun the five-move arc: "Looking back… → One thing I've gotten better at… → Something I still need to work on… → My goal… → I'll keep practicing…". Use the dashboard self-ratings so data, not ego, names the gaps.
Over-harsh self-assessment — "My English is terrible" → loses confidence, quitsEnglish self-assessment is balanced and specific: always pair a win with a gap. One named weakness is a target; a wall of criticism is paralysis.
Vague self-assessment — "I need to improve my English" (no specific area, no specific drill)Name a chunk-level area: "An area I want to focus on is my final consonants." Vague goals → vague practice.
No present perfect → "I get better" instead of "I've gotten better at…"Vietnamese has no perfect tenses. The win needs the present perfect ("I've gotten / I've noticed") — it signals "a change that happened and still holds now."
No maintenance plan — finishes 180 days and stops → fluency decays (forgetting curve)Always close with a forward plan: "My goal for the next month is… I'll keep practicing…". Spaced re-exposure moves chunks to permastore.
"Practice" vs "practise" confusion (UK verb spelling)US: both noun and verb are "practice". UK: verb is "practise", noun is "practice." Pick one variety and stay consistent.
Pro-drop → "Want to focus on…" instead of "An area I want to focus on…"Supply the subject. English reflection demands "I've… / I want… / I'll…" — the subject signals ownership of the assessment.
Drops the final consonant in improvement /ɪmˈpruːvmənt/ → "improvemen", month /mʌnθ/ → "mon"Release every final: the /t/ on improvement, the /θ/ on month, the /l/ on goal. A dropped final undercuts the intelligibility you are reviewing.
Cannot name a weakness in front of a coach (face/deference) → "everything is okay"The English convention is honest, specific, forward-looking: "Something I still need to work on is my past tenses under pressure." That shows self-awareness, not failure.
Translates the reflection word-by-word from Vietnamese — slow, halting, loses the threadRetrieve chunks: "Looking back, one thing I've gotten better at…" is one block. Drill the 8 chunks until they fire as units.
