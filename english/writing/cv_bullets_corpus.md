## A. The action-verb engine (what every bullet starts with)

A CV bullet is led by a **past-tense action verb** (for finished jobs) or a
**present-tense action verb** (for a current role). The verb is the engine; the
metric is the proof. These eleven verbs are the high-frequency set every career
guide (MIT, Yale SOM, Harvard) tells you to open with. Every past-tense form
follows the `-ed` allomorph rule documented in
[`pronunciation/final_consonants_corpus.md`](../pronunciation/final_consonants_corpus.md) §C.

## B. The result / quantifier connectors (task → impact)

The verb opens the bullet; a **participial phrase** (verb-+-ing) or a *that*-
clause links the task to its **quantified result**. This connector is what turns
a duty list ("responsible for marketing") into an achievement ("Led 5 campaigns,
**increasing engagement by 30%**"). The pattern is cited across MIT, Yale SOM,
and Harvard career guides.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| resulting in | causing / producing (an outcome) | /rɪˈzʌltɪŋ ɪn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/result | ≈#200 (of *result*) | US/UK |
| increasing … by | making … larger by an amount | /ɪnˈkriːsɪŋ baɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/increase | ≈#300 (of *increase*) | US/UK |
| reducing … by | making … smaller by an amount | /rɪˈdjuːsɪŋ baɪ/ UK · /rɪˈduːsɪŋ baɪ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/reduce | ≈#300 (of *reduce*) | US/UK |
| saving | keeping from being spent/wasted | /ˈseɪvɪŋ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/save | ≈#250 (of *save*) | US/UK |
| serving | providing for / used by (people) | /ˈsɜːvɪŋ/ UK · /ˈsɜːrvɪŋ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/serve | ≈#300 (of *serve*) | US/UK |
| cutting … by | reducing (time/cost) by an amount | /ˈkʌtɪŋ baɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/cut | ≈#120 (of *cut*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `result` /rɪˈzʌlt/ + `-ing` = /rɪˈzʌltɪŋ/ confirmed in
> Cambridge ("result in something" = to cause a situation); the participial
> connectors `increasing` /ɪnˈkriːsɪŋ/, `reducing` /rɪˈdjuːsɪŋ/–/rɪˈduːsɪŋ/,
> `saving` /ˈseɪvɪŋ/, `serving` /ˈsɜːvɪŋ/–/ˈsɜːrvɪŋ/, `cutting` /ˈkʌtɪŋ/ are
> the standard Cambridge `-ing` forms; all link a task to a quantified impact in
> the MIT/Yale model bullets below.

## D. The tense rule (past for finished jobs, present for current)

A CV is a tense-disciplined document: every bullet under a **past** job takes
the past tense; every bullet under a **current** job takes the present. Mixing
them (past tense under your current role, or present under a former one) is a
top résumé error flagged by MIT and Yale SOM.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Led a team of 5 engineers (past job) | past-tense model: finished role | /led ə tiːm əv faɪv/ | https://capd.mit.edu/resources/resumes-writing-about-your-skills/ | — | US |
| Manage a team of 5 engineers (current job) | present-tense model: current role | /ˈmænɪdʒ ə tiːm əv faɪv/ | https://capd.mit.edu/resources/resumes-writing-about-your-skills/ | — | US |

> **Verification note:** the past-for-past / present-for-current rule is stated
> in the MIT Career Development Handbook ("tenses: Past for past work …") and
> the Yale SOM CDO Resume Writing Guide.
