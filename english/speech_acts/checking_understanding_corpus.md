## A. Checking the listener — full-form questions (the speaker checks)

The speaker's side of the speech act: pause your explanation and **explicitly
ask the listener whether they followed**. This is the move Vietnamese learners
under-use, because in Vietnamese a listener's *vâng / dạ* already signals
attention — the speaker trusts it. English speakers **do not trust silence or a
nod**, so the speaker must check. These are the four highest-frequency
full-form checks.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Does that make sense? | speaker asks: was that clear? | /ˌdʌz ðæt ˌmeɪk ˈsens/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make-sense | ≈#600 (of *sense*) | US/UK |
| Are you with me? | speaker asks: are you following? | /ɑː juː ˈwɪð miː/ UK · /ɑːr juː ˈwɪθ miː/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/be-with | ≈#30 (of *with*) | US/UK |
| Does that sound OK? | speaker asks: is that acceptable/clear? | /dʌz ðæt ˌsaʊnd ˌəʊˈkeɪ/ UK · /dʌz ðæt ˌsaʊnd oʊˈkeɪ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sound | ≈#250 (of *sound*) | US/UK |
| Are we on the same page? | speaker asks: do we share the same understanding? | /ɑː wiː ɒn ðə ˌseɪm ˈpeɪdʒ/ UK · /ɑːr wiː ɑːn ðə ˌseɪm ˈpeɪdʒ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/page | ≈#300 (of *page*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *make sense* is listed as an idiom in the Cambridge
> entry ("to be clear and easy to understand") and corroborated in Collins
> ("If something makes sense, you can understand it. 'Yes, the figures make
> sense.'"). *be with someone* is the Cambridge entry that attests *Are you
> with me?* verbatim — "to understand what someone is saying: You look puzzled —
> are you with me? I'm sorry, I'm not with you." *on the same page* is the
> Cambridge idiom under the *page* entry — "to have the same ideas as someone
> else: Everyone in the office has to be on the same page about what our top
> priorities are." *sound* (= seem) is the Cambridge *sound* entry, sense 2.
> UK/US vowel splits (*with* /wɪð/ vs /wɪθ/, *on* /ɒn/ vs /ɑːn/, *OK* /əʊˈkeɪ/
> vs /oʊˈkeɪ/) are documented in the Cambridge per-word entries.

## B. Checking the listener — quick / elliptical (informal, fast)

The same speech act, **compressed**. Used in fast spoken explanations,
classrooms, standups, pair-programming — anywhere a full question would slow
the flow. Vietnamese learners tend to skip the check entirely in fast speech
(because the L1 listener-token covers it); these short forms keep the check
in without breaking rhythm.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Make sense? | elliptical: does that make sense? | /ˌmeɪk ˈsens/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make-sense | ≈#600 (of *sense*) | US/UK |
| Right? | tag: you follow? / correct? | /raɪt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/right | ≈#40 (of *right*) | US/UK |
| Does that sound right? | speaker asks: is that accurate? | /dʌz ðæt ˌsaʊnd ˈraɪt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sound | ≈#250 (of *sound*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *Make sense?* is the elliptical (subject + auxiliary
> dropped) form of *Does that make sense?* — a standard spoken reduction
> documented in Cambridge's usage notes on ellipsis. *Right?* as a checking tag
> is the Cambridge *right* entry (adverb, sense 3: "used to ask if what you
> have just said is correct"). *Does that sound right?* pairs the Cambridge
> *sound* (= seem) sense with *right* (= correct).

## C. Confirming by paraphrasing — the listener reflects back

The listener's side of the speech act: **repeat the speaker's idea in your own
words** to prove you understood (and to give them a chance to correct you if
you didn't). This is the move Vietnamese learners most often **skip** — a
Vietnamese listener signals comprehension with *vâng / dạ / gật đầu* (a nod),
which in English **reads as "I'm listening" but not "I understood."** English
expects a **verbal paraphrase** before the conversation moves on.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| So you're saying… | paraphrase opener: in other words, you mean… | /səʊ jʊə ˈseɪ.ɪŋ/ UK · /soʊ jʊr ˈseɪ.ɪŋ/ US | https://www.pearson.com/languages/en-nz/community/blogs/phrases-for-easier-english-conversations-3-25.html | — | US/UK |
| If I understand correctly,… | hedged paraphrase: if I've got this right,… | /ɪf aɪ ˌʌndəˈstænd kəˈrektli/ UK · /ɪf aɪ ˌʌndərˈstænd kəˈrektli/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/understand | ≈#300 (of *understand*) | US/UK |
| In other words,… | rephrase signal: let me put it differently | /ɪn ˈʌð.ər ˈwɜːdz/ UK · /ɪn ˈʌð.ɚ ˈwɜːrdz/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/word | ≈#200 (of *word*) | US/UK |
| Let me make sure I understand… | listener slows down to verify | /let mi ˌmeɪk ˈʃʊər aɪ ˌʌndəˈstænd/ UK · /let mi ˌmeɪk ˈʃʊr aɪ ˌʌndərˈstænd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sure | ≈#120 (of *sure*) | US/UK |
| So, to clarify,… | restate-to-verify (slightly formal) | /səʊ tə ˈklærəfaɪ/ UK · /soʊ tə ˈklerəfaɪ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/clarify | ≈#2500 (of *clarify*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *So you're saying…* is attested as the canonical
> paraphrase opener in Pearson PTE's "7 essential phrases for easier
> conversations in English" ("I see. So, you're saying…"), in LinkedIn
> Learning's *Effective Listening — Paraphrasing what was said* ("So you're
> saying that…"), and in BBC Learning English ("So, you're saying the meeting
> is tomorrow at 10 AM, right?"). *understand* /ˌʌndəˈstænd/ UK ·
> /ˌʌndərˈstænd/ US and *correctly* /kəˈrektli/ are the standard Cambridge
> transcriptions. *in other words* is the Cambridge idiom under the *word*
> entry. *make sure* is under the Cambridge *sure* entry ("make sure = to make
> certain"). *clarify* /ˈklærəfaɪ/ UK · /ˈklerəfaɪ/ US is the Cambridge
> transcription; the US /eɪ/→/e/ vowel in the stressed syllable is the standard
> US/UK split.

## D. Responding when you are checked — close the loop

The often-forgotten half: **when the speaker asks "Does that make sense?"**, the
listener must answer **out loud**. A Vietnamese listener's silent nod reads as
"I'm being polite" in Vietnamese but as "I didn't understand but won't admit
it" in English. Say one of these — green (got it) or amber (didn't fully).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Got it. | yes, I understood | /ˈɡɒt ɪt/ UK · /ˈɡɑːt ɪt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/got | ≈#80 (of *get*) | US/UK |
| Makes sense. | yes, that's clear / logical | /ˌmeɪks ˈsens/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make-sense | ≈#600 (of *sense*) | US/UK |
| I'm with you. | yes, I'm following | /aɪm ˈwɪð juː/ UK · /aɪm ˈwɪθ juː/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/be-with | ≈#30 (of *with*) | US/UK |
| Not quite. | polite: I didn't fully follow | /nɒt kwaɪt/ UK · /nɑːt kwaɪt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/quite | ≈#500 (of *quite*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *Got it.* is the Cambridge *get*/*got* past participle
> used as a spoken acknowledgement ("I've got it" → "Got it"). *Makes sense.* is
> the 3rd-person present of the *make sense* idiom, used as a standalone
> acknowledgement. *I'm with you.* is the Cambridge *be with someone* entry in
> the first person ("to understand what someone is saying"). *Not quite.* uses
> the Cambridge *quite* entry ("quite = completely" — so *not quite* = "not
> completely, but not a flat no," the polite softener).
