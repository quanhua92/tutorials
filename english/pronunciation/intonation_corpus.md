## A. The pinned minimal pair — same words, opposite meaning

The single most important demonstration of intonation: the **identical string**
"She's a teacher" is a **statement** (falling ↘) or a **question** (rising ↗)
purely by changing the nuclear tone. Wells (2006) calls this the **grammatical
function** of intonation; Wikipedia (*Intonation (linguistics)*, "Functions →
grammatical function") gives the parallel pair *"He's going ↗home?"* and notes
this statement→question flip "is more typical of American English than of
British."

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| She's a teacher.↘ | statement — assertive, final ("this is a fact") | /ʃiːz ə ˈtiːtʃə↘/ — nuclear **fall** on "teach-" | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/intonation | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |
| She's a teacher?↗ | yes/no question — "is that so? / really?" (surprise, or checking) | /ʃiːz ə ˈtiːtʃə↗/ — nuclear **rise** on "teach-" | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US (common) |

> **Verification note:** the statement↔question flip on identical word strings is
> documented in Cambridge Grammar Today ("Intonation" → rising/falling at the
> end of statements/questions) and in Wikipedia *Intonation (linguistics)* →
> "Functions" → grammatical function (the *"He's going ↗home?"* example). The
> ↘/↗ arrow-on-nucleus notation is Roach's (2009, pp. 119–160) and Wells's
> (2006) standard.

## B. The three nuclear tones + their pragmatic meanings

English intonation is conventionally analysed around the **nucleus** (the most
prominent syllable, usually in the last lexical word) and the pitch movement on
it — the **nuclear tone**. Roach (2009) and Wells (2006) recognise the fall,
the rise, and the fall-rise as the three high-frequency, contrastive tones
(rise-fall exists but is rarer / attitudinal). Their meanings are **pragmatic**,
not lexical.

### B1. Falling tone ↘ — finality, completeness, assertion

The fall is the default "this is settled" tone: statements, Wh-questions,
commands, and the last item of a list all land on a fall. A fall says *"I'm
telling you / I'm done."*

| Leave it on the table.↘ | command — firm, final, not open to negotiation | /ˈliːv ɪt ɒn ðə ˈteɪbl↘/ — nuclear **fall** on "ta-" | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |
| It's on the ↘table. | declarative statement — information given, complete | /ɪts ɒn ðə ˈteɪbl↘/ — nuclear **fall** on "ta-" | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/intonation | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |

### B2. Rising tone ↗ — yes/no question, listing, uncertainty, politeness

The rise leaves things **open**: it asks, lists (more to come), hedges, or
softens a request into politeness. Cambridge Grammar Today lists yes/no
questions and the rising list item as core uses of the rise.

| apples, ↗oranges, ↗bananas, and ↘grapes | **listing** — rise on every item except the last, which falls (the fall signals "that's everything") | /ˈæplz ↗ ˈɒrɪndʒɪz ↗ bəˈnɑːnəz ən ˈɡreɪps↘/ | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the listing contour (rise on each item, fall on the

> "Functions" → psychological function: *"You can have it in ↗red | ↗blue |
> ↗green | ↗yellow | or ↘black."* The rise-on-request politeness effect is
> described in Cambridge Grammar Today ("Intonation" → rising tone uses).

### B3. Fall-rise tone ↘↗ — implication, reservation, "but…"

The fall-rise says *"there's more to this than I'm saying"* — a warning, a
hedged disagreement, an implication, or an incomplete contrast (*"It's ↘good↗
… but expensive"*). Cambridge Grammar Today: "We use fall-rise intonation at the
end of statements when we want to say [that something is true but…]".

| I ↘didn't↗. | contrast — "…I did something else" (denying one thing, implying another) | /aɪ ˈdɪdnt ↘↗/ — **fall-rise** on "did-" | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |

## C. Focus / nuclear stress — moving the nucleus changes the meaning

The **nucleus** (the syllable with the main pitch movement) does not have to be
the last lexical word — you can **shift it** to mark which part is **new
information**. Same words, different nucleus → answers a different question.
Wells (2006) calls this **tonicity**; Wikipedia illustrates it verbatim:
*"I saw a ↘man in the garden"* answers "Whom did you see?", whereas
*"I ↘saw a man in the garden"* answers "Did you hear a man in the garden?".

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I saw a ↘MAN in the garden. | nucleus on "man" → answers **"Whom did you see?"** (the man is the new info) | /aɪ sɔː ə ˈmæn ↘ ɪn ðə ˈɡɑːdn/ | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |
| I ↘SAW a man in the garden. | nucleus on "saw" → answers **"Did you hear a man…?"** (the seeing is the new info) | /aɪ ˈsɔː ↘ ə mæn ɪn ðə ˈɡɑːdn/ | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |
| She's a ↘TEACHER?↗ | nucleus + rise on "teacher" → surprise/disbelief on that specific word ("teacher, really?") | /ʃiːz ə ˈtiːtʃə ↗/ — nucleus **rise** on "teach-" | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) | n/a (prosody) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the focus-stress shift is Wikipedia *Intonation
> (linguistics)* → "Functions" → focusing function, cited there to Wells (2006)
> and Cruttenden (1997). The ↘/↗ arrows-on-nucleus notation follows the same
> source.

## Native audio (YouGlish — phrase search, verified to resolve, HTTP 200)

YouGlish supports **phrase** search (not just single words), so the intonation
carrier phrases can be heard in real native speech. URL pattern (returns 200):
`https://youglish.com/pronounce/{phrase}/english/us?`

Verified-resolving phrase clips used by the player (HTTP 200 on 2026-06-23):
`She's a teacher`, `Where do you live`, `Do you want coffee`, `Could you help
me`, `See you tomorrow`, `I saw a man`.

> **Honest caveat:** YouGlish returns the **phrase as it is naturally spoken**;
> the speaker's own tone may vary by context (e.g. *"She's a teacher?"* in a
> real clip may carry a rise *or* a fall depending on the scene). The clips are
> for **segmental + rhythm** modelling; the **arrow marks (↘/↗/↘↗) in the
> player and corpus are the prescribed target tones**, grounded in Cambridge /
> Roach / Wells, not in any single clip's accident.
