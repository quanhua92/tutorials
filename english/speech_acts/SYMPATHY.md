## Why this is bundle #24 (read this first)

When a friend tells you bad news in Vietnamese, the culture hands you a script:
*chia buồn* for everyday grief, *phúng điếu* for a funeral, and a ritualized set
of offerings. The script is so fixed that **you rarely have to invent words.**
English gives you no such script — instead it gives you **a three-move sequence**
that you must assemble on the spot:

1. **Acknowledge** the news (*I'm so sorry to hear that*),
2. **React** to its weight (*That sounds awful*), and
3. **Name the feeling** before you offer anything (*You must be devastated*).

The two failure modes for a Vietnamese learner are symmetric and equally
damaging: **go silent** (because there is no ready formula), or **jump to
problem-solving** ("You should apply again!") because fixing feels safer than
feeling. Neither reads as sympathy in English. This bundle drills the three moves
and the register ladder — casual *That sounds rough* up to formal *My
condolences* — so the response comes out automatically.

## 1. The mechanism: *sorry* is a dual-function word

English *sorry* does **two jobs** that Vietnamese splits across two words:

| | Vietnamese (L1) | English (target) |
|---|---|---|
| "Sorry, I broke your cup" (apology) | *xin lỗi* | *I'm sorry* (apology) |
| "Sorry your dog died" (sympathy) | *chia buồn* | *I'm sorry to hear that* (sympathy) |
| The fix when ambiguous | context | add **to hear that / about X** to force the *sympathy* reading |

Bare *I'm sorry* is an apology — it sounds like you caused the problem. To make
it sympathy you **extend the chunk**: *I'm so sorry to hear that.* The extension
is what flips the function, and it is the single highest-frequency sympathy move
in the language.

> From `sympathy_corpus.md`:
>
> | I'm so sorry to hear that. | I'm really sorry to hear that. |
> |---|---|
> | /aɪm səʊ ˈsɒri tə hɪə ðæt/ UK | /aɪm ˈrɪəli ˈsɑːri tə hɪr ðæt/ US |
>
> Both are attested in the Cambridge Dictionary blog *Expressing sympathy*
> (Liz Walter, CUP) and the Oxford *sorry* "Express Yourself" box. The pinned
> sympathy anchor of the whole bundle.

## 2. The three-move sequence (the core chunk)

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    N["Friend shares<br/>bad news"] --> A["1 · ACKNOWLEDGE<br/>I'm so sorry to hear that."]
    A --> R["2 · REACT to weight<br/>That sounds awful / rough."]
    R --> F["3 · NAME the feeling<br/>You must be devastated."]
    F --> O["(only then) offer<br/>I'm here for you."]
    style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style R fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
    style F fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
```

Move 2 is where Vietnamese learners most often stumble — either skipping it or
mis-calibrating the weight. The reaction must **match** the news:

> From `sympathy_corpus.md` (the reaction ladder, verbatim):
>
> - Mild / disappointing → **That's such a shame.** /ðæts sʌtʃ ə ʃeɪm/
> - Serious / spoken → **That sounds awful.** /ðæt saʊndz ˈɔːfl/
> - Informal, sympathetic → **That sounds rough.** /ðæt saʊndz rʌf/
> - Naming their pain → **How awful for you.** /haʊ ˈɔːfl fə(r) juː/

**The trap:** *rough* is informal and warm — great with friends, too casual for
a written condolence. *Awful* and *terrible* are neutral-to-serious and travel
across registers. *Shame* is for **disappointment**, not death — do not say
*"What a shame your father died."*

---

## 3. Move 3 — *must be* + adjective (the English empathy guess)

Where Vietnamese might say a single empathetic *bùn quá* or stay quiet, English
makes the empathy **grammatical**: modal *must* + *be* + adjective guesses the
other person's feeling. This is the structure that sounds native.

> From `sympathy_corpus.md`:
>
> - **You must be devastated.** /juː məst biː ˈdevəsteɪtɪd/ — serious news
> - **That must be hard.** /ðæt məst biː hɑːd/ UK · /hɑːrd/ US — neutral
> - **I can't imagine how you must feel.** /aɪ kɑːnt ɪˈmædʒɪn…/ — when you
>   truly can't know their pain (death, major loss)

Note the **stress on the adjective**: you MUST be de-VAS-ta-ted. The adjective
carries the emotion; *must be* is just the grammatical glue.

---

## 4. The formal rung — *condolences* is only for death

The top of the register ladder is fixed collocations, and they are **reserved
for death**. Using *condolences* for a lost job or an illness reads as if the
person has died — a serious pragmatics error.

> From `sympathy_corpus.md`:
>
> | My condolences. | Please accept my deepest sympathies. |
> |---|---|
> | /maɪ kənˈdəʊlənsɪz/ UK · /maɪ kənˈdoʊlənsɪz/ US | /pliːz əkˈsept maɪ ˈdiːpɪst ˈsɪmpəθiz/ |
>
> | Our heartfelt sympathy goes out to you. | I'm sorry for your loss. |
> |---|---|
> | /aʊə(r) ˈhɑːtfelt ˈsɪmpəθi…/ | /aɪm ˈsɒri fə(r) jɔː(r) lɒs/ UK |

Three fixed patterns to memorize: ***condolences*** (always plural, formal),
***deepest sympathies*** (formal, written), ***sorry for your loss*** (the
standard spoken line at a death). All four are quoted verbatim from the Oxford
*sympathy*/*condolence* entries and the Cambridge *Expressing sympathy* blog.

## 5. Cheat sheet — the ≤8 survival chunks

The Pareto set. These eight cover the whole register ladder from "heard the bad
news" to "writing a condolence card." (Every row is a corpus attestation above.)

| # | Chunk | IPA | Why it's here |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **I'm so sorry to hear that.** | /aɪm soʊ ˈsɑːri tə hɪr ðæt/ US | the #1 acknowledgement move (pinned) |
| 2 | **That sounds rough.** | /ðæt saʊndz rʌf/ | informal, warm reaction (pinned) |
| 3 | **That sounds awful.** | /ðæt saʊndz ˈɔːfl/ | serious-news reaction |
| 4 | **How awful for you.** | /haʊ ˈɔːfl fɔːr juː/ US | name their pain (empathy) |

## 6. Vietnamese → English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits when expressing sympathy — extend, don't replace, the seed rows
from the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Goes silent** — no ready sympathy formula, so you say nothing | Deploy the chunk: *I'm so sorry to hear that.* Silence reads as cold/uncaring in English; the acknowledgement line is the minimum social obligation. |
| **Uses bare *I'm sorry* for sympathy** → sounds like an apology (you caused it) | Extend it: *I'm so sorry **to hear that**.* The extension flips *sorry* from apology → sympathy. |
| **Jumps to advice / problem-solving** ("You should apply again!") before acknowledging feeling | Run the three moves first (acknowledge → react → name feeling). 🔗 See [ADVISING](./ADVISING.md) — advice comes **after** empathy, never instead of it. |
| **Uses *condolences* for non-death** (lost job, illness) → sounds like the person died | *Condolences / deepest sympathies* are **death-only**. For a lost job use *I'm so sorry to hear that*; for illness, *How awful for you.* |
| **Over-uses *chia buồn* register** (funeral-formal) for everyday bad news | Match the weight: everyday bad news → *That sounds rough / awful* (warm, not ritualized). Save *chia buồn*-equivalents for death. |
| **Says *I'm sorry for your loss* in casual chat** (too heavy for the moment) | *Sorry for your loss* is the **death** line. For a failed exam it's *That's such a shame* — disappointment register, not bereavement. |
| **Drops finals** → *devastated* → "devasta", *condolences* → "condolence" | Hold every final: -ated /eɪtɪd/, -ces /ɪz/. 🔗 [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md) — these exact clusters are bundle #01. |
| **Confuses *sympathy* and *empathy*** or treats *sympathy* as a false friend (some languages use it for "liking") | In English *sympathy* = feeling **sorry** for someone's bad situation (**bad news only**). Don't say *"I have sympathy for your new car."* |
| **Translates *bùn quá* literally** → "I'm sad for you" (clunky) | Use the *must be* + adjective guess: *You must be devastated / That must be hard.* It's the native empathy structure. |
| **/θ/ in *sympathy* → /s/** → "sympasy" | *sympathy* /ˈsɪmpəθi/ has a /θ/. Tongue between the teeth. 🔗 [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md). |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) — this guide, §1–§4.
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) — open `sympathy.html`, drill the 8 flip cards + the
   role-play **aloud**, exaggerating every final (-*ated*, -*ces*) and stressing
   the adjective in *You MUST be de-VAS-ta-ted*.
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) — the writing task: write **one sympathy line** for a
   friend who failed an interview (*I'm so sorry to hear that. That sounds
   rough.*) and **one** for a colleague whose parent died (*I'm sorry for your
   loss. My condolences.*). Read both aloud; check the register matches the
   weight.
