## Why this bundle exists (read this first)

The single highest-leverage workplace skill a Vietnamese-L1 professional can add
is **graceful feedback reception**. In Vietnamese workplace culture, criticism
collides with **face** (*thŠĽÉ diŠĽán*): being corrected in front of others is felt
as a status wound, so the default L1 responses are a **defensive justification**
("Nh∆įng m√†‚Ä¶", "Kh√īng phŠļ£i lŠĽói cŠĽßa em" = "But‚Ä¶", "It wasn't my fault") or a **stony
silence** that the giver reads as sulking. Both leak into English and read, to a
native manager, as **immature or uncoachable** ‚ÄĒ even when the work is excellent.

Native English speakers, by contrast, are trained from school age in a three-move
ritual that *defuses* the threat before anything is argued:

```mermaid

    ACK["1. ACKNOWLEDGE<br/>Thanks for the feedback.<br/>That's a fair point."] --> CLAR["2. CLARIFY<br/>(only if needed)<br/>Could you give me<br/>an example?"]
    CLAR --> COM["3. COMMIT<br/>I'll work on that.<br/>Next time I'll‚Ä¶"]
    style ACK fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
    style CLAR fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style COM fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
```

**Acknowledge ‚Üí (Clarify) ‚Üí Commit.** That arc is the whole bundle. Every chunk
below is one of those three moves. Drill the arc and you will sound ‚ÄĒ in the space
of one sentence ‚ÄĒ like a professional who can *take* feedback, which is what
managers actually promote.

## 1. The acknowledge move (don't skip it)

The most common L1-Vietnamese error is to **skip straight to the defense**. The
native convention demands an **uptake token first** ‚ÄĒ a short phrase that says "I
heard you, and I'm not fighting you." Cambridge records `Thanks for‚Ä¶` as the
standard grateful formula; `That's a fair point` concedes the validity of the
point *without* conceding the argument; `I appreciate‚Ä¶` expresses grateful
recognition (CALD sense 2: *"to be grateful for something"*).

feedback as *new information* rather than an accusation. It tells the giver "you

time ‚ÄĒ the most efficient double-move in the language.

## 2. The clarify move (ask, don't defend)

If the feedback is vague or you genuinely don't know what to fix, the skilled
move is a **clarifying question**, *not* a counter-argument. A defensive *"but‚Ä¶"*
turns feedback into a fight; a curious *"Could you say more about‚Ä¶?"* turns it
into a dialogue. The modal `Could you‚Ä¶?` is Cambridge's polite-request form; it
keeps the tone **collaborative, not combative**.

> From `feedback_receiving_corpus.md` (the clarify set, verbatim):
>
> - **Could you give me an example?** /k äd ju ňą…°…™v mi …ôn …™…°ňąz…Ďňźmp…ôl/ UK ¬∑
>   /k äd ju ňą…°…™v mi …ôn …™…°ňąz√¶mp…ôl/ US
> - **Could you say more about‚Ä¶?** /k äd ju ňąse…™ m…Ēňźr …ôňąba ät/

**The Vietnamese trap:** learners either (a) mount an instant defense ("But I
did‚Ä¶") or (b) nod silently without understanding and then repeat the same
mistake. The clarify move avoids *both* ‚ÄĒ it shows engagement *and* buys you the
specifics you need to actually improve.

## 3. The commit move (close the loop)

Acknowledgement alone reads as passive; a **commitment move** is what separates
graceful reception from dismissal. Cambridge records `work on` (phrasal verb:
*"to try hard to achieve or improve something"*) and `keep something in mind`
(CALD: *"to remember a fact or piece of information, especially one that might be
useful in the future"*). `Next time I'll‚Ä¶` is the forward-looking repair frame.

## 4. Delivery notes (how it should *sound*)

- **Stress the feeling word, not the filler.** Say *"I **appreciate** that"* ‚ÄĒ
  not *"I appreciate **that**"*. The content word (`appreciate`, `fair`, `work`)
  carries the meaning; the grammar words (`I`, `that`, `the`) reduce to schwas.
- **`I'll` is one syllable** /a…™l/, not two ("I will"). Dropping it to "I will"
  sounds stiff and formal for spoken reception.
- **`Thanks` /őł√¶Ňčks/** ‚ÄĒ the **/őł/** is the th-sound (tongue between teeth).
  Vietnamese learners often say *"tanks"* (/t√¶Ňčks/) which a native hears as the
  military vehicle. ūüĒó Drill this in [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md).
- **Soften the consonant cluster in `next time`** /nekst ta…™m/ ‚ÄĒ the /kst/+
  /t/ sequence is exactly where Vietnamese drops a sound. ūüĒó See
  [CONSONANT CLUSTERS](../pronunciation/CONSONANT_CLUSTERS.md).
- **`Could you`** often reduces to /k…ôd í…ô/ in fast speech ‚ÄĒ "Couldja give me‚Ä¶?"
  Don't force it, but recognise it when you hear it. ūüĒó See
  [REDUCTIONS](../pronunciation/REDUCTIONS.md).
