## A. The register ladder — ONE message at three levels

Register is a **ladder, not a switch**. The same intent (ask for a file) climbs
from **formal** (to a stranger / client / senior) through **neutral** (default
workplace) to **casual** (to a close teammate). Each rung swaps **vocabulary**,
**contractions**, and **hedging**. Confirmed as the core model of business-
writing register in the Cambridge *Writing* guidance and the Manchester Academic
Phrasebank ("Formality" / "Hedging" sections).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I am writing to inquire about the document referenced in your message. | (FORMAL) open an inquiry, full forms, no contractions | phrase | https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/being-critical/ | phrase | US/UK |
| Could you send over the file when you have a moment? | (NEUTRAL) polite request, one contraction, modal "could" | phrase | https://www.scribbr.com/effective-communication/end-an-email/ | phrase | US/UK |
| Hey, quick one — can you send that file? | (CASUAL) IM/email to a peer, "hey" + "quick one" + "that" | phrase | https://youglish.com/pronounce/quick%20one/english/us? | phrase | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the three-run ladder (formal ↔ neutral ↔ casual) is the
> canonical register model in Cambridge Grammar of English (Carter & McCarthy,
> CUP) and the Manchester Academic Phrasebank. The full-form vs contracted
> contrast (*I am* vs *I'm* vs *I'd*) and the modal ladder (*would* → *could* →
> *can*) are the two levers that move a message between rungs.

## C. Casual markers (the "down the ladder" vocabulary)

These **lower** register: contractions, one-syllable connectors, fillers
("hey", "just"), and slangy sign-offs. Confirmed as informal in Cambridge,
Collins, and Merriam-Webster, each tagged `[informal]` in the source.
