## A. The "big three" auxiliary reductions (going to / want to / got to)

These three are the highest-frequency spoken reductions in English. Each
collapses a two-word auxiliary + *to* into one chunk where the final consonant
of the first word **assimilates** across the word boundary (/t/ → /d/ or drops)
and *to* /tu/ weakens to schwa /ə/. Confirmed in the Cambridge Advanced
Learner's Dictionary entries (which now list these as headwords) and in the
UB Graz / UDPU phonetics references.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| gonna | informal for *going to* | /ˈɡə.nə/ UK · /ˈɡɑː.nə/ US (stressed form often /ˈɡʌnə/) | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/gonna | ≈#200 (spoken) | US/UK |
| wanna | short form of *want to* or *want a* | /ˈwɒn.ə/ UK · /ˈwɑː.nə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/wanna | ≈#350 (spoken) | US/UK |
| gotta | *have got to* / *have to*; also *got a* | /ˈɡɒtə/ UK · /ˈɡɑtə/ US | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gotta | ≈#400 (spoken) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** Cambridge lists `gonna` (UK /ˈɡə.nə/, US /ˈɡɑː.nə/) and
> `wanna` (UK /ˈwɒn.ə/, US /ˈwɑː.nə/) as full headwords; the commonly-cited
> stressed form of *gonna* /ˈɡʌnə/ is recorded in Wiktionary and matches the
> Cambridge Academic Content Dictionary variant `/ˌɡɔ·nə, ˌɡɑn·ə, ɡən·ə/`.
> `gotta` UK /ˈɡɒtə/ · US /ˈɡɑtə/ confirmed in Wiktionary with the voicing
> note [ˈɡɒʔə] UK / [ˈɡɑɾə] US (flap-T, the American hallmark).

## B. Pronoun & question-word reductions (you / them / do you / what do you)

English eats its function words. The pronouns *you* and *them* and the question
clusters *do you* / *what do you* / *what are you* collapse in fast speech into
single syllables. These are the reductions that **break listening
comprehension** for a Vietnamese learner even when every word is known.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ya | reduced form of *you* | /jə/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/ya | (of *you* ≈#10) | US/UK |
| 'em | reduced form of *them* (historic, from *hem*) | /əm/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27em | (of *them* ≈#60) | US/UK |
| d'ya | reduced form of *do you* | /djə/ · /dʒə/ (yod-coalesced) | https://youglish.com/pronounce/do%20you/english/us? | — | US/UK |
| whaddya | reduced form of *what do you* / *what are you* | /ˈwʌd.jə/ US · /ˈwɒd.jə/ UK (often /ˈwɑdʒə/ US fully coalesced) | https://youglish.com/pronounce/whaddya/english/us? | — | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `ya` /jə/ is the standard weak form of *you* (Cambridge
> "ya" headword). `'em` /əm/ is recorded in Wiktionary as the cliticised form of
> *them* (historically from Old English *hem* — the *th-* was never there; this
> is **not** a "lazy" drop of *th*). `d'ya` and `whaddya` are **not dictionary
> headwords** — they are spoken reductions; IPA is reconstructed from the
> Cambridge weak-form of *you* /jə/ plus the documented assimilation
> /dj/ → /dʒ/ (yod-coalescence, same mechanism that produces *gotcha* in §C).
> Attestation is via YouGlish clips (linked) and the pronunciation-teaching
> sources cited below. Flagged: these two rows rely on teaching/clip sources
> rather than a dictionary headword.

## C. Phrase reductions (out of / because / don't know / got you / let me / give me)

Whole two- and three-word phrases collapse in casual speech. *Got you* →
*gotcha* is the textbook case of **yod-coalescence** (/tj/ → /tʃ/), the same
process that turns *did you* → *didja* and *meet you* → *meetcha*.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| outta | *out of* | /ˈaʊtə/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/outta | ≈#800 (spoken) | US/UK |
| 'cuz / cuz | informal for *because* | /kʌz/ · /kɒz/ strong · /kəz/ weak | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cuz | (of *because* ≈#70) | US/UK |
| dunno | *don't know* / *does not know* | /ˈdʌnəʊ/ UK · /dəˈnoʊ/ US (after a stressed pronoun: /dn̩oʊ/) | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dunno | ≈#1500 (spoken) | US/UK |
| gotcha | *got you* (also "I understand / caught you") | /ˈɡɒtʃə/ UK · /ˈɡɑːtʃə/ US | https://youglish.com/pronounce/gotcha/english/us? | ≈#3000 (spoken) | US/UK |
| lemme | *let me* | /ˈlemə/ | https://youglish.com/pronounce/lemme/english/us? | — | US/UK |
| gimme | *give me* | /ˈɡɪmə/ | https://youglish.com/pronounce/gimme/english/us? | — | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `outta` /ˈaʊtə/ confirmed in Wiktionary (UK
> [ˈaʊtə], US [ˈaʊɾə] flap-T). `cuz` /kʌz/–/kɒz/ strong, /kəz/ weak confirmed in
> Wiktionary. `dunno` RP /ˈdʌnəʊ/–/dəˈnəʊ/, GenAm /dəˈnoʊ/ confirmed in
> Wiktionary. `gotcha` /ˈɡɒtʃə/ (from *got you* /ˈɡɒtju/) confirmed in *Practical
> Phonetics of the English Language* (UDPU) and *English Accents and Dialects*
> (Udon Data Repository) as a cross-word yod-coalescence — "the colloquial
> *gotcha* /ˈɡɒtʃə/ (for *got you* /ˈɡɒtju/)". `lemme` /ˈlemə/ and `gimme`
> /ˈɡɪmə/ confirmed in the UB Graz reductions reference and the Demirezen
> (2020) ERIC paper on pronunciation spelling.
