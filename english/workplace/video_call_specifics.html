6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Stays silent when tech fails — sees the mute/freeze but waits, hoping it self-fixesFire the chunk in <1s: "Hold on, you're on mute." / "You're frozen — can you repeat?" Silence reads as incompetence; a fast chunk reads as fluency.
Shame about tech hiccups — apologises profusely ("I'm so sorry, my internet is bad")One light apology + move on: "Sorry — I was on mute. As I was saying,…" Mute is a non-event to natives.
Drops final consonants → "You're on mew" for mute, "You're froze" for frozenRelease the final: mute /mjuːt/ (hit the /t/), frozen /ˈfrəʊ.zən/ (hit the /n/).
No past/participle agreement → "You are freeze" / "You are break up"Use the participle: You're frozen. / You're breaking up. / You're cutting out. — fixed be + -ing/-en.
/θ/ → /t/, /ð/ → /z/ → "I tink we lost dem"Tongue-between-teeth: think /θɪŋk/, them /ðəm/.
Literal translation → "You are being turned off sound"Retrieve the fixed chunk: "You're on mute." / "You're muted." — non-compositional; memorise as units.
Over-explains the failure → "The connection of network have some problem with delay"Name the symptom in one chunk: "Your audio is lagging." / "There's an echo." Diagnose in chat, never aloud.
Mis-stresses the chunk → "You're ON mute" (wrong word)Stress the content word: "You're on MUTE." Grammar words (you're, on, is) stay weak.
