5Writing task — write a complete 4-part email

Write a complete email: subject line + salutation + BLUF body + closing + sign-off. Use the prompt, then reveal the annotated model.

✉ Prompt

You need your manager, Ms Park, to review your project report (Q3-report.pdf) by Wednesday. Write a 4-part email: subject, salutation, BLUF body (ask first, then context), closing, sign-off.

Annotated model email (every part labelled):

[SUBJECT] ACTION: Q3 report review needed by Wednesday

— keyword tag (ACTION) + topic + deadline. Clear before the reader even opens it.


[SALUTATION] Dear Ms Park,

— formal: title + surname (she's your manager).


[BLUF BODY]

Could you review the attached Q3 report by Wednesday?

— the ask + deadline in sentence 1. BLUF: bottom line up front.

I hope this finds you well. Please find attached the report for your feedback before the Thursday meeting.

— polite opening + attachment note + brief context. The ask is already stated above.


[CLOSING] Looking forward to hearing from you.

— the formal closing: states the next step (a reply).


[SIGN-OFF]

Best regards,

[Your name]

— two words. Done. No elaborate ceremony.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Essay-style structure — long opening, request buried at the end (văn nghị luận style)BLUF: state the ask in sentence 1, then context. Invert the essay.
Vague subject lines — "hello", "question", or your own nameUse [Action] Topic — deadline: "ACTION: Budget approval needed by Friday."
Over-formal everywhere — "Dear Sir/Madam" to colleagues you email dailyCalibrate: "Hi [first name]," for colleagues; "Dear Mr/Ms [surname]," for new contacts.
Under-formal with strangers — "Hi" to a hiring manager you've never metDefault to "Dear [Job Title]," or "Dear Mr/Ms [Surname]," until invited to first-name.
Word-for-word translation — "I send you the file" (← tôi gửi bạn file)Use fixed chunks: "Please find attached …" Don't translate — retrieve the chunk.
Burying the ask behind politeness — "I was wondering if perhaps you might…"Be direct but polite: "Could you confirm the meeting time by Friday?"
Elaborate closing rituals — long sign-offs with wishes (Trân trọng kính chào)"Best regards," — two words, full stop. English emails are transactional, not ceremonial.
"Kindly" overuse — "Kindly find attached", "Kindly do the needful"Use "Please find attached" (standard) — kindly reads as archaic to US/UK readers.
