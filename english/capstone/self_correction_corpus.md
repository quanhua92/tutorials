## A. STEP 1 — Quick correction markers (the smooth, native move)

The single highest-frequency self-repair strategy in native speech: notice a
slip, drop **one** short marker, supply the corrected version, **keep going**.
These five are the workhorses. The two **pinned** chunks (bolded ⭐) are the ones
the corpus must contain verbatim so the attestation is sanity-checkable.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ⭐ **Sorry, what I meant was…** | "let me give you the version I actually intended" (smooth mid-sentence fix) | /ˈsɒri wɒt aɪ ment wɒz/ UK · /ˈsɑːri wɑːt aɪ ment wɑːz/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/meant | ≈#1200 (of *mean*) | US/UK |
| ⭐ **Or rather…** | "to be more precise / to correct that" (one-word pivot) | /ɔː ˈrɑːðə/ UK · /ɔːr ˈræðər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/rather | ≈#350 (of *rather*) | US/UK |
| I mean… | "what I really mean is…" (light self-repair / softener) | /aɪ miːn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/mean | ≈#60 (of *mean*) | US/UK |
| Actually, let me rephrase | "in fact, let me say it in clearer words" | /ˈæktʃuəli let mi ˌriːˈfreɪz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/actually | ≈#90 (of *actually*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned anchors — sanity check these are real):**
> - ⭐ *Sorry, what I meant was…* — *mean* → *meant* /ment/ is the standard
>   Cambridge past form; the self-repair function ("what I meant was…") is
>   attested as a conversation-repair gambit in English with Kim's *Recover and
>   Keep Going After Making a Mistake* and the Oxford English Grammar Course
>   *Advanced* gambits chapter.
> - ⭐ *Or rather…* — **Cambridge Grammar (*English Grammar Today*) attests it
>   verbatim for self-correction**: *"We use or rather to correct ourselves:
>   He commanded and I obeyed, or rather, I pretended to."* (see source URL,
>   the "Or rather" subsection). Cross-attested in the University of Babylon
>   humanities gambits reference.
> - *I mean…* — attested as a self-repair / correction discourse marker in
>   Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks (1977) and Levelt (1989); Cambridge's *Spoken
>   English* discourse-markers section lists it as a correction/softening
>   marker ("I mean can be used to correct oneself").
> - *Actually, let me rephrase* — *actually* /ˈæk.tʃu.ə.li/ Cambridge attests
>   as a correction marker (sense "SAYING NO": *"Actually, Gavin, it was
>   Tuesday of last week, not Wednesday"*); *rephrase* /ˌriːˈfreɪz/ confirmed in
>   Cambridge, Oxford, Collins, Britannica (≥4 sources).
> - *To put it differently* — *differently* /ˈdɪfrəntli/ UK · /ˈdɪfərəntli/ US
>   is the standard Cambridge form; the chunk is a textbook rephrasing gambit
>   (University of Babylon gambits list).
> - Connected-speech reductions: *let me* → /lemi/ ("lemme"), *what I* →
>   /wɒtaɪ/ → /wəɾaɪ/, *to* → /tə/. These are standard documented reductions;
>   the citation form above is the retrievable form the learner practises first.

## B. STEP 2 — Restart (when the sentence has gone off the rails)

Sometimes the slip is too tangled for a quick patch — the whole clause needs to
be abandoned and re-launched. These two mark a **clean restart**: a brief
acknowledgement, then a fresh start. The discipline is the same — **do not
freeze, do not over-apologize** — just re-enter the sentence.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wait, let me start over | "let me abandon that and begin again" | /weɪt let mi stɑːt ˈəʊvə/ UK · /weɪt let mi stɑːrt ˈoʊvər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/start-over | ≈#600 (of *start*) | US/UK |
| Let me try that again | "let me have another go at that" | /let mi traɪ ðæt əˈɡen/ UK · /let mi traɪ ðæt əˈɡen/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/try | ≈#150 (of *try*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *start over* /ˌstɑːt ˈəʊvə/ UK · /ˌstɑːrt ˈoʊvər/ US
> is the standard Cambridge phrasal verb ("to begin to do something again from
> the beginning"); *try* /traɪ/, *again* /əˈɡen/ are standard Cambridge forms.
> Both chunks are attested as restart gambits in English with Kim's *Recover and
> Keep Going After Making a Mistake* ("Let me try that again", "start over").

## C. STEP 3 — Acknowledge a slip (own the mistake, lightly)

When the slip is a **fact or word error** (the wrong number, the wrong name), the
native move is to **name it briefly** and substitute the correct version — one
short acknowledgement, then move on. This is *not* a confession of incompetence;
it is the normal rhythm of native speech, where speakers correct themselves
constantly (Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks 1977 call self-repair the **preferred**
organization of conversation).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sorry, I misspoke | "apologies — that was the wrong word/fact" | /ˈsɒri aɪ ˌmɪsˈspəʊk/ UK · /ˈsɑːri aɪ ˌmɪsˈspoʊk/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/misspeak | ≈#9000 (of *misspeak*) | US/UK |
| Well, more accurately… | "to state it more precisely…" | /wel mɔː ˈækjərətli/ UK · /wel mɔːr ˈækjɚətli/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/accurate | ≈#1800 (of *accurate*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *misspeak* /ˌmɪsˈspiːk/, past *misspoke*
> /ˌmɪsˈspəʊk/ UK · /ˌmɪsˈspoʊk/ US is the standard Cambridge form ("to say
> something that is not correct, by mistake"); *accurate* /ˈæk.jə.rət/ UK ·
> /ˈæk.jɚ.ət/ US (Cambridge) → adverb *accurately* /ˈækjərətli/ UK ·
> /ˈækjɚətli/ US. *Sorry, I misspoke* is the canonical public-figure
> self-correction formula (attested across news corpora); *Well, more
> accurately…* is the hedge-repair form documented in the Manchester Academic
> Phrasebank hedging section.
