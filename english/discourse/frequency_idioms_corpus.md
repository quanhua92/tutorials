# `frequency_idioms_corpus.md` — Ground Truth

> **Phase 4 · discourse · bundle #69.** Every English line that appears in
> `FREQUENCY_IDIOMS.md` or `frequency_idioms.html` is a real, attested row in this
> file with a clickable source. **Nothing is invented.**
>
> **Column contract** (copied from the style anchor,
> `pronunciation/final_consonants_corpus.md`):
>
> `| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |`
>
> - **IPA** transcribed verbatim from a real learner's dictionary (Cambridge /
>   Oxford Learner's). Idioms have no single headword, so each idiom's IPA is the
>   concatenation of its component words' Cambridge transcriptions (stress on the
>   final content word marked `ˈ`). US/UK given where they differ.
> - **source URL** resolves to the attested form — a dictionary idiom entry
>   (Cambridge / Oxford / Merriam-Webster).
> - **frequency rank** has TWO witnesses per idiom, per the brief:
>   1. a **dictionary idiom entry** (Cambridge / Oxford / Merriam-Webster), and
>   2. a **frequency source**. Where Liu (2003) directly attests the idiom, the
>      band is cited. Otherwise the **Cambridge CEFR level** (B1/B2) is the
>      frequency proxy — Cambridge's B1–B2 band *is* a frequency ranking for
>      learners (it lists only high-frequency vocabulary/idioms). The methodology
>      is cited, not a fabricated integer.
> - **accent** = the variety the IPA was pulled for (`US` / `UK` / `US/UK`).
>
> **CURATION PRINCIPLE (the brief):** learn only the ~10–15 highest-utility
> idioms; **skip rare ones** — that time is better spent on phrasal verbs and
> collocations. All ten below are **non-compositional**: their meaning cannot be
> decoded from the words, so literal translation into Vietnamese (or any L1)
> produces nonsense.
>
> **Sources at the bottom of this file.** IPA spot-checks: each transcription was
> built from ≥2 component-word dictionary entries (Cambridge + Oxford).

| on the same page | **lit.** "reading the same physical page" → **fig.** sharing the same understanding/aims | UK /ɒn ðə ˈseɪm ˈpeɪdʒ/ · US /ɑːn ðə ˈseɪm ˈpeɪdʒ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/page | Cambridge Business English; Merriam-Webster entry | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `a piece of cake` — Cambridge (B2, informal): "something
> that is very easy to do: *The exam was a piece of cake.*"; component IPAs
> `piece` /piːs/, `cake` /keɪk/ both Cambridge. `on the same page` — Cambridge
> Business English: "to have the same ideas as someone else: *Everyone in the
> office has to be on the same page about what our top priorities are.*";
> Merriam-Webster: "agreeing about something (such as how things should be done)";
> `page` /peɪdʒ/ Cambridge (UK & US identical), `on` /ɒn/ UK /ɑːn/ US Cambridge.

> expected, because Liu's corpora (MICASE, professional AmE) under-sample the
> colourful everyday register where these idioms live. They are not rare; they are
> register-mismatched to that one corpus.
