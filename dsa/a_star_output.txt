========================================================================
  SECTION B: the f = g + h formula (and the monotonic-f property)
========================================================================
Walk the optimal path and read off the true g, h, f at each cell.
(g = cost so far along the path; every step costs 1, so g = step index.)

  | cell    | g (cost so far) | h (Manhattan to G) | f = g + h |
  |---------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------|
  | (0, 0) | 0               | 8                   | 8         |
  | (0, 1) | 1               | 7                   | 8         |
  | (0, 2) | 2               | 6                   | 8         |
  | (0, 3) | 3               | 5                   | 8         |
  | (1, 3) | 4               | 6                   | 10        |
  | (2, 3) | 5               | 7                   | 12        |
  | (3, 3) | 6               | 8                   | 14        |
  | (4, 3) | 7               | 9                   | 16        |
  | (5, 3) | 8               | 10                  | 18        |
  | (5, 4) | 9               | 9                   | 18        |
  | (5, 5) | 10              | 8                   | 18        |
  | (4, 5) | 11              | 7                   | 18        |
  | (3, 5) | 12              | 6                   | 18        |
  | (2, 5) | 13              | 5                   | 18        |
  | (1, 5) | 14              | 4                   | 18        |
  | (0, 5) | 15              | 3                   | 18        |
  | (0, 6) | 16              | 2                   | 18        |
  | (0, 7) | 17              | 1                   | 18        |
  | (0, 8) | 18              | 0                   | 18        |

  f is NOT constant on the path - it RISES. The first four cells head
  straight toward G so h shrinks 1-for-1 with g and f stays 8. But the wall
  forces the path to detour DOWN column 3, away from G: there h GROWS while g
  keeps growing, so f climbs 8 -> 10 -> 12 -> 14 -> 16 -> 18. Once the path
  turns back toward G (across the gap and up column 5) h shrinks 1-for-1
  again and f holds at 18 - the true optimal cost - all the way to G.
  [check] f non-decreasing along the optimal path?  YES
  [check] f non-decreasing along the EXPANSION order?  YES (consistent h => no reopen)

Why consistency holds for Manhattan on a unit-cost grid: moving one cell
changes the true distance to G by at most 1, and h = Manhattan changes by at
most 1 per step, so h(n) <= 1 + h(neighbour) = cost(n,neighbour) + h(neighbour).
Consistency => f never decreases as A* expands => a closed node is never reopened.

========================================================================
  SECTION C: admissible vs inadmissible heuristic
========================================================================
ADMISSIBLE: h(n) <= h*(n) for all n (never overestimates the true cost).
  - guarantees A* is OPTIMAL.
INADMISSIBLE: h(n) > h*(n) somewhere (overestimates).
  - A* runs FASTER (explores fewer cells) but may return a SUBOPTIMAL path.

Admissible: h = Manhattan (never overestimates on a unit grid).
  -> A* cost = 18 (OPTIMAL), expands 31 cells.

Now OVERESTIMATE by scaling h (inadmissible 'Weighted A*'):

  | h multiplier | meaning        | A* cost | optimal? | cells expanded |
  |--------------|----------------|---------|----------|----------------|
  | 1.0          | Manhattan      | 18      | yes      | 31             |
  | 2.0          | Weighted A* (2.0x) | 18      | yes      | 30             |
  | 3.0          | Weighted A* (3.0x) | 18      | yes      | 29             |
  | 5.0          | Weighted A* (5.0x) | 18      | yes      | 28             |

  On THIS grid the overestimate happens to stay optimal (only one good route),
  but it explores fewer cells and the optimality GUARANTEE is gone. To prove
  inadmissibility can actually break optimality, here is a tiny weighted graph:

    S --1--> A --100--> G        (long route, total 101)
     \                       
      1                       
       v                      
        B -------1------> G    (short route, total 2)

  | heuristic           | h(A) | h(B) | A* cost to G | optimal (2)? |
  |---------------------|------|------|--------------|--------------|
  | admissible (=true)  | 100  | 1    | 2            | yes          |
  | inadmissible        | 0    | 200  | 101          | NO |

  With h(B)=200 (overestimate), A* settles A first, pops G via the long route
  (f=101) BEFORE ever expanding B (f=1+200=201) -> returns 101, NOT 2.
  THE RULE: drop admissibility and you trade OPTIMALITY for SPEED, knowingly.

========================================================================
  SECTION E: why admissible h guarantees an optimal path
========================================================================
THEOREM (Hart, Nilsson, Raphael 1968): if h is ADMISSIBLE (h(n) <= h*(n) for
all n), then when A* pops the goal G it has found an optimal path.

Proof sketch (by the f-bound):
  Let f(n) = g(n) + h(n). A* always pops the open node with smallest f.
  - When G is popped, f(G) = g(G) + h(G) = g(G) + 0 = g(G).
  - For ANY other open node n on a path to G, admissibility gives
        f(n) = g(n) + h(n) <= g(n) + h*(n) = (true cost of best path through n)
    so f(n) is a lower bound on any path through n.
  - Since G was popped first, f(G) <= f(n) for every open n, hence g(G) is <=
    the best achievable through any open node. No cheaper path can exist.

Consistency (a stronger condition) adds: f never decreases, so A* never needs
to REOPEN a closed node. Manhattan is consistent on unit-cost grids.

Numerical confirmation on the worked grid:
  A*        cost = 18    (admissible Manhattan h)
  Dijkstra  cost = 18    (h = 0, trivially admissible)
  brute-force min (BFS by cost) = 18
  [check] A* optimal?  A* == Dijkstra == 18 -> YES

Bottom line: A* = Dijkstra + a heuristic. A good admissible h prunes the
search (Section D) WITHOUT sacrificing optimality (this section). That is why
A* dominates pathfinding in games, maps, and robotics.
