## Why this is bundle #30 (read this first)

A Vietnamese learner who has cleared Phase 1's social functions (#11‚Äď#29) can
greet, small-talk, agree, clarify, and close. But the moment the channel changes
‚ÄĒ a phone rings, a Zoom call opens ‚ÄĒ two new failure modes appear that
face-to-face practice never exposed:

1. **The identification trap.** On a phone you cannot be *seen*, so the first
   turn must *identify the speaker*. English uses the dummy *"it"* ‚ÄĒ **"Hi,
   it's X"** ‚ÄĒ or the demonstrative *"this is"* ‚ÄĒ **"Hello, this is X"**.
   Vietnamese learners, translating *"t√īi l√† X"* word-for-word, say **"I am X"**,
   which to a native ear sounds like roll-call or a formal meeting
   self-introduction, not a phone call. It is the single most common phone
   opening error.
2. **The tech-freeze trap.** When a video call's audio or video fails, a native
   speaker **narrates the problem out loud** ‚ÄĒ *"you're on mute"*, *"you're
   frozen"*, *"I think we lost them"*. A Vietnamese learner tends to **freeze
   and wait** in silence, because these call-specific diagnostic chunks were
   never in any textbook. The call stalls; everyone is uncomfortable.

This bundle drills the chunks that turn those two traps into automatic turns.
After it, you open a call like a native and you **talk through** a tech failure
instead of freezing in it.

## 1. The mechanism: the four beats of a call

Every phone or video call, native or learner, runs the same four-beat skeleton.
The beats are fixed; only the chunks inside them vary by channel and register:

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    OPEN["BEAT 1 ‚ÄĒ OPEN<br/>(identify + ask for)<br/>'Hi, it's X.'<br/>'I'd like to speak to‚Ä¶'"] --> XFER{"transfer / hold?"}
    XFER -->|"yes"| HOLD["BEAT 1b ‚ÄĒ HOLD<br/>'I'll put you through.'<br/>'Hold the line, please.'"]
    XFER -->|"no / video"| CHECK["BEAT 2 ‚ÄĒ TECH CHECK<br/>(video only)<br/>'Can you hear me?'<br/>'Can everyone see my screen?'"]
    HOLD --> CHECK
    CHECK --> BODY["BEAT 3 ‚ÄĒ BUSINESS<br/>(the actual call)"]
    BODY --> CLOSE["BEAT 4 ‚ÄĒ CLOSE<br/>'I'll let you go.'<br/>'Thanks for the call. Talk soon.'"]
    style OPEN fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style HOLD fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
    style CHECK fill:#f4ecf7,stroke:#b9a9e8,stroke-width:2px
    style CLOSE fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:3px
```

Beat 2 (the tech check) is **video-only** ‚ÄĒ you never ask *"can you hear me?"*
on a phone, because the answer is the call itself. Beat 1b (transfer/hold) is
**phone-only** ‚ÄĒ video calls dial direct. Knowing *which beat belongs to which
channel* is half the battle.

## 2. Phone openings ‚ÄĒ "Hi, it's X" (the identification beat)

The non-negotiable rule: on a phone, **identify with *it's* or *this is***, never
with *I am*. Cambridge documents *"it's me"* as the phone self-identification at
the `it` entry; *"this is X"* is the formal/neutral twin.

| Chunk | Register | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **Hi, it's X.** | Casual, the default | The #1 phone opener. *it's*, not *I am*. Pinned example. |
| **Hello, this is X.** | Neutral ‚Üí formal | Use with clients, strangers, reception. |
| **Am I speaking to‚Ä¶?** | Neutral check | Confirms *who answered* before you continue. |
| **Am I through to‚Ä¶?** | UK, neutral | "Am I connected to the right line/extension?" |
| **I'd like to speak to‚Ä¶, please.** | Formal request | The canonical ask to reach someone. |
| **Could I speak to‚Ä¶?** | Very polite | Softens the request further. |

> From `phone_video_corpus.md`:
>
> - **Hi, it's X.** /ha…™ …™ts/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `it` documents *"it's me"* as phone ID.
> - **I'd like to speak to‚Ä¶, please.** /a…™d la…™k t…ô spiňźk t…ô/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge
>   `speak to somebody` + `I'd like to` (C1 polite request).
> - **Am I through to‚Ä¶?** /…ôm a…™ őłruňź tuňź/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `through` phone sense:
>   *"I'll put you through (= connect you by phone)."*

**The Vietnamese trap:** *"t√īi l√† X"* ‚Üí **"I am X"**. On a call this sounds
stilted and wrong ‚ÄĒ like announcing yourself to a room, not identifying to a
caller. Drill the *it's / this is* swap until it is automatic. (The reverse trap
exists too: face-to-face you *do* say *"I'm X"* when introducing yourself ‚ÄĒ see
ūüĒó [GREETINGS & INTROS](./GREETINGS_INTROS.md). The register is channel-specific.)

## 3. Phone transfer & hold ‚ÄĒ "I'll put you through"

When the caller has reached the wrong person, you **transfer** or ask them to
**hold**. Cambridge (B1) and Oxford (C1) both gloss `put through` under the
*Phones* topic with the verbatim example *"Could you put me through to customer
services, please?"* Oxford attests `hold the line` verbatim: *"She asked me to
hold the line."*

| Chunk | When to use it | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **I'll put you through.** | You will connect the caller to the right person | The transfer chunk. Cambridge B1, pinned. |
| **Hold the line, please.** | Ask the caller to wait and not hang up | Oxford b2, verbatim attested. |
| **Just a moment, please.** | Brief wait before a transfer | Pairs with "I'll put you through." |
| **Bear with me.** | Polite patience while you sort something out | Oxford: *"If you will bear with me a little longer‚Ä¶"* |
| **Can you hold? / Please hold.** | Ask permission / instruct to wait | Oxford: *"That extension is busy right now. Can you hold?"* |

> From `phone_video_corpus.md`:
>
> - **I'll put you through.** /a…™l p ät juňź őłruňź/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `put someone through`
>   B1: "to connect a person using a phone."
> - **Hold the line, please.** /h…ô äld √į…ô la…™n/‚Äď/ho äld √į…ô la…™n/ ‚ÄĒ Oxford `hold`
>   "on phone": *"hold the line ‚ÄĒ She asked me to hold the line."*
> - **Bear with me.** /be…ôr w…™√į miňź/‚Äď/ber w…™√į miňź/ ‚ÄĒ Oxford `bear with`:
>   "to be patient with somebody/something."

**The Vietnamese trap:** the direct translation of *"ńĎŠĽ£i mŠĽôt ch√ļt"* is *"wait a
moment"* ‚ÄĒ said bare to a caller it sounds like a command. Wrap it: *"Just a
moment, please"* or *"Bear with me"* softens it to the native register.

## 4. Video-call tech check ‚ÄĒ "can you hear/see me?"

A video call opens with a **tech check** before any business. This is the beat
Vietnamese learners freeze on most, because the chunks are call-specific and
absent from general ESL material. Cambridge documents `mute` (adjective /mjuňźt/)
for the microphone-off state ‚ÄĒ the source of *"you're on mute"*.

| Chunk | When to use it | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **Can you hear me?** | Open every video call; confirm your mic works | The #1 video opener. |
| **Can you see me?** | Confirm your camera works | Usually paired with "hear me?" |
| **You're on mute.** | Tell someone their mic is off | The most-used video phrase in English. |
| **I think you're muted.** | Softer version of "on mute" | The *hedged* diagnostic (see ūüĒó [OPINIONS HEDGED](./OPINIONS_HEDGED.md)). |

> From `phone_video_corpus.md`:
>
> - **Can you hear me?** /k…ôn juňź h…™…ôr miňź/‚Äď/h…™r miňź/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `hear` + `can`
>   weak /k…ôn/.
> - **You're on mute.** /j…Ēňźr …ín mjuňźt/‚Äď/j är …Ďňźn mjuňźt/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `mute` adj
>   /mjuňźt/ + `on` /…ín/‚Äď/…Ďňźn/.

**The Vietnamese trap:** learners often **stay silent** when they cannot hear
someone, or type *"I can't hear"* in the chat instead of saying it. The native
move is to **say the diagnostic aloud**: *"You're on mute"*, *"I can't hear
you"*, *"Can you hear me now?"*. Narrating the problem keeps the call moving.

## 5. Video-call connection problems ‚ÄĒ "you're frozen / breaking up / lost"

When the connection fails, **narrate it**. Cambridge documents both relevant
senses: `frozen` ("not moving at all" ‚ÄĒ Immobility) for a stuck video frame, and
`break up` ("STOP BEING HEARD: If someone who is talking on a mobile phone is
breaking up, their voice can no longer be heard clearly") for degrading audio.

| Chunk | When to use it | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **You're frozen.** | The other person's video has stopped moving | `frozen` literal Immobility sense. |
| **You're breaking up.** | Their audio is becoming impossible to hear | Cambridge `break up` phone sense. |
| **You're cutting out.** | Their audio keeps dropping in and out | `cut out` = "stop working." |
| **I think we lost him/her/them.** | A participant's connection dropped entirely | Oxford `lose` "not hear" sense. **Pinned.** |
| **Can you hear me now?** | After a fix ‚ÄĒ has audio returned? | The reconnect check. |
| **You're back.** | Confirming their audio/video returned | The all-clear. |

> From `phone_video_corpus.md`:
>
> - **You're frozen.** /j…Ēňźr ňąfr…ô ä.z…ôn/‚Äď/j är ňąfro ä.z…ôn/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `frozen`
>   "not moving at all."
> - **You're breaking up.** /j…Ēňźr ňąbre…™.k…™Ňč  Ćp/ ‚ÄĒ Cambridge `break up` "STOP
>   BEING HEARD."
> - **I think we lost them.** /a…™ őł…™Ňčk wiňź l…íst √į…ôm/‚Äď/l…Ďňźst √į…ôm/ ‚ÄĒ Oxford `lose`
>   "not understand/hear": *"His words were lost (= could not be heard)."*

**The Vietnamese trap:** *"I think we lost them"* is **idiomatic** ‚ÄĒ it does
*not* mean the people are physically lost. It means the *connection* dropped and
we can no longer hear them. Translating it back to Vietnamese word-for-word
(*"t√īi nghń© ch√ļng ta mŠļ•t hŠĽć"*) makes no sense; you have to learn it as a chunk.
ūüĒó This is the same chunk-not-word discipline drilled in every Phase 1 bundle.

## 6. Screen sharing ‚ÄĒ "let me share my screen"

The other video-call-specific function. *"Can everyone see my screen?"* is the
canonical Zoom/Teams/Meet prompt after you start sharing.

| Chunk | When to use it | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **Let me share my screen. / I'll share my screen.** | Announce you are starting a share | Cambridge `share` + `screen`. |
| **Can everyone see my screen? / Can everyone see this?** | Confirm the share is visible to all | The standard post-share check. |
| **Let me stop sharing.** | End your share | Returns focus to people/gallery. |

## 7. Closings on a call ‚ÄĒ "I'll let you go"

A call close reuses the **two-step close** (signal ‚Üí farewell) drilled in
ūüĒó [CLOSINGS](./CLOSINGS.md) (bundle #22), with call-specific framing. The
pre-closing *"I'll let you go"* releases the person from the line; the
leave-taking *"Talk soon"* ends it.

| Chunk | Register | Pragmatic note |
|---|---|---|
| **Well, I'll let you go.** | Pre-closing, all-register | Releases the caller politely. |
| **Thanks for the call.** | Appreciation | The call-specific thank-you. |
| **Talk soon.** | Leave-taking, casual | Hints at next contact. |

## 8. Pronunciation & delivery notes

- **Final consonants carry the beat.** *"frozen"* /ňąfr…ô ä.z…ôn/ drops its /n/ and
  the listener hears *"froze"* ‚ÄĒ unfinished. *"lost"* /l…íst/ drops its /t/ and
  becomes *"loss"*. *"through"* /őłruňź/ is the word that makes *"put you
  through"* mean *connect* ‚ÄĒ drop the final consonant or the /őł/ and the call
  fails. ūüĒó [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md).
- **Linking.** *"put you through"* ‚Üí /p ät Éuňź őłruňź/ (the /t/ of *put* glues to
  the /j/ of *you* ‚Üí /t É/); *"can you"* ‚Üí /k…ôn juňź/ often ‚Üí /k…ônt Éuňź/. ūüĒó
  [LINKING](../pronunciation/LINKING.md).
- **Weak forms.** *"can"* ‚Üí /k…ôn/ (not /k√¶n/) in *"Can you hear me?"*; *"to"* ‚Üí
  /t…ô/ in *"I'd like to speak to"*. Strong forms sound robotic. ūüĒó
  [SENTENCE STRESS](../pronunciation/SENTENCE_STRESS.md).
- **Intonation.** *"Can you hear me?"* rises ‚Üó (a real yes/no check); *"You're
  on mute."* falls ‚Üė (a statement of fact, not a question); *"I think we lost
  them."* falls ‚Üė with a slight sympathetic dip. A rising *"you're on mute?"*
  sounds unsure ‚ÄĒ say it as a calm statement. ūüĒó [INTONATION](../pronunciation/INTONATION.md).
- **/őł/ in *through*.** Tongue-between-teeth ‚ÄĒ do not substitute /t/ or /s/.
  *"put you through"* with a /t/ ‚Üí *"put you truu"* is unintelligible. ūüĒó
  [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md).

## 10. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits on phone and video calls ‚ÄĒ extend, don't replace, the seed rows
from the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Says "I am X" on a phone** (word-for-word *"t√īi l√† X"*) | Say **"Hi, it's X"** (casual) or **"Hello, this is X"** (formal). *it's / this is* ‚ÄĒ never *I am*. Cambridge documents *"it's me"* as phone ID. |
| **Opens with "al√ī"** (Vietnamese phone greeting, from French *all√ī*) | English **"Hello?"** (rising, on answering) or **"Hi, it's X"** (on calling). *al√ī* is not used in English; *hello?* with a rise = "who's this?" |
| **Asks "Who is this?" bluntly** (translating *"ai ńĎŠļ•y?"*) | Soften: **"Who's calling, please?"** or **"May I ask who's calling?"** Bare *"who is this?"* sounds suspicious/rude. |
| **Freezes in silence when tech fails** instead of narrating | **Say the diagnostic aloud**: *"You're on mute"*, *"You're frozen"*, *"I think we lost them"*. Narrating keeps the call moving; silence stalls it. |
| **Unfamiliar with video-call tech vocabulary** (mute / frozen / lag / breaking up / share screen) | Drill the 8 survival chunks in ¬ß9. These are call-specific and absent from general ESL ‚ÄĒ learn them as fixed chunks. |
| **Translates "mŠļ•t kŠļŅt nŠĽĎi" literally** ‚Üí "we lost connection" (ok but stiff) | The native chunk is **"I think we lost them"** ‚ÄĒ idiomatic; *them* = the dropped caller(s), not physical loss. |
| **Says "wait a moment" bare** to a caller (sounds like a command) | Wrap it: **"Just a moment, please"** or **"Bear with me"** ‚ÄĒ the polite hold register. |
| **Drops final consonants** ‚Üí *"frozen"* ‚Üí "froze", *"lost"* ‚Üí "loss", *"through"* ‚Üí "thruu" | Release every final: /ňąfr…ô ä.z…ôn/, /l…íst/, /őłruňź/. The final carries the meaning on a call. ūüĒó [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md). |
| **/őł/ in "through" ‚Üí /t/** ‚Üí *"put you through"* ‚Üí *"put you truu"* | Tongue-between-teeth for /őł/. ūüĒó [TH SOUNDS](../pronunciation/TH_SOUNDS.md). |
| **Strong-forms grammar words** ‚Üí *"CAN you HEAR me?"* (robotic, every word stressed) | Weak forms: *"k…ôn juňź h…™…ôr miňź?"* ‚ÄĒ only *hear* and *me* carry stress. ūüĒó [SENTENCE STRESS](../pronunciation/SENTENCE_STRESS.md). |
| **Says "I will go now" to end a call** (word-for-word *"t√īi ńĎi ńĎ√Ęy"*) | Use the call pre-closing: **"Well, I'll let you go"** + **"Talk soon."** ūüĒó [CLOSINGS](./CLOSINGS.md). |
| **Rising intonation on "you're on mute?"** (sounds unsure) | Say it as a calm **statement** (‚Üė): *"You're on mute."* It is information, not a question. ūüĒó [INTONATION](../pronunciation/INTONATION.md). |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚ÄĒ this guide, ¬ß1‚Äď¬ß5. Internalize the **four beats** and the
   *it's / this is* (not *I am*) rule.
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) ‚ÄĒ open `phone_video.html`, drill the 8 flip cards + the
   role-play **aloud**. Exaggerate the tech-check questions (rising) and the
   diagnostics (falling, calm).
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) ‚ÄĒ the writing task: write **a video-call opening + a
   tech-issue line** (e.g. *"Can everyone see my screen?"* / *"You're on
   mute."*). Read it aloud; check each final consonant is audible and each
   question rises.
