6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Perfectionism / fear-of-error (sợ sai) → on noticing your own slip you FREEZE, go silent, and the conversation stalls.Self-correction is normal native behavior. Catch → drop one marker ("Sorry, what I meant was…") → fix → keep going. Aim for under a second.
Over-apologize — "sorry, sorry, sorry, my English is bad" — stacking three or four sorrys.Exactly one acknowledgement word, then the fix. "Sorry, I misspoke — Tuesday, not Wednesday." The apology pile is the error; the slip is not.
Switches to Vietnamese or gives up ("ôi nói sai rồi, thôi để sau") the moment a slip happens.Stay in English and finish the notice → mark → fix → continue cycle in one breath. The flow is fluency.
Doesn't realize natives self-correct constantly → treats every slip as evidence of broken English.Natives self-repair every few sentences. "I mean…", "or rather…", "actually…" are native habits. Reach for Or rather… — it sounds fully native.
Drops final consonants on the FIX so the corrected version is misunderstood again — "men" for meant, "ova" for over.Re-release every final on the fix: meant /ment/, over /ˈəʊvə/, misspoke /ˌmɪsˈspəʊk/.
No stress on the marker → "or rather" comes out flat, so the listener misses the correction signal.Stress the marker's content word (RAther, MEANT, start OVer) and weaken the glue (let me → /lemi/).
Narrates the failure — "oh no, that's wrong, I said it wrong, my brain stopped, haha…" — longer than the slip itself.Skip the narration. The native norm is marker + fix. "Wait, let me start over —" then the fresh sentence. No commentary on the error.
PANIC-restarts the whole answer from zero when only one word was wrong.Use a targeted fix ("what I meant was…") for one slip; reserve the full restart ("let me start over") for a genuinely tangled sentence.
