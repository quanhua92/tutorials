Service Mesh (Istio / Envoy) — interactive 📖 SERVICE_MESH.md 📄 service_mesh.py

📖 Read the full guide — diagrams, decision tables, common pitfalls, and a cheat sheet. This page is the interactive companion.
A sidecar proxy (Envoy) rides every pod. The control plane (Istiod) programs them; the data plane (the Envoys) carries all traffic and gives you mTLS, traffic splitting, retries/circuit-breaking, and the golden signals — with zero app code changes. Toggle the mesh on/off and watch traffic splitting, resilience, and observability recompute live from the same deterministic model as service_mesh.py.

① Sidecar injection — every pod gets an Envoy, traffic is hijacked transparently

What to do: toggle the mesh. An init container writes iptables rules so ALL traffic flows through Envoy (15006 inbound, 15001 outbound). The app container is unchanged — it still listens on :8080 and sees plaintext on loopback.

② mTLS — Envoy-to-Envoy mutual TLS with SPIFFE identity (auto-rotated)

What this shows: both Envoys present a short-lived cert whose SAN is a SPIFFE ID. The app on each end sees only plaintext on 127.0.0.1. Toggling mTLS off shows the plaintext wire the app would otherwise be exposed to.

④ Resilience — retries + outlier detection hide failures; circuit breaker caps overflow

pod-a returns 503 x5 then heals; pod-b is healthy. retry=3 attempts · eject after 5 consecutive 5xx. Step through and compare WITH vs WITHOUT the mesh.

Circuit breaking (per-cluster overflow): maxConnections=100. A burst of 105 concurrent requests → Envoy forwards 100, rejects 5 locally (503 UPSTREAM_OVERFLOW).
