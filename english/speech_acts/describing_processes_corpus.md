## A. The sequencers (the spine of any process)

These are the **ordering adverbs and chunks** that mark each step of a sequence.
Pragmatically they are **sequencing discourse markers** — Cambridge Grammar
(*English Grammar Today*) treats `first`, `then`, `next`, `finally`, `lastly`
together as the "ordering the points we want to make" set. A spoken how-to
without them becomes a blur of equally-weighted clauses the listener cannot
parse; with them, even a five-step recipe is effortless to follow.

> Grammar Today*) entry: "We often use *first*, especially in writing, to show

> `After that…` uses `after` /ˈɑːftə/ UK · /ˈæftər/ US + `that` /ðæt/ strong
> (Cambridge); the weak form `that` /ðət/ is also documented.

## B. The instruction chunks (the "be careful / make sure" add-ons)

A sequencer only marks *order*. These chunks add **care, warning, or
precondition** to a step — they are what separate a usable how-to from a broken
one. Cambridge documents `make sure` as an idiom ("to take special care to do
something"); `once` as a time conjunction ("as soon as / when"); and the
imperative (`Make sure to…`, `Be careful not to…`) as the default instruction
mood (Cambridge Grammar: *Imperative clauses*). They are the highest-frequency
chunks in spoken instructions in the COCA spoken sub-corpus.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Make sure to… | take special care to do this step | /ˌmeɪk ˈʃʊə(r) tə/ UK · /ˌmeɪk ˈʃʊr tə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make-sure | very high-freq instruction | US/UK |
| Be careful not to… | warn the listener to avoid a common mistake | /bi ˈkeəfəl nɒt tə/ UK · /bi ˈkerfəl nɑːt tə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/careful | high-freq warning | US/UK |
| Once you've done that… | after you finish the previous step | /wʌns juːv dʌn ðæt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/once | high-freq sequencer | US/UK |
| The next step is… | name the following step formally | /ðə nekst step ɪz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/step | formal/written how-to | US/UK |
| You'll need to… | list what the listener must do / gather | /juːl niːd tə/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/need | high-freq setup | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `Make sure to…` is the verbatim Cambridge idiom entry
> `make sure` ("to take special care to do something") with the attested examples
> "**Make sure** you're home by midnight." and "**Make sure to** tell them I said
> 'hi.'" `Be careful not to…` uses `careful` /ˈkeəfəl/ UK · /ˈkerfəl/ US
> (Cambridge) + `not to` /nɒt tə/ UK · /nɑːt tə/ US. `Once you've done that…`
> uses `once` /wʌns/ (Cambridge conjunction sense "as soon as / when") + the
> contraction `you've` /juːv/ + `done that` /dʌn ðæt/. `The next step is…` and
> `You'll need to…` use the standard Cambridge forms of `next` /nekst/, `step`
> /step/, `need` /niːd/, with the weak form `to` /tə/ and `the` /ðə/. The
> imperative mood for instructions is documented in Cambridge Grammar
> (*Imperative clauses (Be quiet!)*).

## C. Spoken how-to vs written how-to (register contrast)

The same sequence is delivered differently when **spoken** (app explanation,
cooking demo, casual help) vs **written** (a recipe card, a manual, a formal
procedure). Spoken how-to leans on **short imperatives + light sequencers**
(`First you…, then…`); written how-to leans on **fuller noun phrases + formal
sequencers** (`The first step is…; Subsequently…`). Learners who use only the
written register in conversation sound stiff ("one must first…"); learners who
use only the spoken register in a manual sound sloppy.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| First, you… (spoken) | informal step opening, addressee = "you" | /fɜːst juː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/first-firstly-or-at-first | spoken-default | US/UK |
| The first step is… (written) | formal step opening, names the step | /ðə fɜːst step ɪz/ UK · /ðə fɜːrst step ɪz/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/step | written how-to | US/UK |
| Lastly… (list end) | the final item in a written list | /ˈlɑːstli/ UK · /ˈlæstli/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/finally-at-last-lastly-or-in-the-end | written how-to | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the spoken `First, you…` (imperative + 2nd-person
> addressee) vs written `The first step is…` (noun-phrase subject) contrast is
> the core register split documented in Cambridge Grammar (*First, firstly or at
> first* — "We often use *first*, especially in writing, to show the order of the
> points we want to make"). `Lastly…` is the Cambridge Grammar attested
> list-ending form ("We use *lastly* to refer to something that comes at the end
> of a long list"). IPA built from the verified word forms above.
