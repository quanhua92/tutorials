## A. Assign the task (polite ownership — the delegation opener)

The professional-English delegation opener is a **polite modal question or a
softened statement that hands over ownership** — never a bare imperative. Oxford
documents `delegate` (verb) /ˈdelɪɡeɪt/ as "to give part of your work, power or
authority to somebody in a lower position than you" with the example *"Some
managers are reluctant to delegate work to their subordinates."* Cambridge
documents `handle` (verb, DEAL WITH sense) as "to deal with, have responsibility
for, or be in charge of" — *"Who handles the marketing in your company?"* — the
exact ownership-assignment verb. `take the lead on` is the canonical "own this
workstream" phrase, attested across management and ESL sources (e.g. the
Vietnamese-learner TikTok @englishonlinewithnice teaching the chunk: *"Maria,
can you take the lead on the client presentation next week?"*). These are
**ownership anchors** — the opposite of the top-down imperative ("You must do
this") that Vietnamese hierarchy makes sound natural but that English reads as
authoritarian.

## B. Clarify scope + deadline (the accountability core)

The engine of English delegation: name the **goal**, name the **scope of
responsibility**, and pin a **deadline** — three things a Vietnamese learner
often omits (assuming they are "understood"). Cambridge documents `be
responsible for` verbatim as the delegation phrase: *"He is responsible for the
council's waste management department."* Cambridge Business English documents
`deadline` UK /ˈded.laɪn/ · US /ˈded.laɪn/ with the collocations `set/give a
deadline`, `a deadline of [date]`, `tight deadline`. The frame `The goal is
to…` is the canonical scope-setter (attested across goal-setting and
OKR-management sources); `…by Friday` is the deadline-pinning prepositional
phrase. These are **accountability anchors** — the opposite of the vague
"please help with the report" that leaves the recipient guessing.

## C. Offer support (the scaffolding — never just dump and disappear)

The "support" move: after assigning scope and deadline, the English delegator
**explicitly offers help** — never dumps the task and disappears. This is the
move that separates good delegation from abdication, and it is the move
Vietnamese learners most often omit (assuming the offer is implicit).
`Let me know if you hit any roadblocks` is the canonical support offer
(Cambridge `roadblock` figurative + `let me know`). `What do you need from
me?` is the resource-check question (Cambridge `need` + `from`). Cambridge
documents `check in` (phrasal verb, CONTACT sense) UK /tʃek/ · US /tʃek/ —
*"to contact someone by making a phone call, short visit, etc., usually in
order to make sure there are no problems"* — with the collocation `check in
with`: *"My son checks in regularly with me when he's travelling."* The frame
`Let's check in on this on…` schedules a follow-up, closing the accountability
loop.

**Pragmatics / politeness theory (the "why"):**
- Brown, P. & Levinson, S. *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage*
  (CUP, 1987) — face-threatening acts (FTAs); a direct command ("Do X by
  Friday") threatens the hearer's negative face (freedom from imposition),
  which the modal question `Could you…?` and the ownership frame `I'd like
  you to own…` redress. Cultures with high power-distance (Hofstede's index:
  Vietnam scores high) default to imperative delegation; English professional
  norms redress it.

  but reads as authoritarian in low power-distance English workplaces).
