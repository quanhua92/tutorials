6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
States opinions as bald fact — "This is the best option" (no modal, no hedge) — sounds aggressivePrepend a hedge: "I'd say this is the best option." The hedge marks it as your view, not a decree.
Drops the hedge L1 would carry via particles — Vietnamese uses theo tôi optionally and silentlyTreat the hedge as part of the chunk. Drill "I'd say …", "It seems to me …" as fixed openers.
Overuses "I think" on every sentence — loses force, sounds hesitant not opinionatedCycle the ladder: I'd say (confident), It seems to me (soft), As far as I can tell (scoped). One I think per three opinions, max.
Direct "You should…" / "You must…" without softening — bare English modal reads as an orderSwap for a hedged view: "I'd say you might want to …"
"Correct me if I'm wrong" used literally — pragmatically it's an assertion with a shield, not a real request for correctionUse it to state a strong view politely. For real uncertainty use "I might be wrong, but…" (honest).
Drops the final consonant on the hedge word — "I thin" for think, "wron" for wrong → hedge inaudible, politeness failsRelease every final: /θɪŋk/, /rɒŋ/. The politeness lives in the audible hedge.
Mis-stresses multi-word hedges — "in my o-PIN-ion" — because Vietnamese is syllable-timed (every syllable equal)Stress the content word: I'd SAY, It SEEMS to me, As FAR as I KNOW. Content strong, grammar weak.
Translates "theo tôi" as "according to me" — sounds wrong/childish to nativesUse "In my opinion, …" / "If you ask me, …". Reserve according to for citing others.
No rise-fall intonation on the opinion — flat tone reads as a statement of factEnd a hedged opinion with a slight fall (settled) or rise (inviting response) — never flat.
