## A. What a discourse marker IS (and why function beats word)

A discourse marker is a word or short phrase that **does not add propositional
content** — it tells the hearer *how to take* what comes next. `well`, `so`,
`I mean`, `you know`, `right`, `actually`, `basically`, `look` are NOT being
used in their dictionary meaning (good / therefore / I signify / you possess
knowledge / correct / in fact / fundamentally / use your eyes). They are doing
**interactional work**: opening a turn, repairing your own speech, checking the
hearer is following, softening a disagreement, summing up.

Schiffrin (1987) and Fraser (1999) call these **procedural** (or pragmatic)
markers: they instruct the hearer on how to process the utterance, not on what
it means. The single biggest Vietnamese-L1 error is treating them as
**translatable words** and either dropping them (sounds robotic / abrupt) or
over-using one (typically *so*) in every slot. The fix is to learn the
**FUNCTION**, not the word.

| Function family | Core markers | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **Turn / response shift** | well · so · (oh) | opens a response; buys a beat; shifts to a new turn |
| **Result / inference / start** | so · (then) | "therefore"; inference; opens a new part of the talk |
| **Self-repair / clarification** | I mean · (or rather) | corrects or re-expands what you just said |
| **Shared-knowledge appeal** | you know · (you see) | assumes the hearer follows; also a filler |
| **Agreement check** | right · (okay) | checks the hearer agrees / has understood |
| **Contrast / surprise / correction** | actually · (in fact) | signals what follows contrasts with expectation |
| **Summarizing / bottom-line** | basically · (essentially) | "in short"; states the gist |
| **Sincerity preface** | to be honest · (honestly) | flags "here's what I really think" |
| **Direct attention** | look · (see) | "pay attention to what I'm about to say" |
| **Acknowledge understanding** | I see (what you mean) | "I've understood you" |

> **Verification note:** the function taxonomy above follows Schiffrin, D.
> (1987), *Discourse Markers* (CUP), the founding study that lists *well, so,
> I mean, you know, oh, and, but, or* as the core set; Fraser, B. (1999), "What
> are discourse markers?", *Journal of Pragmatics* 31, which defines them as
> "sequentially dependent elements that bracket units of talk"; and the
> Cambridge Grammar entry
> `dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-markers-so-right-okay`,
> which groups *so, right, okay* and glosses them as "marking a new part of the
> conversation."

## B. Response & turn-shift markers — PINNED "well"

`well` is the **archetypal discourse marker** — Schiffrin (1987) opens her book
with it. As an *exclamation* (Oxford *well* exclamation, sense 4–9), it has at
least six pragmatic functions: continuing after a break, signalling uncertainty,
pausing to consider, accepting the unavoidable, unwilling agreement, and
self-correcting ("There were thousands of people there — well, hundreds,
anyway."). Its core job for a learner: **it opens a response** when a straight
"yes/no" would be too blunt, buying a beat to frame the answer.

| so | used to introduce a comment, question, result, or next part of the talk (conjunction, discourse use) | /səʊ/ UK · /soʊ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/so_3 | ≈#15 (spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `well` (continuing after a break): "**Well**, as I was saying…" (Oxford,
>   *well* exclamation, sense 4).
> - `well` (uncertainty): "'Do you want to come?' '**Well**, I'm not sure.'"
>   (Oxford, *well* exclamation, sense 5).
> - `well` (pausing to consider next words): "I think it happened, **well**,
>   towards the end of last summer." (Oxford, *well* exclamation, sense 8).
> - `well` (self-correction): "There were thousands of people there — **well**,
>   hundreds, anyway." (Oxford, *well* exclamation, sense 9).
> - `so` (introduce a comment / question): "**So**, let's see. What do we need
>   to take?" / "**So**, what have you been doing today?" (Oxford, *so*
>   conjunction, sense 4).
> - `so` (introduce the next part of a story): "So after shouting and screaming
>   for an hour she walked out in tears." (Oxford, *so* conjunction, sense 5).
> - `so` (final statement / summary): "**So**, that's it for today." (Oxford,
>   *so* conjunction, sense 7).

> **Verification note:** `well` /wel/ confirmed in Oxford *well* exclamation
> (identical to Oxford *well* adverb /wel/ — one phonological form, many
> pragmatic senses). `so` /səʊ/ UK · /soʊ/ US confirmed in Oxford *so*

> and Schiffrin (1987).

## C. Self-repair & clarification markers — PINNED "I mean"

`I mean` is the **self-repair** marker par excellence. Cambridge Dictionary
glosses it as "used to correct what you have just said" (cited via the Unipd
thesis *Formulaic Language in Spoken English*, which quotes the Cambridge entry:
*"I really do love him, as a friend, I mean"*). Schiffrin (1987) calls it the
marker of the "information state" — it signals the speaker is re-expanding or
modifying their own utterance. A related job: introducing an **explanation or
clarification** of the preceding claim.

`you know` does a different job: it **appeals to shared knowledge** ("you and I
both know this") or acts as a **filler** while the speaker thinks. Oxford
(*know*, idioms) lists three senses: thinking-of-what-to-say-next, referring to
something the hearer knows, and emphasizing a statement.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I mean | used to correct, clarify, or re-expand what you have just said | /aɪ ˈmiːn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/i-mean | ≈#60 (phrase, spoken) | US/UK |
| you know | (1) filler while thinking; (2) appeal to shared knowledge; (3) emphasis | /juː ˈnəʊ/ UK · /juː ˈnoʊ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/know_1 | ≈#25 (phrase, spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `I mean` (self-correction): "I really do love him, as a friend, **I mean**."
>   (Cambridge Dictionary, *I mean*, cited via Gobbato, *Formulaic Language in
>   Spoken English*, Unipd 2023).
> - `you know` (thinking / filler): "Well, **you know**, it's difficult to
>   explain." (Oxford, *know*, idioms → "you know" sense 1).
> - `you know` (shared-knowledge appeal): "Guess who I've just seen? Maggie!
>   **You know** — Jim's wife." / "**You know** that restaurant round the
>   corner? It's closed down." (Oxford, *know*, idioms → "you know" sense 2).
> - `you know` (emphasis): "I'm not stupid, **you know**." (Oxford, *know*,
>   idioms → "you know" sense 3).

> **Verification note:** `I mean` /aɪ ˈmiːn/ — `I` /aɪ/ and `mean` /miːn/ are
> the standard Oxford transcriptions (*I*, *mean* verb); the self-repair
> function is the Cambridge Dictionary *I mean* gloss, corroborated by Schiffrin
> (1987, ch. "I mean") and the Cambridge *Pragmatic Markers in British English*
> chapter on *I mean* (cambridge.org/core/books/pragmatic-markers-in-british-
> english/i-mean/). `you know` /juː ˈnəʊ/–/juː ˈnoʊ/ — `you` /juː/, `know`
> /nəʊ/ UK · /noʊ/ US confirmed in Oxford *know*; the three pragmatic senses are
> the Oxford *know* idioms "you know" block (cross-checked in Cambridge Grammar
> "Discourse markers").

## D. Agreement-check & contrast markers

`right` (as an exclamation) is the **agreement-check** marker. Oxford (*right*
exclamation, sense 3) glosses it: "used to check that somebody agrees with you
or has understood you." It also gets attention ("Right! Let's get going.") and
accepts a statement ("'Barry's here.' 'Oh, right.'"). Sense 4 is the ironic
"Yeah, right" (= I don't believe you).

`actually` is the **contrast / surprise** marker — it signals that what follows
is *not* what the hearer expects. Oxford (*actually*) lists four senses:
emphasize a fact, show contrast/surprise, **correct somebody politely**
("We're not American, actually. We're Canadian."), and introduce a new topic /
something the hearer may not like ("Actually, I'll be a bit late home.").

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| right | used to check agreement / get attention / accept a statement (exclamation) | /raɪt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/right_5 | ≈#50 (spoken) | US/UK |
| actually | used to show contrast / surprise / polite correction / introduce a point | /ˈæktʃuəli/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/actually | ≈#120 (spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `right` (check agreement): "So that's twenty of each sort, **right**?" /
>   "And I didn't think any more of it, **right**, but Mum says I should see a
>   doctor." (Oxford, *right* exclamation, sense 3).
> - `right` (get attention): "**Right!** Let's get going." (Oxford, *right*
>   exclamation, sense 2).
> - `right` (accept a statement): "'Barry's here.' 'Oh, **right**.'" (Oxford,
>   *right* exclamation, sense 1).
> - `actually` (polite correction): "We're not American, **actually**. We're
>   Canadian." / "They're not married, **actually**." (Oxford, *actually*,
>   sense 3).
> - `actually` (contrast / surprise): "It was **actually** kind of fun after
>   all." / "Our turnover **actually** increased last year." (Oxford,
>   *actually*, sense 2).
> - `actually` (introduce a new topic / something the hearer may not like):
>   "**Actually**, I'll be a bit late home." / "**Actually**, I'm busy at the
>   moment — can I call you back?" (Oxford, *actually*, sense 4).

> **Verification note:** `right` /raɪt/ confirmed in Oxford *right* exclamation
> (identical to Oxford *right* adverb / adjective /raɪt/). `actually`
> /ˈæktʃuəli/ confirmed in Oxford *actually* (single transcription, no US/UK
> split — both varieties use /ˈæktʃuəli/). Both cross-checked against the
> Cambridge Grammar "Discourse markers (so, right, okay)" page.

## E. Summarizing & sincerity markers

`basically` is the **bottom-line / summarizing** marker. Oxford (*basically*)
glosses it "in the most important ways, without considering things that are less
important" (synonym *essentially*), and gives a second sense "used when you are
giving your opinion or stating what is important about a situation" — exactly
the summarizing move.

`to be honest` (and its abbreviation **TBH**) is the **sincerity preface** — it
flags "here's what I really think." Oxford (*honest*) attests it in the
"not hiding the truth" sense: "To be honest (= what I really think is), it was
one of the worst books I've ever read." Oxford also gives *TBH* its own entry
(informal, text/social-media).

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| basically | used to summarize / state the gist / give your opinion | /ˈbeɪsɪkli/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/basically | ≈#400 (spoken) | US/UK |
| to be honest | used to flag "here is what I really think" (sincerity preface) | /tə biː ˈɒnɪst/ UK · /tə biː ˈɑːnɪst/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/honest | ≈#700 (phrase, spoken) | US/UK |
| TBH | abbreviation for "to be honest" (informal, text / social media) | /ˌtiː biː ˈeɪtʃ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/tbh | n/a (abbreviation) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `basically` (summarize): "There have been some problems but **basically**
>   it's a good system." / "Yes, that's **basically** correct." (Oxford,
>   *basically*, sense 1).
> - `basically` (opinion / bottom-line): "**Basically**, there's not a lot we
>   can do about it." / "He **basically** just sits there and does nothing all
>   day." (Oxford, *basically*, sense 2).
> - `to be honest`: "To be honest (= what I really think is), it was one of the
>   worst books I've ever read." / "To be perfectly honest, this was the worst
>   film I've ever seen." (Oxford, *honest*, sense 2).
> - `TBH`: "I don't know anything about it, **TBH**." (Oxford, *TBH*
>   abbreviation).

> **Verification note:** `basically` /ˈbeɪsɪkli/ confirmed in Oxford *basically*
> (single transcription, both varieties). `to be honest` /tə biː ˈɒnɪst/ UK ·
> /tə biː ˈɑːnɪst/ US — `to` /tə/ (weak form), `be` /biː/, `honest` /ˈɒnɪst/
> UK · /ˈɑːnɪst/ US confirmed in Oxford *honest* (note the silent *h* — Oxford
> flags "Use **an**, not **a**, before **honest**"). `TBH` /ˌtiː biː ˈeɪtʃ/
> confirmed in Oxford *TBH*.

## F. Direct-attention & understanding markers

`look` (as an exclamation) is the **direct-attention** marker. Oxford (*look*
exclamation) glosses it: "used to make somebody pay attention to what you are
going to say, often when you are annoyed." It's the spoken-English way to
foreground your next point without sounding like a textbook ("I want to draw
your attention to…").

`I see what you mean` is the **acknowledge-understanding** move — it tells the
hearer you've followed them. Oxford (*see*) attests it in the "understand"
sense: "Oh yes, **I see what you mean**." Merriam-Webster (*see*) lists it under
"to understand the meaning or importance of."

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| look | used to make somebody pay attention to what you are going to say (exclamation) | /lʊk/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/look_3 | ≈#100 (spoken) | US/UK |
| I see what you mean | used to show you have understood somebody (acknowledge understanding) | /aɪ ˈsiː wɒt juː ˈmiːn/ UK · /aɪ ˈsiː wɑːt juː ˈmiːn/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/see_1 | ≈#1500 (phrase, spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `look` (direct attention): "**Look**, I think we should go now." / "**Look**,
>   that's not fair." (Oxford, *look* exclamation).
> - `I see what you mean`: "Oh yes, **I see what you mean**." (Oxford, *see*,
>   "understand" sense — synonym note for *understand* / *see* / *get*).
> - Merriam-Webster (*see*): "**I see what you mean**" glossed under "to
>   understand the meaning or importance of" (merriam-webster.com/dictionary/see).

> **Verification note:** `look` /lʊk/ confirmed in Oxford *look* exclamation
> (identical to Oxford *look* verb /lʊk/). `I see what you mean`
> /aɪ ˈsiː wɒt juː ˈmiːn/ UK · /aɪ ˈsiː wɑːt juː ˈmiːn/ US — `I` /aɪ/, `see`
> /siː/, `what` /wɒt/ UK · /wɑːt/ US, `you` /juː/, `mean` /miːn/; the phrase is
> attested in Oxford *see* and Merriam-Webster *see*, and Dictionary.com *I see*
> ("I see what you mean. This idiom uses *see* in the sense of 'perceive' or
> 'comprehend,' a usage dating from 1300").
