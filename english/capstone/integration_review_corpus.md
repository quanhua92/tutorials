## A. Look back — open the self-assessment

The capstone reflection move: pause and **cast your mind over the 180 days** before
you judge what improved. Collins COBUILD (*look back*) defines the phrasal verb:
*"If you look back, you think about things that happened in the past,"* and prints
the attested example *"Looking back, I am staggered how easily it was all
arranged."* Oxford (*look back*) glosses it *"to think about something in the past;
synonym reflect"* — *"to look back on your childhood."* This is the opening frame
of every honest self-assessment: you cannot name what improved until you first
*look back* at where you started.

## B. Name a win — "what I've gotten better at"

After looking back, the self-assessment names **at least one concrete
improvement**. Oxford (*improvement*) defines the noun: *"the act of making
something better; the process of something becoming better,"* and prints the
phrases *"an area for improvement"*, *"room for improvement"*, and *"There is a
need for continuous improvement in performance."* The present-perfect chunk *"One
thing I've gotten better at…"* (US; UK *"One thing I've got better at…"*) names a
specific win using the verb *improve* in the present perfect — the tense English
uses for "a change that happened and still holds now." Cambridge (*get*) lists
*get better* = "to improve"; Oxford (*better*) glosses *"to get better at
something."*

## C. Name a gap — "what I still need to work on"

The honest self-assessment then names **at least one concrete weakness** — not
vaguely ("my English is bad") but as a specific, fixable area. Oxford (*work on*)
defines the relevant sense: *"work on something = to try hard to improve or achieve
something,"* and prints the attested example *"You need to work on your
pronunciation a bit more."* Oxford (*focus*) defines *"focus on"* = "to give a lot
of attention to one particular person, subject, or thing." These two chunks turn a
weakness from a source of shame into a **named target** you can re-drill.

## D. Look forward — set a goal + plan maintenance

The self-assessment closes by **looking forward**: one concrete goal for the next
month, plus a maintenance commitment so fluency does not decay. Oxford (*goal*)
defines the noun: *"something that you hope to achieve; synonym aim,"* and prints
*"You need to set yourself some long-term goals"* / *"Their goal was to eradicate
malaria."* Cambridge (*keep*) glosses *"keep doing something = to continue doing
something,"* and Cambridge (*practice*) prints the attested example *"I need to
**practise** my French."* The maintenance chunk *"I'll keep practicing…"* is the
commitment that beats the forgetting curve — the learner keeps re-touching the
chunks so they move from short-term to *permastore* (Bahrick, 1984).
