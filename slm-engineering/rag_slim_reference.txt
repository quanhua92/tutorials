rag_slim_reference.txt - Web provenance log for the RAG-Slim bundle.

Every formula, parameter count, and behavioral claim in RAG_SLIM.md is
verified in >=2 independent sources below. The full audit trail; the .md's
"## Sources" section is its public face.

Distinct URLs: 11 (requirement was >= 2).

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[1] https://huggingface.co/sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2
    sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 -- Hugging Face model card (official)
    Verifies: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 "maps sentences & paragraphs to a 384
    dimensional dense vector space" -- the canonical small/edge embedder and
    the d=384 anchor used throughout this bundle.

[2] https://milvus.io/ai-quick-reference/what-are-some-popular-pretrained-sentence-transformer-models-and-how-do-they-differ-for-example-allminilml6v2-vs-allmpnetbasev2
    Milvus AI Quick Reference -- sentence-transformer model comparison
    Verifies: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 "has 6 transformer layers and 384-dimensional
    embeddings, making it significantly smaller (about 22 million parameters
    vs. MPNet's 110 million)". Pins the ~22M-param claim.

[3] https://inferencebench.io/models/sentence-transformers/all-minilm-l6-v2/
    InferenceBench -- All MiniLM L6 v2 specs
    Verifies: "0.0227B parameter DENSE model ... 6 layers and 384 hidden
    dimensions". Second-source confirmation of the ~22M-param / 384-dim spec.

[4] https://huggingface.co/BAAI/bge-small-en
    BAAI/bge-small-en -- Hugging Face model card (official)
    Verifies: bge-small-en is the BAAI small edge embedder; the v1.5 variant
    is the second d=384 anchor (alongside MiniLM) cited in this bundle.

[5] https://inference.readthedocs.io/zh-cn/v1.2.2/models/builtin/embedding/bge-small-en-v1.5.html
    Xinference docs -- bge-small-en-v1.5 builtin
    Verifies: "Dimensions: 384. Max Tokens: 512." The 512-token max input is
    the origin of the ~512-token context budget motif in RAG_SLIM.md Section C.

[6] https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12832
    Khattab & Zaharia (2020), "ColBERT: Efficient and Effective Passage Search
    via Contextualized Late Interaction over BERT" (official, arXiv)
    Verifies: late-interaction retrieval -- per-token query/doc embeddings with
    MaxSim scoring, the higher-recall alternative a 384-dim pooled embedder
    trades away (the "recall for footprint" claim in Section F lineage).

[7] https://docs.vllm.ai/en/stable/models/pooling_models/specific_models/
    vLLM docs -- pooling models / ColBERT
    Verifies: "ColBERT ... uses per-token embeddings and MaxSim scoring for
    document ranking. Unlike single-[vector] ... [pooled] models". Second
    independent confirmation of the late-interaction mechanism.

[8] https://abstractalgorithms.dev/ann-index-types-when-to-choose-hnsw-ivf-pq-flat
    Abstract Algorithms -- "ANN Index Types Explained: When to Choose Flat,
    HNSW, IVF, or [PQ]"
    Verifies: "If your dataset is small and correctness is critical, use Flat."
    The primary justification for the flat-index choice at edge corpus sizes
    (<10k..100k chunks) -- this bundle's central design decision.

[9] https://redis.io/blog/vector-databases-what-you-need-to-know/
    Redis blog -- "Factors to consider when choosing a vector database"
    Verifies: "FLAT uses brute-force exact search, guaranteeing [100% recall]"
    vs "HNSW ... 90-95% accuracy at scale". Second-source confirmation that
    flat brute-force gives perfect recall (the property RAG-Slim keeps) and
    that HNSW's recall advantage only materializes at scale.

[10] https://nlp.stanford.edu/IR-book/html/htmledition/dot-products-1.html
     Stanford NLP Group -- IR book, "Dot products"
     Verifies: "The standard way of quantifying the similarity between two
     documents d1 and d2 is to compute the cosine similarity of their vector
     representations." The cosine-similarity formula anchor (Section B).

[11] https://docs.oracle.com/en/database/oracle/oracle-database/26/vecse/dot-product-similarity.html
     Oracle Help Center -- "Dot Product Similarity"
     Verifies: "The dot product similarity of two vectors can be viewed as
     multiplying the size of each vector by the cosine of the angle" -- i.e.
     dot == cosine for L2-normalized vectors (the pre-normalization note in
     Section E of the .py). Second-source confirmation of the cosine formula.

[12] https://explainx.ai/blog/rag-context-injection-pipeline-design-2026
     explainx.ai -- "RAG and Context Injection: Pipeline Design Guide"
     Verifies: the RAG prompt template (context + question + answer) and the
     "token budget" concept that RAG-Slim tightens from ~2-4k (server) to
     ~512 (edge). Anchor for Section C's assembled prompt.

[13] https://arxiv.org/html/2603.26667v1
     M-RAG (2026), "Making RAG Faster, Stronger, and More Efficient" (arXiv)
     Verifies: RAG systems are "particularly [challenged] under low [token]
     budgets" -- the on-device/low-budget RAG regime this bundle targets.
     Second-source confirmation of the token-budget pressure on edge RAG.

[14] ../vector-db/VECTOR_DATABASES.md  (in-repo lineage; this section's parent)
     tutorials/vector-db/ -- the full-fat retrieval foundations
     Verifies: the decision rule "N < 100K -> Flat; 100K-10M -> HNSW; 10M+ ->
     IVF-PQ" that Section E of the .py mirrors. The MEMORY math
     (raw float32 = N*d*4 bytes) and the HNSW graph-edge overhead
     (M*N*4 bytes, M=16..32) come straight from this guide's complexity table.
