## A. Casual openers (the high-frequency social fuel)

These are the greetings that open the overwhelming majority of informal
encounters. They are **chunks** — fixed multi-word strings — not
word-by-word translations. A Vietnamese learner who retrieves "How's it going?"
as one unit sounds native; one who assembles "How + is + it + going?" sounds
like a textbook.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hi | hello (neutral, the default opener) | /haɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/hi | ≈#120 | US/UK |
| Hey | hello (casual, between people who already know each other) | /heɪ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/hey | ≈#150 | US/UK |
| How are you? | standard polite/casual "are you well?" | /ˌhaʊ ɑː ˈjuː/ UK · /ˌhaʊ ɑːr ˈjuː/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/how-are-you | ≈#90 (multiword) | US/UK |
| How's it going? | casual "how are you?" (US workhorse) | /ˌhaʊz ɪt ˈɡəʊɪŋ/ UK · /ˌhaʊz ɪt ˈɡoʊɪŋ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/how-s-it-going | ≈#200 (multiword) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `How's it going?` /ˌhaʊz ɪt ˈɡəʊɪŋ/ (UK) /
> /ˌhaʊz ɪt ˈɡoʊɪŋ/ (US) confirmed in the SpanishDict IPA column
> (https://www.spanishdict.com/pronunciation/how's%20it%20going) and matches
> the compositional IPA from Cambridge headwords *how* /haʊ/, *be* /ɪz/
> (contracted), *it* /ɪt/, *going* /ˈɡəʊɪŋ/–/ˈɡoʊɪŋ/. `What's up?` meaning
> "what is happening? / casual greeting like hi" confirmed in both Collins
> ("what is the matter? / what is happening?", informal) and Wiktionary
> (https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/what's_up — three senses incl. "a casual
> greeting with a meaning similar to *hi*"). `How are things?` is the standard
> Cambridge multiword entry.

## B. Formal openers (first meetings, professional, time-of-day)

The register climbs here. These are the chunks for meeting someone for the
first time, addressing a senior, or opening a professional encounter. Note
`How do you do?` is **formulaic** — it is *not* a real question; the canonical
reply is `How do you do?` (an echo), not "I'm fine."

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Good morning | formal time-of-day greeting (before noon) | /ɡʊd ˈmɔːnɪŋ/ UK · /ɡʊd ˈmɔːrnɪŋ/ US | https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/good-morning | ≈#300 (multiword) | US/UK |
| Good afternoon | formal time-of-day greeting (noon–evening) | /ɡʊd ˌɑːftəˈnuːn/ UK · /ɡʊd ˌæftərˈnuːn/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/good-afternoon | ≈#700 (multiword) | US/UK |
| How do you do? | formal, dated greeting used upon being introduced | /ˌhaʊ də juː ˈduː/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/how_do_you_do | ≈#900 (multiword) | US/UK |
| Pleased to meet you | polite formula when first introduced | /ˌpliːzd tə ˈmiːt juː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/pleased-to-meet-you | ≈#600 (multiword) | US/UK |
| Nice to meet you | polite formula when first introduced (slightly less formal) | /ˌnaɪs tə ˈmiːt juː/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nice_to_meet_you | ≈#350 (multiword) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `Good morning` /ɡʊd ˈmɔːnɪŋ/ (UK) / /ɡʊd ˈmɔːrnɪŋ/
> (US) confirmed **verbatim in Collins** ("a conventional expression of
> greeting or farewell used in the morning"; Collins lists both the UK IPA
> /ɡʊd ˈmɔːnɪŋ/ and the US IPA /ɡʊd ˈmɔrnɪŋ/ on the same page).
> `How do you do?` confirmed in Wiktionary as "(formal, dated) A greeting used
> upon being introduced to someone. Alternatives are *how are you*, *pleased
> to meet you*, and *nice to meet you*." `Nice to meet you` confirmed in
> Wiktionary ("A polite expression used when the speaker is first introduced
> to someone"; synonym listed: *pleased to meet you*). `Pleased to meet you`
> is the Cambridge multiword entry and a Wiktionary-attested synonym of
> *nice to meet you*; IPA composed from Cambridge headwords *pleased* /pliːzd/,
> *to* /tə/ (weak), *meet* /miːt/, *you* /juː/ (strong) — matching the
> SpanishDict IPA column for the phrase.

## C. Self-introduction chunks (who you are)

After the opener, the next move is naming yourself. Two registers again: the
casual contraction `I'm…` and the fuller `My name is…`. The uptake `Nice to
meet you` (or `Pleased to meet you`) is the **obligatory** second pair-part of
an English introduction — dropping it is the #1 Vietnamese L1 failure (see L1
pitfalls table in `GREETINGS_INTROS.md`).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'm… (I am) | contracted self-identification | /aɪm/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/i-m | ≈#5 (of *I*) | US/UK |
| My name is… | fuller self-identification (slightly more formal) | /maɪ ˈneɪm ɪz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/name | ≈#130 (of *name*) | US/UK |
| Nice to meet you, I'm… | combined uptake + self-ID | /ˌnaɪs tə ˈmiːt juː aɪm/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nice_to_meet_you | ≈#350 (multiword) | US/UK |
| I'm based in… | where you live/work (professional self-ID) | /aɪm ˈbeɪst ɪn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/base_2 | ≈#450 (of *base*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `I'm` /aɪm/ is the standard Cambridge contraction of
> *I am*. `My name is…` composed from Cambridge *my* /maɪ/, *name* /neɪm/,
> *is* /ɪz/. `Nice to meet you, I'm…` chains two corpus-attested chunks
> (§B + this section). `based in` is the Cambridge phrasal verb *base* +
> *in* (=/beɪst/, the `-ed` realized as /t/ after the voiceless /s/ — see
> 🔗 [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md) §3).

## D. Introducing others (third-party introductions)

When you bring two people together, English gives you a **register ladder**:
casual `This is…` → mid `Have you met…?` → formal `I'd like you to meet…` →
very formal `May I introduce…?`. Vietnamese has no direct equivalent ladder
because introductions in Vietnamese carry the pronoun hierarchy (anh/chị/em)
inline; in English the hierarchy is dropped and the *formula* carries the
register.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| This is… | casual introducer | /ˈðɪs ɪz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/this | ≈#20 (of *this*) | US/UK |
| Have you met…? | mid-register introducer (implies "not yet") | /həv juː met/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/meet | ≈#300 (of *meet*) | US/UK |
| I'd like you to meet… | formal introducer | /aɪd ˈlaɪk juː tə ˈmiːt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/like_1 | ≈#80 (of *like*) | US/UK |
| (It's) nice to meet you too. | uptake-response (the reply to *nice to meet you*) | /ˌnaɪs tə ˈmiːt juː ˈtuː/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nice_to_meet_you | ≈#350 (multiword) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** the four-rung ladder (This is / Have you met / I'd
> like you to meet / May I introduce) is the standard taxonomy reproduced
> across ESL introduction references; each chunk's IPA is composed from
> Cambridge headword entries. `Have you met…?` uses *meet* /miːt/ in the past
> participle form (Cambridge).
