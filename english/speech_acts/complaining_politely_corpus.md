## A. Soft openers — the hedges that protect "negative face"

A complaint is, by Brown & Levinson's definition, a **face-threatening act**
(FTA): it imposes on the hearer's freedom of action ("negative face") and risks
theirs and yours. English redresses that threat with a **hedge opener** — a
soft, indirect first move that says "I don't want to attack you, but here is a
problem." Vietnamese *khiển责* / *phàn nàn* has no obligatory softener slot, so
this opening is the single highest-value habit in the bundle.

## B. Stating the problem (after the soft opener)

Once the hedge is out, the problem itself is stated **plainly and factually** —
no adjectives of emotion ("terrible," "disgusting"), no generalisations ("you
always…"). English service complaints are remarkably **flat** in tone; the hedge
does the softening, the statement does the reporting.

| there's a mistake on the bill/invoice | factual: an error on a document | /ðeəz ə mɪˈsteɪk ɒn ðə bɪl/ UK · /ðerz ə mɪˈsteɪk ɑːn ðə bɪl/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/mistake | ≈#900 (of *mistake*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *work* /wɜːk/ UK · /wɜːrk/ US, *arrive* /əˈraɪv/,

> Cambridge transcriptions. The factual-tone principle ("hedge softens, statement
> reports — no emotion adjectives") is documented in the Brown & Levinson
> face-threatening-act framework (see Sources).

## C. Requesting resolution (the ask, also hedged)

The third move is the **request for action** — and it, too, is hedged. English
uses modal questions (*Could you…?*) and conditional gratitude
(*I'd appreciate it if…*) rather than imperatives (*Fix this. / Give me a
refund.*) because an imperative maximises the imposition on the hearer's
negative face.

| Could you fix / replace / refund…? | request a concrete remedy | /kʊd juː fɪks/ · /rɪˈfleɪs/ · /ˈriːfʌnd/ (noun) | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/refund (+ Cambridge "refund" noun/verb) | ≈#2500 (of *refund*) | US/UK |
| Could you sort this out? | please resolve it (informal but common) | /kʊd juː sɔːt ðɪs aʊt/ UK · /kʊd juː sɔːrt ðɪs aʊt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sort-out (+ Cambridge "sort out" phrasal verb) | ≈#800 (phrasal verb) | US/UK |
