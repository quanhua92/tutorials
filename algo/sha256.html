SHA-256 is a one-way meat grinder: feed 512-bit blocks through 64 rounds of bit-mixing that fold each block into an 8-word state; the final state is the 256-bit digest. This page re-runs the whole thing in JS from scratch (verified against hashlib) — step the padding, schedule, and compression on b"abc".  |  SHA-256 feeds RSA padding →

① Padding — Merkle-Damgård preprocessing (message → 512-bit blocks)

What to do: type a message. The page appends a 0x80 stop bit, zero-fills to 448 bits, then a 64-bit length field — so the total is a multiple of 512 bits.
Word 0 holds the bytes; the 0x80 is the single '1' bit; the last word holds the bit-length. Multi-block messages repeat the compress once per 512-bit block.

② Message schedule — 16 block words expand into 64

What this shows: the first 16 schedule words W[0..15] are the block (copied). Words 16..63 are derived by mixing earlier words: W[t] = σ1(W[t-2]) + W[t-7] + σ0(W[t-15]) + W[t-16]. Drag the slider to see how each derived word is built.
σ0/σ1 are rotate+shift mixes. Even for near-zero input the derived words look random — that bit-spreading is what makes one flipped input bit avalanche across the whole hash.

③ Compression — 64 rounds remix the 8-word state

What to do: step the slider through the 64 rounds on block 0 of "abc". Each round computes T1 = h+Σ1(e)+Ch(e,f,g)+K[t]+W[t] and T2 = Σ0(a)+Maj(a,b,c), then shifts the register.
just-updated (e, a) T1, T2 injected values round constant K[t] + schedule word W[t] are folded into T1

④ Final digest — the 8 state words ARE the hash

After the last block, the state is added into the IV. The 8 resulting words concatenate into the 256-bit digest. The page recomputes this from scratch in JS and compares to Python's hashlib.
