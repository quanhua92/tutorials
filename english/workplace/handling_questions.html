5Writing task

Write a defer line + a partial-knowledge hedge ("Let me come back to that" / "Off the top of my head…"). Then reveal the model answer.

Model (defer line + partial-knowledge hedge):

That's a great question. /ˌðæts ə ˌɡreɪt ˈkwestʃən/ — acknowledge first

Let me come back to that after I check the latest numbers. /ˌlet mi ˌkʌm ˈbæk tə ðæt/ — defer in-session

Off the top of my head, I'd say growth was around 15%. /ɑːf ðə ˈtɑːp əv maɪ ˈhed/ — hedge the partial answer

Does that answer your question? /ˌdʌz ðæt ˈænsər jɔːr ˈkwestʃən/ — check back to close the loop

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Bluffs an answer to save face (thể diện) rather than admit not knowingUse defer + follow-up: "Let me come back to that." Defer = professional, not incompetent.
Freezes / goes silent when a tough question lands — reads as unpreparedAcknowledge first to buy 2 seconds: "That's a great question."
Jumps straight to bare "I don't know" — sounds abruptWrap it: acknowledge → defer → check-back. Never lead with bare "I don't know."
States partial knowledge as fact (no L1 evidential system)Hedge before the part: "As far as I know…" / "Off the top of my head…"
Never checks back — reads a check-back as "doubting the listener"A check-back reads as helpful: "Does that answer your question?" Add one to every answer.
Memorises ONE phrase and over-uses it ("Yes, thank you" to everything)Drill all four acknowledges and rotate them: great question / good question / glad you asked / thanks for asking.
Skips the acknowledgement and answers cold — sounds abruptAlways open with an acknowledge chunk. The sequence is the polite default.
Drops final consonants → question → "ques-chin", asked → "ass"Release every final: ques-tion /tʃən/, askt /æskt/, check /tʃek/.
Reads "100%" number-for-number ("one zero zero percent")Read it as "a hundred percent": /ə ˈhʌndrəd pərˈsent/.
