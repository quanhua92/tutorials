## A. The request politeness ladder (ask someone to DO something)

English marks politeness by **indirectness**: the more words/hedges between you
and the imperative, the more respectful the request sounds. Cambridge Grammar
("Requests") attests the ladder explicitly — `_can you_` / `_will you_` are
"more informal"; `_could you_` + `_please_` is the polite default; `_would you
mind_` + -ing is "more polite and more common"; and formal letters climb to
`_I would be grateful if you could_`.

## B. Offering to do something (ask if someone WOULD LIKE you to act)

Cambridge Grammar ("Offers") attests the offering set explicitly and flags the
key pragmatic rule: *"We don't use the present simple to offer to do something
for someone. We most commonly use _'ll_."* The offer ladder runs from
`_Shall I_` / `_Would you like me to_` (asking permission to help) → `_Let me_`
(confident, almost-certain-they-want-it) → `_I'll_` (spontaneous, in-the-moment).

## C. Responding to offers (accepting & declining — the "uptake")

Cambridge Grammar ("Offers") is explicit: *"We usually say _yes, please_ or
_no, thanks_ when we reply to offers."* Vietnamese learners frequently omit this
uptake entirely (accepting with a bare nod, declining with a bare "no" that
sounds blunt). Every offer needs a **please** or **thanks** bookend.
