## A. Acknowledge (accept the feedback without defending)

The engine of graceful reception: **name the feedback before you respond to it**.
This is positive-face work (Brown & Levinson 1987) — by thanking or validating,
you signal the criticism was *received*, not fought. Cambridge records `Thanks
for…` as the standard grateful-uptake formula; `That's a fair point` concedes a
point's validity without conceding defeat; `I appreciate…` + object expresses
grateful recognition (CALD sense 2: "to be grateful for something"). The
game-changer for L1-Vietnamese learners is `I hadn't thought of it that way` —
it reframes the feedback as *new information* rather than an accusation, which is
why it disarms instead of defends.

## B. Commit (signal you'll act on it)

The follow-through: acknowledge alone reads as passive; a **commitment move** is
what separates a graceful receiver from a dismissive one. Cambridge records
`work on` (phrasal verb: "to try hard to achieve or improve something"); `keep
something in mind` (CALD: "to remember a fact or piece of information, especially
one that might be useful in the future"); and `Next time…` as the forward-looking
repair frame. These three moves turn feedback into a contract with yourself —
audible, specific, and time-bounded.

## C. Clarify without defending (ask for specifics, not an excuse)

The skilled move: ask a **clarifying question** instead of mounting a defense.
A defensive "but…" or "it wasn't my fault" turns feedback into a fight; a
clarifying question turns it into a dialogue. Cambridge records `Could you…?` as
the polite-request modal (CALD); `give someone an example` is the standard
specification request; `say more` / `say more about…` is the open-ended probe
(modelled in YouGlish attestation). These keep the tone **curious, not
combative** — the hearer hears interest, not resistance.
