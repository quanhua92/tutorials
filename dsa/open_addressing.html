Every key lives directly in the table — on a collision you probe down a sequence for the next empty slot. Linear probing welds keys into contiguous clusters; quadratic scatters them; double hashing gives every key its own step and best tracks the 1/(1−α) bound. Step through inserts, slide the load factor, and compare strategies — every number is recomputed in JS from the identical formulas as open_addressing.py, gold-checked below.

② The load factor α — where each strategy lands vs the 1/(1−α) bound

What to do: drag α toward 1 and watch linear blow up (primary clustering: ~1/2(1+1/(1−α)²)) while double hashing hugs the uniform-hashing formula. Toggle successful / unsuccessful.
m = 1009 (prime), LCG keys — matches open_addressing.py Section E
Uniform-hashing bound (CLRS Thm 11.6): unsuccessful 1/(1−α), successful (1/α)·ln(1/(1−α)). Double hashing approximates it best; linear probing's own model ½(1+1/(1−α)²) is far worse as α→1.  © Section E

④ Primary vs secondary clustering — the victim key pays 6 (linear) or 2 (quadratic)

What this shows: insert 6 keys that all hash to slot 5, then insert key 6 (home 6). Linear packs them into a contiguous wall 5..10, so key 6 walks 6 slots. Quadratic scatters them, so key 6 walks 2 slots. Same keys, same probe counts to build — but a different tax on the next arrival.
