6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Vietnamese has no verb+particle structure → treats "follow up" as two words, translates only "follow"Learn the chunk as one lexical unit. Drill follow up like a single word; never split the meaning.
Avoids phrasal verbs entirely — "I will investigate the issue" / "I will postpone the meeting"Swap to the workplace chunk: look into, put off, push back. The Latinate verb sounds like a legal brief in a standup.
Wrong object placement — "look it into" (inseparable, WRONG) vs "look into it" (RIGHT); "bring up it" (separable, WRONG) vs "bring it up" (RIGHT)Learn separable vs inseparable per verb. Pronoun objects go between separable verbs; always after inseparable verbs.
Translates the parts literally — thinks bring up = "carry upward", roll out = "unroll a mat"Memorize the figurative meaning: bring up = mention; roll out = launch. The particle carries no spatial sense.
Drops the particle entirely — "I will follow the client" instead of "follow up with"Always produce the full chunk. The particle IS the workplace meaning — without it, follow is a different verb.
Register confusion — uses the Latinate synonym in casual speech ("I will initiate contact") and the phrasal verb in formal writingMatch register: phrasal verb = standard/casual workplace (Slack, standup); Latinate verb = formal (board memo). (See REGISTER SWITCHING.)
Mis-stresses the verb — FOLLOW up, REACH out (syllable-timed like Vietnamese)Stress the particle: follow UP, reach OUT, push BACK. English phrasal verbs are not equal-beat.
No linking → pronounces follow up as two words with a glottal breakLink verb-to-particle: /ˈfɒləʊ‿ʌp/, /riːtʃ‿aʊt/. (See LINKING.)
Confuses follow up (pursue/check) with follow (go behind) as the same verbFollow up = take further action on a previous contact; follow = go behind. Different verbs — the particle creates a new word.
