① XOR is its own inverse — encrypt and decrypt are the same function

What to do: type a plaintext and a key of the same length. The table shows the bit-level XOR (1 where inputs differ). Applying the key again recovers the plaintext — that is the whole cipher.

② The one-time pad — every plaintext is equally likely (perfect secrecy)

A truly-random, full-length, used-once key gives perfect secrecy. For any guess P' there is a key K' = P' ^ C that yields the same ciphertext, so C leaks zero info: H(P|C) = H(P). The key below is the seeded(42) pad from xor_cipher.py Section B.

③ Why key reuse is catastrophic — repeating key breaks, two-time pad leaks

A short repeating key (Vigenère) leaks its period and falls to frequency analysis. Reusing a pad once is a "two-time pad": c1 ^ c2 = p1 ^ p2 — the key vanishes. Drag a crib across it to read the other message.

④ Malleability — flip a ciphertext bit to edit the plaintext (no key)

XOR is a group operation, so decrypt(c ^ d) = p ^ d: an attacker edits the plaintext by editing the ciphertext, without the key. Watch $1000 become $9000.
