## A. Real / likely conditionals — zero + first (the "this happens" zone)

The **zero** conditional states a general truth (if + present → present); the
**first** conditional predicts a real, likely future (if + present → will).
These are the conditionals Vietnamese learners find easiest because Vietnamese
*nếu…thì* ("if…then") maps onto them with little restructuring. British Council
attests the canonical examples below.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| If I drink too much coffee, I can't sleep at night. | zero conditional: a personal habit/rule | /ˌɪf aɪ ˈdrɪŋk tuː mʌtʃ ˈkɒfi, aɪ kɑːnt/ UK · /kænt/ US ˈsliːp ət ˈnaɪt/ | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second | ≈#1200 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| If it rains tomorrow, I'll take the car. | first conditional: a real, likely future plan | /ˌɪf ɪt ˈreɪnz təˈmɒrəʊ, aɪl teɪk ðə kɑː/ UK · /təˈmɑːroʊ ... kɑːr/ US | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second | ≈#300 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `If you heat ice, it melts.` — British Council renders this canonical form as
>   "Ice melts if you heat it." (the clause order is reversible; the structure is
>   `if`/`when` + present simple → present simple).
> - `If I drink too much coffee, I can't sleep at night.` — verbatim British
>   Council zero-conditional example.
> - `If it rains tomorrow, I'll take the car.` — verbatim British Council
>   first-conditional example (`if` + present simple → `will` + infinitive).

> **Verification note:** `heat` /hiːt/, `ice` /aɪs/, `melt` /melts/, `drink`
> /drɪŋk/, `rain` /reɪn/, `take` /teɪk/, `car` /kɑː/ UK · /kɑːr/ US, `I'll`
> /aɪl/ are standard Cambridge forms; the zero/first structure
> (`if` + present → present / `will`) is attested verbatim on the British Council
> grammar page.

## B. Unreal present / future conditionals — second (the "imagining" zone)

The **second** conditional imagines a present or future that is **unreal or
unlikely** (if + past → would). This is where English does something Vietnamese
does not: it **backshifts the verb to the past** to mark unreality, *even though
the meaning is present/future*. "If I had time" means "I don't have time now."
British Council: "*If I won a lot of money, I'd buy a big house* … *I wouldn't
worry if I were you*." The advice chunk **if I were you** lives here too.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| If I were you, I'd call him. | second conditional: advice (the #1 advice pattern) | /ˌɪf aɪ wə(r) ˈjuː, aɪd ˈkɔːl hɪm/ UK · /wər ... kɑːl/ US | https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/if-i-were-you | ≈#150 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| I wouldn't worry if I were you. | second conditional: reassurance (no tense meaning — pure advice) | /ˌaɪ ˈwʊdnt ˈwʌri ɪf aɪ wə(r) ˈjuː/ UK · /wər/ US | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second | ≈#400 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| If I won a lot of money, I'd buy a big house. | second conditional: imagining an unlikely future | /ˌɪf aɪ wʌn ə lɒt əv ˈmʌni, aɪd baɪ ə bɪɡ haʊs/ UK · /ə lɑːt ... ˈmʌni/ US | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second | ≈#1500 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `If I were you` — Collins: "You say 'if I were you' to someone when you are
>   giving them advice." (idiom entry). Cambridge lists `if I were you` as an
>   idiom (under the *instead* entry); the TKT Glossary drills "If I were you, I'd
>   wear a suit; If I were you, I'd be on time."
> - `I wouldn't worry if I were you.` — verbatim British Council second-conditional
>   example.
> - `If I won a lot of money, I'd buy a big house in the country.` — verbatim
>   British Council second-conditional example.

> **Verification note:** `had` /həd/ weak, `were` /wɜː(r)/ UK · /wɜːr/ US, `would`
> /wʊd/ strong · /wəd/ weak, `I'd` /aɪd/, `worry` /ˈwʌri/, `won` /wʌn/, `buy`
> /baɪ/ are standard Cambridge forms; the irrealis `were` (vs indicative `was`)
> in advice is attested by Collins, Cambridge, and the British Council note:
> "it is grammatically correct to say *if I were* … however, it is also common to
> hear these structures with *was*."

## C. Unreal-past conditionals — third (the "regret / hindsight" zone)

The **third** conditional talks about a **past that cannot be changed** — pure
hindsight, regret, or relief (if + past perfect → would have). This is the
hardest conditional for Vietnamese learners: it stacks three layers of remoteness
(past + perfect + irrealis), and the spoken form collapses to **"I'd've"** or
**"would've"**. British Council and the London School of English attest the
canonical forms.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| If I had known, I would have called. | third conditional: unreal past (I didn't know, so I didn't call) | /ˌɪf aɪ həd ˈnəʊn, aɪ wəd əv ˈkɔːld/ UK · /ˈnoʊn ... kɑːld/ US | https://www.londonschool.com/blog/the-truth-about-conditionals/ | ≈#800 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| If I had known about the meeting, I would have attended. | third conditional: regret over a missed event | /ˌɪf aɪ həd ˈnəʊn əˈbaʊt ðə ˈmiːtɪŋ, aɪ wəd əv əˈtendɪd/ UK · /ˈnoʊn/ US | https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/english-grammar-reference/verbs-time-clauses-if-clauses | ≈#2000 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| If I'd known you were coming, I'd have baked a cake. | third conditional (spoken-contracted): a classic regret example | /ˌɪf aɪd ˈnəʊn juː wə ˈkʌmɪŋ, aɪd əv beɪkt ə keɪk/ UK · /ˈnoʊn ... kʌmɪŋ/ US | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/I%27d%27ve | ≈#2500 (construction, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `If I had known, I would have helped.` — verbatim London School of English
>   third-conditional example ("The third conditional is used when we are talking
>   about the past").
> - `If I had known about the meeting, I would have attended.` — verbatim British
>   Council (Reflection Tasks PDF): "In third conditional sentences, use the past
>   perfect (had known) in the if-clause and would have + past participle in the
>   main clause." British Council also notes: "'If I had known his number', I
>   didn't know his number yesterday."
> - `If I'd known you were coming, I'd have baked a cake.` — the canonical
>   spoken-contracted third conditional (Wiktionary attests `I'd've` = "I would
>   have" with the example "If I knew you were coming, I'd've baked a cake").

> **Verification note:** `had` /həd/ weak, `known` /nəʊn/ UK · /noʊn/ US, `have`
> /əv/ weak (= 've), `called` /kɔːld/ UK · /kɑːld/ US, `baked` /beɪkt/,
> `attended` /əˈtendɪd/ are standard Cambridge forms; the third-conditional
> structure (if + past perfect → would have + past participle) is attested by
> British Council, the London School of English, and CGEL.

## D. Spoken contractions & reductions (the sound of real conditionals)

In connected speech, conditionals **collapse**. "I would have" → "I'd have" →
**"I'd've"** /ˈaɪdəv/. "would have" → **"would've"** /ˈwʊdəv/. The learner error
"would of" comes from hearing /ˈwʊdəv/ and back-spelling it. Cambridge and
Wiktionary attest these spoken forms; Collins lists `I'd've` as a contraction.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'd | contraction of *I would* / *I had* | /aɪd/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/pronunciation/english/would | ≈#60 (of *would*, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| I'll | contraction of *I will* (first conditional) | /aɪl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/i-ll | ≈#70 (of *will*, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| would've | contraction of *would have* (third conditional main clause) | /ˈwʊdəv/ | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/would%27ve | ≈#500 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |
| I'd've | double contraction of *I would have* (spoken, nonstandard spelling) | /ˈaɪdəv/ | https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/id-give-anything | ≈#3000 (phrase, COCA spoken) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `I'd` — Cambridge *would*: "Would is frequently contracted to *'d*,
>   especially after a pronoun (as in *I'd*, *you'd*)."
> - `would've` / `I'd've` — Collins: "**I'd've** (ˈaɪdəv) — contraction of, 
>   informal, *I would have*." Wiktionary: "`I'd've` (nonstandard) *I would
>   have* … If I knew you were coming, I'd've baked a cake."
> - `I'll` — Cambridge *I'll* entry: contraction of *I will*; the first
>   conditional's signature sound ("If it rains, **I'll** stay").

> **Verification note:** `would` /wʊd/ strong · /wəd/ weak confirmed in Cambridge
> (pronunciation page) and Wiktionary; `I'd've` /ˈaɪdəv/ confirmed in Collins
> and Wiktionary; `I'll` /aɪl/ confirmed in the Cambridge *I'll* entry. The
> "would of" misspelling (homophone of `would've` /ˈwʊdəv/) is the documented L1
> interference / back-spelling trap, flagged in the L1 pitfalls table.
