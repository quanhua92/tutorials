## A. Acknowledging bad news (the everyday workhorse)

The single most useful sympathy move in English: **hear the news, name it as
bad, do not jump to a fix.** This is the workhorse register — illness, a lost
job, a failed exam, a break-up. Vietnamese *chia buồn* is a ritualized funeral
formula; for everyday bad news it is **too heavy**, so learners go silent. These
chunks fill that gap.

## B. Reacting to the weight (acknowledge the feeling, don't fix it)

The second move: a short reaction that **matches the weight** of the news. Mild
→ *That's a shame*; serious → *That sounds awful / How awful for you*. The
Vietnamese trap is to skip this and go straight to advice. These chunks buy the
time and the warmth that advice alone cannot.

## C. Showing concern (name the feeling before you offer anything)

The move that separates a warm response from a cold one: **acknowledge the
person's feeling explicitly**. English uses *must be* + adjective ("You must be
devastated") as the canonical empathy guess. Vietnamese learners often skip this
step — they either go silent or jump to problem-solving. Say the feeling first;
the fix (if any) comes after.

## D. Formal condolence (death / serious loss / written)

The high-formality end — reserved for **death** or written messages. Vietnamese
*phúng điếu* / *chia buồn* is exactly this register, so the mapping is close,
but the English collocations are fixed: *condolences* (always plural), *my/our
deepest sympathies*, *heartfelt sympathy*. Do **not** use *condolences* for a
lost job or a cold — it reads as if the person has died.

> sympathies* is quoted verbatim from the Cambridge Dictionary blog. *I'm sorry
