## A. The three-register ladder — ONE message at three levels

Register is a **ladder, not a switch**. The same intent (ask for a file) climbs
from **casual** (to a close friend / teammate) through **neutral** (default
workplace) to **formal** (to a stranger / client / senior). Each rung swaps
**vocabulary** (Germanic → Latinate), **contractions**, and **hedging**.
Confirmed as the core model of spoken register in the Cambridge *Grammar of
English* (Carter & McCarthy, CUP) and the Manchester Academic Phrasebank.

> **Verification note:** the three-run ladder (casual ↔ neutral ↔ formal) is the
> canonical register model in *Cambridge Grammar of English* (Carter & McCarthy,
> CUP) and the Manchester Academic Phrasebank ("Formality" section). The two
> levers that move a message between rungs are **vocabulary origin** (Germanic →
> Latinate, see §B) and **grammatical formality** (contractions → full forms;
> *can* → *could* → *would like to*).

## B. The formal ↔ casual vocabulary ladder (the core anchors)

English keeps **two words for the same idea** — a **Latinate** one (French /
Latin, brought in after the Norman Conquest of 1066) and a **Germanic** one
(Anglo-Saxon, the original everyday base). The Latinate twin marks **formal**
register; the Germanic twin marks **casual** register. This is a **historical
fact about English**, not a style preference (see §C for the WHY). Confirmed in
the etymology of every entry below and in *The Cambridge Encyclopedia of the
English Language* (Crystal, CUP).

## C. The historical WHY — Latinate (formal) vs Germanic (casual)

English is unusual among Germanic languages in keeping **two layers of
vocabulary** for the same idea. This is not random — it is the residue of a
political event:

- **Before 1066:** English was a West Germanic language (Anglo-Saxon). Its words
  for everyday life — *buy, get, help, start, enough, end, ask, need* — were
  Germanic.
- **1066 — the Norman Conquest:** French-speaking Normans conquered England and
  became the ruling class. Law, government, the church, and high culture ran in
  **French and Latin** for ~300 years. Their words — *purchase, obtain, assist,
  commence, sufficient, terminate, request, require* — entered English as the
  **formal, official, learned** layer.
- **The result:** for hundreds of core concepts, English has a **Latinate twin**
  (formal) and a **Germanic twin** (casual). Picking the wrong twin in the wrong
  context is the register error.

Confirmed in: *The Cambridge Encyclopedia of the English Language* (Crystal,
CUP); Wikipedia "English language" (Old English = West Germanic; Norman
Conquest = French/Latin adoption); the British Council ("Norman French words …
didn't always replace the Anglo-Saxon words"); and the etymology field of every
dictionary entry in §B.

```text
   GERMANIC (Anglo-Saxon, pre-1066)      LATINATE (French/Latin, post-1066)
   ─────────────────────────────────      ──────────────────────────────────
   buy           get      help   start     purchase    obtain   assist  commence
   enough        end      ask    need      sufficient  terminate request require
   ▲ casual / everyday / spoken              ▲ formal / official / written
```

> **Verification note (the historical fact):** "Old English emerged from a group
> of West Germanic dialects spoken by the Anglo-Saxons" (Wikipedia, *English
> language*); "Upon the conquest of England by the Normans in 1066, numerous
> words came to be adopted from French and, subsequently, also from Latin" (UT
> Austin Linguistics Research Center, *Introduction to Old English*); the
> Latinate/Germanic register split is documented in Crystal, *The Cambridge
> Encyclopedia of the English Language* (CUP).
