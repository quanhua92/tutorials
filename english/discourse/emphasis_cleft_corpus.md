## A. It-cleft frames ‚ÄĒ "It was X that‚Ä¶" (foreground ONE element)

The **it-cleft** is the most common cleft in English. It takes the shape
`It + be + [focus] + relative clause (that/who)`. The element right after *It
was‚Ä¶* is the **new/contrasted** information; the *that*-clause carries the
already-understood background. Confirmed in *English Grammar Today* (Cambridge)
and the British Council C1 grammar page.

## B. Wh-cleft frames ‚ÄĒ "What I want is‚Ä¶" (pseudo-clefts)

The **wh-cleft** (also called *pseudo-cleft*) takes the shape
`What-clause + be + [focus]`. Here the *what*-clause carries the
already-understood background, and the element **after** *is* is the foregrounded
new information. Confirmed in *English Grammar Today* (Cambridge) and the
British Council C1 grammar page.

## C. Discourse-cleft frames ‚ÄĒ "What I mean is‚Ä¶", "The thing is‚Ä¶"

These are the **spoken, interactive** wh-clefts. They are so frequent in
conversation that Cambridge lists them under **discourse markers**, not just
grammar. Their function: re-state, clarify, or introduce the key point. This is
where the "emphasis" function meets "repair/clarification."
