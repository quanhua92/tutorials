## A. Adding to a point (extending what someone said)

The core collaborative move in an English-language meeting: you take the floor
not to disagree but to *extend* a colleague's idea. These chunks signal "I'm
with you, and here's more." Oxford Learner's Dictionary records `add` as
"[intransitive] to say something more; to make a further remark — *Do you have
anything to add?*" and `build on` as "to use something as a basis for further
progress — *This study builds on earlier work.*"

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'd like to add… | polite opener to add a remark to the discussion | /aɪd ˈlaɪk tə æd/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/add_1 | ≈#100 (of *add*) | US/UK |
| Just to add to that… | a lighter way to extend the previous point | /dʒʌst tə æd tə ðæt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/add_1 | ≈#100 (of *add*) | US/UK |
| Building on what X said… | take a colleague's idea as a base and extend it | /ˈbɪldɪŋ ɒn wɒt ɛks sɛd/ UK · /ˈbɪldɪŋ ɑːn wʌt ɛks sɛd/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/build-on | ≈#400 (of *build*) | US/UK |
| That's a good point, and… | validate the prior point before adding your own | /ðæts ə ɡʊd ˈpɔɪnt ænd/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/point | ≈#130 (of *point*) | US/UK |
| I'd like to come back to… | flag that you want to return to an earlier thread | /aɪd ˈlaɪk tə kʌm ˈbæk tə/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/come-back-to | ≈#90 (of *come back*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `add` /æd/, `adding` /ˈædɪŋ/ confirmed verbatim in the
> Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (verb forms table) and corroborated in
> Cambridge; `build on` "to use something as a basis for further progress" with
> the example *This study builds on earlier work* is the exact Oxford entry;
> `point` /pɔɪnt/ and `good point` are the standard Cambridge transcriptions;
> `come back to` is the Oxford phrasal-verb entry ("to return to a subject").

## B. Entering the discussion (taking the floor)

When nobody has handed you the floor, you must claim it politely. These chunks
let you *enter* without interrupting rudely. Oxford records `jump in` as
"to interrupt a conversation — *Before she could reply Peter jumped in with an
objection*" and `chime in` as "to join or interrupt a conversation — *He kept
chiming in with his own opinions.*"

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| If I could jump in… | polite way to take the floor mid-discussion | /ɪf aɪ kʊd dʒʌmp ɪn/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/jump-in | ≈#1200 (of *jump*) | US/UK |
| Can I come in here? | ask permission to join the discussion | /kæn aɪ kʌm ɪn hɪə/ UK · /kæn aɪ kʌm ɪn hɪr/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/come-in | ≈#90 (of *come*) | US/UK |
| To chime in… | join the conversation, usually to agree/extend | /tə tʃaɪm ɪn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/chime-in | ≈#4000 (of *chime*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `jump in` "to interrupt a conversation" is the exact
> Oxford phrasal-verb entry (example *Peter jumped in with an objection*);
> `chime in` "to interrupt or speak in a conversation, usually to agree with what
> has been said" is the Cambridge entry, with `chime` /tʃaɪm/ confirmed in both
> Cambridge and Oxford; `come in` is the Oxford entry, with `come` /kʌm/ and
> `here` /hɪə/ UK · /hɪr/ US the standard dictionary forms.

## C. Low-stakes offering (hedged contribution)

When your idea is tentative or you are not the expert in the room, a hedge
softens the entry so a "wrong" idea costs no face. *For what it's worth* is the
canonical low-stakes opener — Merriam-Webster glosses it as "an expression used
to say that one is not sure how helpful something one is about to say will be."

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| For what it's worth… | low-stakes hedge: "I'm not sure this helps, but…" | /fə wɒt ɪts wɜːθ/ UK · /fər wʌts wɜːrθ/ US | https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/for%20what%20it%27s%20worth | ≈#1500 (of *worth*) | US/UK |
| I'd just add that… | soften an addition with "just" (hedge) | /aɪd dʒʌst æd ðæt/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/add_1 | ≈#100 (of *add*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `worth` /wɜːθ/ UK · /wɜːrθ/ US is the standard Cambridge
> transcription; *for what it's worth* is documented as an idiom in
> Merriam-Webster ("said when offering advice or an opinion to signal that the
> listener is free to disregard it"); the reduction of *it's* → /ɪts/ and *for*
> → /fə/–/fər/ follows the Cambridge connected-speech notes for weak forms.
