## Why this is bundle #45 (read this first)

Vietnamese workplaces run on **hierarchy**. Hofstede's power-distance index
scores Vietnam ‚Čą70 ‚ÄĒ one of the highest in the world ‚ÄĒ which means a manager
delegating to a report does it **imperatively**: *"Em l√†m c√°i b√°o c√°o n√†y"*
(literally "You do this report") is normal, polite, and expected. The report
hears an order from above and executes it. No face is lost, because the
hierarchy already grants the manager the right to command.

English professional workplaces run on **low power-distance norms**. The *same*
sentence ‚ÄĒ "Do this report" ‚ÄĒ sounds **authoritarian, rude, and micromanaging**
to a native English ear. The English delegator wraps the assignment in three
layers of redress:

1. **A polite modal question** ‚ÄĒ *"Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶?"* (not "Do
   this").
2. **A clear scope + deadline** ‚ÄĒ *"You'll be responsible for X, by Friday."*
3. **An explicit support offer** ‚ÄĒ *"Let me know if you hit any roadblocks."*

This three-move arc ‚ÄĒ **assign ‚Üí scope + deadline ‚Üí support** ‚ÄĒ is the whole
bundle. Master it and you sound like a competent English-speaking lead; skip any
of the three and you sound either bossy (imperative) or vague (no scope).
Vietnamese learners fail this bundle in two opposite directions: they are
**too directive** ("You must do this") when delegating down, and **too vague**
(no deadline, no scope) when delegating across or up, because pinning a peer or
senior down feels face-threatening. English gives you the language to be **clear
+ kind + accountable** at once.

> From `delegating_instructions_corpus.md`:
>
> | Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶? | Let me know if you hit any roadblocks. |
> |---|---|
> | /k äd juňź te…™k √į…ô liňźd …ín/ UK ¬∑ /k…ôd ju te…™k √į…ô liňźd …Ďňźn/ US | /let miňź n…ô ä …™f juňź h…™t ňąeni ňąr…ô äd.bl…íks/ UK ¬∑ /let miňź no ä …™f ju h…™t ňąeni ňąro äd.bl…Ďňźks/ US |
>
> The opener assigns ownership *politely* (modal question, not imperative); the
> closer offers support *explicitly* (you do not disappear after handing off).
> Between them sits the scope + deadline. Three moves, one delegation.

## 1. The mechanism: why English delegation is polite-ownership, not command

Oxford documents the verb `delegate` /ňądel…™…°e…™t/ as *"to give part of your
work, power or authority to somebody in a lower position than you"* ‚ÄĒ and notes
that *"Some managers find it difficult to delegate."* The English word itself
carries the idea that you are **handing over authority**, not issuing an order.
That is why every chunk in this bundle is phrased to **transfer ownership
while preserving the other person's autonomy**.

```mermaid
flowchart TD
    START["You need to delegate a task"] --> A1{"Move 1: ASSIGN<br/>(polite ownership)"}
    A1 -->|"modal question"| A2["Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶?<br/>Can you handle‚Ä¶ by Friday?"]
    A1 -->|"softened statement"| A3["I'd like you to own‚Ä¶"]
    A2 --> B["Move 2: SCOPE + DEADLINE<br/>(the accountability core)"]
    A3 --> B
    B --> B1["The goal is to‚Ä¶<br/>You'll be responsible for‚Ä¶<br/>The deadline is‚Ä¶ / ‚Ä¶by Friday"]
    B1 --> C["Move 3: SUPPORT<br/>(close the loop)"]
    C --> C1["Let me know if you hit any roadblocks.<br/>What do you need from me?<br/>Let's check in on this on‚Ä¶"]
    style A1 fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#58a6ff,stroke-width:2px
    style B fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#f59e0b,stroke-width:2px
    style C fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#27ae60,stroke-width:2px
```

Skip move 1 ‚Üí you sound bossy. Skip move 2 ‚Üí the recipient guesses the scope
and the deadline, and the work misses. Skip move 3 ‚Üí you have **abdicated**,
not delegated ‚ÄĒ the task is dumped, not handed off. All three moves, every
time.

## 2. Move 1 ‚ÄĒ Assign (the polite-ownership opener)

The opener's job is to **transfer ownership without sounding authoritarian**.
English gives you two shapes for this:

**A modal question** ‚ÄĒ *"Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶?"* / *"Can you handle‚Ä¶ by
Friday?"* The modal (`could` / `can`) softens the imperative into a request;
the verb (`take the lead on` / `handle`) names ownership, not compliance.

**A softened statement** ‚ÄĒ *"I'd like you to own‚Ä¶"* The `I'd like you to`
frame (modal `would` + `like`) turns a command into a stated preference, which
is how English managers give an instruction that is *functionally* an order but
*pragmatically* a request.

> From `delegating_instructions_corpus.md` (the three assignment shapes,
> verbatim):
>
> - **Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶?** ‚ÄĒ /k äd juňź te…™k √į…ô liňźd …ín/ UK ¬∑
>   /k…ôd ju te…™k √į…ô liňźd …Ďňźn/ US ‚ÄĒ the polite modal question (pinned)
> - **I'd like you to own‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ /a…™d la…™k juňź t…ô …ô än/ UK ¬∑
>   /a…™d la…™k ju t…ô o än/ US ‚ÄĒ the softened ownership statement
> - **Can you handle‚Ä¶ by Friday?** ‚ÄĒ /k√¶n juňź ňąh√¶n.d…ôl ba…™ ňąfra…™.de…™/ ‚ÄĒ
>   assignment with the deadline built in

**The Vietnamese trap:** learners reach for *"You must‚Ä¶"* or *"Please do‚Ä¶"*
because that is how hierarchy talks in Vietnamese (*"Em phŠļ£i l√†m‚Ä¶"* /
*"L√†m ∆°n l√†m‚Ä¶"*). Both sound like orders in English. The fix is mechanical ‚ÄĒ
**swap the imperative for a modal question** (`Could you‚Ä¶?` / `Can you‚Ä¶?`) or
the `I'd like you to‚Ä¶` frame. ūüĒó This is the same modal-politeness move you
drilled in [REQUESTING & OFFERING](../speech_acts/REQUESTING_OFFERING.md).

## 3. Move 2 ‚ÄĒ Scope + deadline (the accountability core)

This is the move Vietnamese learners omit most often. Once ownership is
assigned, the English delegator **names the goal, the scope of responsibility,
and the deadline** ‚ÄĒ explicitly, in the same breath. Cambridge documents `be
responsible for` as the delegation phrase verbatim: *"to have control and
authority over something or someone and the duty of taking care of it"* ‚ÄĒ
*"He is responsible for the council's waste management department."*
Cambridge Business English documents `deadline` with the full collocation set:
`set/give a deadline`, `a deadline of [date]`, `a tight deadline`, `meet/miss/
extend the deadline`.

> From `delegating_instructions_corpus.md`:
>
> - **The goal is to‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ /√į…ô …°…ô äl …™z t…ô/ UK ¬∑ /√į…ô …°o äl …™z t…ô/ US ‚ÄĒ
>   naming the outcome (scope-setter)
> - **You'll be responsible for‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ /juňźl biňź r…™ňąsp…ín.s…ô.b…ôl f…ô/ UK ¬∑
>   /juňźl biňź r…™ňąsp…Ďňźn.s…ô.b…ôl f…ôr/ US ‚ÄĒ naming what is owned end-to-end
> - **The deadline is‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ /√į…ô ňąded.la…™n …™z/ ‚ÄĒ pinning the due date
> - **‚Ä¶by Friday** ‚ÄĒ /ba…™ ňąfra…™.de…™/ ‚ÄĒ the deadline-pinning preposition
>   (`by` = no later than)

**The Vietnamese trap:** in a high-context culture, the scope and the deadline
are often **assumed understood** from the relationship and the setting ‚ÄĒ so the
Vietnamese delegator says *"Please help with the report"* and expects the
report to know which report, for whom, by when. English professional norms are
**low-context**: if you do not say "by Friday," there is no Friday. The fix is
to **always attach a deliverable + a time**: *"‚Ä¶by end of day Friday,"*
*"‚Ä¶before our Monday standup."* A delegation without a deadline is not a
delegation ‚ÄĒ it is a wish.

## 4. Move 3 ‚ÄĒ Support (close the loop, never dump and disappear)

The move that separates delegation from **abdication**. After assigning scope
and deadline, the English delegator **explicitly offers help and schedules a
follow-up**. Cambridge documents `check in` (CONTACT sense) ‚ÄĒ *"to contact
someone by making a phone call, short visit, etc., usually in order to make
sure there are no problems"* ‚ÄĒ with the collocation `check in with`.
`roadblock` carries the figurative sense "anything that stops progress" ‚ÄĒ
*"There have been several roadblocks in the peace process."*

> From `delegating_instructions_corpus.md` (the four support shapes, verbatim):
>
> - **Let me know if you hit any roadblocks.** ‚ÄĒ
>   /let miňź n…ô ä …™f juňź h…™t ňąeni ňąr…ô äd.bl…íks/ UK ¬∑
>   /let miňź no ä …™f ju h…™t ňąeni ňąro äd.bl…Ďňźks/ US ‚ÄĒ the canonical support
>   offer (pinned)
> - **What do you need from me?** ‚ÄĒ /w…ít duňź juňź niňźd fr…ím miňź/ UK ¬∑
>   /w…Ďňźt du ju niňźd fr…Ďňźm miňź/ US ‚ÄĒ the resource-check question
> - **Let's check in on this on‚Ä¶** ‚ÄĒ /lets t Éek …™n …ín √į…™s …ín/ UK ¬∑
>   /lets t Éek …™n …Ďňźn √į…™s …Ďňźn/ US ‚ÄĒ scheduling the follow-up
> - **Let me know how it's going.** ‚ÄĒ /let miňź n…ô ä ha ä …™ts ňą…°…ô ä.…™Ňč/ UK ¬∑
>   /let miňź no ä ha ä …™ts ňą…°o ä.…™Ňč/ US ‚ÄĒ a low-pressure status prompt

**The Vietnamese trap:** the support offer feels redundant ‚ÄĒ "if they need
help, they will ask." But in a low power-distance workplace, the **delegator is
expected to offer first**; silence reads as indifference or as setting the
report up to fail. The fix is to **always close the delegation with a support
line** ‚ÄĒ even a short *"Let me know if you hit any roadblocks"* signals "I have
not disappeared, and I am accountable too."

## 6. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits when delegating or giving instructions in English ‚ÄĒ extend, don't
replace, the seed rows from the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|

| **Too vague to save face** ‚ÄĒ "Please help with the report" with no scope, no deadline (Vietnamese indirectness avoids pinning someone down) | Name the **deliverable + the deadline explicitly**: *"You'll be responsible for the Q3 report, by Friday."* A delegation without a deadline is a wish. |
| **Omits the deadline** ‚ÄĒ assumes it is "understood" from context (high-context culture) | Always attach a time: *"‚Ä¶by end of day Friday,"* *"‚Ä¶before our Monday standup."* English professional norms are **low-context** ‚ÄĒ if you don't say it, it isn't there. |
| **Omits ownership language** ‚ÄĒ uses "help with" / "assist on" instead of `own` / `take the lead on` (feels presumptuous to claim ownership) | Use **ownership verbs**: *"I'd like you to own‚Ä¶"*, *"Could you take the lead on‚Ä¶?"* `own` in English workplace culture = "take full responsibility for," not arrogance. |
