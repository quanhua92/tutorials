## A. Buy-time calmly — buy 2 seconds and signal composure

The pressure reflex: a tough question lands and the body wants to **freeze or
blurt**. The composed speaker does neither — they drop a buy-time opener that
buys ~2 seconds of planning while signalling "I am in control, I am thinking."
Cambridge's *fair* entry itself glosses the attested collocate *"fair question"*
("a reasonable thing to say about / to ask"), and the *let me think* /
*let me see* entry attests the *"Let me…"* stalling frame. These are the spoken
capstone of the `discourse/fluency_fillers` chain, now deployed under load.

| Let me take a moment | asks for a second to compose the answer; signals control, not ignorance | /ˌlet mi ˈteɪk ə ˈməʊmənt/ UK · /ˌlet mi ˈteɪk ə ˈmoʊmənt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/moment | ≈ high (stall) | US/UK |
| Give me a second to think | informal buy-time; "give me a moment" frame | /ˌɡɪv mi ə ˈsekənd tə ˈθɪŋk/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/second_1 | ≈ mid (stall) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**

> - `Let me take a moment`: *moment* = "a very short period of time" — Cambridge,

> - `Give me a second to think`: *second* (noun) = "a short unit of time; a
>   moment" — Cambridge, *second*; *think* = "to use the mind to consider" —

## B. Handle not-knowing honestly — composed deferral beats a panicked bluff

The paradox at the heart of this bundle: under pressure, a learner's instinct is
to **fake an answer** (risky — caught out, credibility gone) or to **freeze**
(looks unprepared). The fluent move is the opposite of both — **honest
deferral**: name the limit of what you know, then promise to return with the
verified answer. Cambridge confirms every component: *figure* = "the symbol for
a number", *verify* = "to make certain that something is correct", *accurate* =
"correct, exact, and without any mistakes." Confirmed as the taught
"when-you-don't-know" move in ≥2 interview references.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I don't have the exact figure, but… | honest partial-answer: you lack the number, then give what you *do* know | /aɪ ˌdəʊnt həv ði ɪɡˈzækt ˈfɪɡə, bʌt/ UK · /aɪ ˌdoʊnt həv ði ɪɡˈzækt ˈfɪɡjər, bʌt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/figure | ≈ high (deferral) | US/UK |
| I'd need to verify that | honest full-deferral: you will not guess; you will check first | /aɪd ˈniːd tə ˈverɪfaɪ ðæt/ UK · /aɪd ˈniːd tə ˈverəfaɪ ðæt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/verify | ≈ mid (deferral) | US/UK |
| I want to make sure I give you an accurate answer | frames the deferral as care for accuracy, not ignorance | /aɪ ˈwɒnt tə meɪk ʃɔːr aɪ ɡɪv ju ən ˈækjərət ˈænsə/ UK · /aɪ ˈwɑːnt tə meɪk ʃʊr aɪ ɡɪv ju ən ˈækjərət ˈænsər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/accurate | ≈ mid (deferral) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `I don't have the exact figure, but…`: *figure* = "the symbol for a number or
>   the amount shown" — Cambridge, *figure* (e.g. *"The figure for last year was
>   over $2 million."*); *exact* = "in complete and correct detail" — Cambridge.
>   The *"…, but"* continuation is the standard partial-answer pivot.
> - `I'd need to verify that`: *verify* = "to make certain that something is
>   correct" — Cambridge, *verify* (C1; example *"These numbers… they'll have to
>   be verified."*). The *"I'd need to…"* hedge marks a confident, non-committal
>   deferral.
> - `I want to make sure I give you an accurate answer`: *accurate* = "correct,
>   exact, and without any mistakes" — Cambridge, *accurate* (B1; example *"The
>   figures they have used are just not accurate."*); *answer* /ˈænsər/ US ·
>   /ˈɑːnsə/ UK, *make sure* ("to check") are standard Cambridge entries.

> **Verification note:** `figure` /ˈfɪɡər/ UK · /ˈfɪɡjər/ US, `exact` /ɪɡˈzækt/,
> `verify` /ˈverɪfaɪ/ UK · /ˈverəfaɪ/ US, `accurate` /ˈækjərət/ UK · /ˈækjərət/ US
> confirmed verbatim in the Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary
> (web-fetched 2026-06-24). The "admit the gap, then defer" move is taught across
> interview references: Big Interview frames it as *"if you don't know, say so —
> then explain how you'd find the answer,"* and the APA composure reference
> recommends breathing + a structured response over a panicked guess.

## C. Defer / redirect — park it, or hand off cleanly

When the question is genuinely off-limits (speculation) or outside your
knowledge, **redirect with composure**: park it for later, decline to guess, or
name the boundary of your expertise and pivot. Cambridge confirms every move:
*come back* = "to return to a subject", *speculate* = "to guess possible answers
to a question when you do not have enough information to be certain", *area* =
"a particular subject, or type of activity." These are the spoken capstone of the
`workplace/handling_questions` defer arc.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Let me come back to that | parks the question; promises to address it later in the session | /ˌlet mi ˌkʌm ˈbæk tə ðæt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/come-back | ≈ high (defer) | US/UK |
| I'd rather not speculate | declines to guess; signals you won't invent a number/cause | /aɪd ˈrɑːðə nɒt ˈspekjəleɪt/ UK · /aɪd ˈræðər nɑːt ˈspekjəleɪt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/speculate | ≈ mid (decline) | US/UK |
| That's outside my area, but… | names the boundary of your expertise, then pivots to what you *can* say | /ˌðæts ˌaʊtˈsaɪd maɪ ˈeəriə, bʌt/ UK · /ˌðæts ˌaʊtˈsaɪd maɪ ˈeriə, bʌt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/area | ≈ mid (pivot) | US/UK |

> **Attested uses (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - `Let me come back to that`: *come back (to something)* = "to return to a
>   subject" — Cambridge, *come back* (see `workplace/handling_questions_corpus.md`
>   for the verbatim entry). The *"Let me…"* frame is Cambridge-attested (see §A).
> - `I'd rather not speculate`: *speculate* = "to guess possible answers to a
>   question when you do not have enough information to be certain" — Cambridge,
>   *speculate* (C2; verbatim example *"A spokesperson declined to speculate on
>   the cause of the train crash."*). The *"I'd rather not…"* decline is a
>   standard polite-refusal frame.
> - `That's outside my area, but…`: *area* = "a particular subject, or type of
>   activity" — Cambridge, *area* (e.g. *"That's not really my area."*); *outside*
>   = "beyond the limits of" — Cambridge. The *"…, but"* pivot turns the boundary
>   into an offer of what you *can* contribute.

> **Verification note:** `come back` (phrasal verb) confirmed in Cambridge;
> `speculate` /ˈspekjəleɪt/ UK · /ˈspekjəleɪt/ US confirmed verbatim in the
> Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary (web-fetched 2026-06-24) — note the
> dictionary's own example *"declined to speculate on the cause…"* is exactly the
> composure move this chunk encodes. `area` /ˈeəriə/ UK · /ˈeriə/ US, `outside`
>   /ˌaʊtˈsaɪd/, `rather` /ˈrɑːðər/ UK · /ˈræðər/ US are the standard Cambridge
>   forms.

  is read as **professional and confident, not weak**. (Cross-ref
