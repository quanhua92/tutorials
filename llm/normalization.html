Layer Normalization (LayerNorm → RMSNorm) — interactive 📖 NORMALIZATION.md 📄 normalization.py

📖 Read the full guide — architecture diagrams, decision tables, killer gotchas, cheat sheet. This page is the interactive companion.
What is this? A Transformer is a stack of ~100 blocks that multiply numbers together. Without a safety check, those numbers explode or vanish. Normalization is that safety check: once per block it quietly re-pins every token to a calm scale. This page shows the two ways to do it. Drag the sliders and watch the bars move — LayerNorm centers the numbers around zero *then* scales them; RMSNorm just scales them (faster, used by Llama/Qwen).
Drag the input feature sliders and the learned knob (γ) to see how each layer responds. Every number is recomputed in JS from the identical formula in normalization.py.  |  RMSNorm meets RoPE (QK-Norm) →

8 input features make up one token's vector (E=8). The learned knob (γ) re-emphasizes every feature after scaling (starts at 1 = no change). eps = 1e-5 (a safety number), beta = 0 (LayerNorm's extra shift knob, kept off for clarity). Defaults match the gold input x=[1..8] in normalization.py.

What am I looking at? For each feature there are 3 bars: the grey original, the red LayerNorm result, and the green RMSNorm result. Notice the red bars always straddle the center line (LayerNorm subtracted the average → centered at 0); the green bars stay on whichever side the inputs were (RMSNorm never centers, it only rescales). Drag a slider — only LayerNorm re-centers.

② The numbers each layer computes

What am I looking at? The average (mean) and spread (variance) are what LayerNorm uses — two separate scans over the numbers. The loudness (mean of squares, RMS) is all RMSNorm needs — one scan, no average subtracted. That is why RMSNorm is faster: it skips the average and the second scan.

③ The precision safety trick (why we upcast to float32)

What am I looking at? When inputs are extremely tiny, squaring them makes them even tinier. In low-precision bfloat16 (only ~2-3 digits), that tiny result can round to exactly zero — then we'd divide by ~zero and get NaN (garbage). The fix: do the dangerous squaring in high-precision float32 (~7 digits), then switch back. Toggle "RISKY" and shrink the inputs to watch it break. (This is normalization.py Section D.)
