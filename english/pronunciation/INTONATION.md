## Why this is bundle #09 (read this first)

A Vietnamese learner can pronounce every word correctly and **still not be
understood** ‚ÄĒ or, worse, be **misunderstood** ‚ÄĒ because the **pitch of the
voice** does not match the message. Vietnamese is a **lexical-tone language**:
pitch lives **on each syllable** and distinguishes **words** (*ma* = ghost /
mother / but / tomb / rice seedling / horse, depending on one of **six tones**).
English does the **opposite** ‚ÄĒ pitch moves across a **whole phrase** and
distinguishes **functions** (statement vs question) and **focus** (which word is
the new information), not words.

So a Vietnamese speaker tends to produce **monotone English** (the sentence-level
melody is flattened by the per-syllable tone habit), or **carries Vietnamese
tones into English** (a rising word-tone lands on the wrong syllable and distorts
the intended contour). The result: a statement that **sounds unsure** (wrong
rise), a question that **sounds rude** (wrong fall), or a list that **sounds
unfinished** (no final fall). Intonation is the last 10% of intelligibility ‚ÄĒ
and the first thing a native ear notices when it's wrong.

## 1. The mechanism: Vietnamese tone vs English intonation

The two languages use pitch for **completely different jobs**:

| | Vietnamese (L1) | English (target) |
|---|---|---|
| What pitch does | marks **word** meaning (lexical tone) | marks **sentence** function + focus (intonation) |
| Where pitch lives | on **each syllable** (6 contrastive tones) | on the **nucleus** of the phrase (one main movement) |
| How many tones | 6 (ngang, huyŠĽĀn, sŠļĮc, hŠĽŹi, ng√£, nŠļ∑ng) | 3 high-frequency nuclear tones: **fall ‚Üė ¬∑ rise ‚Üó ¬∑ fall-rise ‚Üė‚Üó** |
| A wrong pitch | changes the **word** (*ma* ‚Üí *m√°*) | changes the **function** (statement ‚Üí question) or the **focus** |

So the learner's mouth, trained to put a tone on every syllable, has to learn to
**let most of the sentence sit on a steady pitch and move it once**, on the
**nucleus** (the most prominent syllable, usually in the last lexical word).

> From `intonation_corpus.md` (the pinned minimal pair ‚ÄĒ corpus ¬ßA):
>
> | She's a teacher.‚Üė | She's a teacher?‚Üó |
> |---|---|
> | / Éiňźz …ô ňątiňźt É…ô‚Üė/ ‚ÄĒ nuclear **fall** | / Éiňźz …ô ňątiňźt É…ô‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ nuclear **rise** |
>
> The **same five words**, stressed identically, are a **statement** (fall ‚ÄĒ
> "this is a fact") or a **question** (rise ‚ÄĒ "is that so?"). Nothing in the
> consonants, vowels, or stress changed ‚ÄĒ only the **pitch movement on the last
> prominent syllable**. That is the whole of intonation in one example.

## 2. The three nuclear tones and what they mean

English intonation is built on the **nucleus** ‚ÄĒ the syllable carrying the main
pitch movement ‚ÄĒ and **three** high-frequency tones on it (Wells 2006; Roach
2009). Learn these three and you cover ~90% of spoken English melody.


### 2.1 Fall ‚Üė ‚ÄĒ "I'm telling you / I'm done"

The default **settled** tone. Statements, Wh-questions, commands, and the last
item of a list all land on a fall. It signals the speaker considers the matter
closed.

> From `intonation_corpus.md` (corpus ¬ßB1):
>
> - **Where do you live?‚Üė** /ňąwe…ô d…ô juňź ňąl…™v‚Üė/ ‚ÄĒ Wh-question, fall on "live"
> - **Leave it on the table.‚Üė** /ňąliňźv …™t …ín √į…ô ňąte…™bl‚Üė/ ‚ÄĒ command, fall on "ta-"
> - **It's on the ‚Üėtable.** /…™ts …ín √į…ô ňąte…™bl‚Üė/ ‚ÄĒ statement, fall on "ta-"

**The Vietnamese trap:** because Vietnamese has no sentence-final "statement
fall," a learner may end statements on a **flat or rising** pitch ‚ÄĒ sounding
**unsure** or **as if still asking**. Drill the fall on the last content word of
every statement.

### 2.2 Rise ‚Üó ‚ÄĒ "over to you / there's more"

The rise keeps the floor **open**: it genuinely asks (yes/no question), signals
**more to come** (a list item), hedges (**uncertainty**), or **softens** a
request into politeness.

> From `intonation_corpus.md` (corpus ¬ßB2):
>
> - **Do you want coffee?‚Üó** /duňź juňź w…ínt ňąk…ífi‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ yes/no question
> - **apples, ‚Üóoranges, ‚Üóbananas, and ‚Üėgrapes** ‚ÄĒ rise on every item **except the
>   last**, which falls (the fall says "that's everything")
> - **Could you help me?‚Üó** /k äd juňź ňąhelp miňź‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ request softened by a rise (a
>   fall here sounds blunt/like an order)
> - **See you ‚Üótomorrow?** /ňąsiňź juňź t…ôňąm…ír…ô ä‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ checking ("is that still ok?")

**The Vietnamese trap:** the opposite error of ¬ß2.1 ‚ÄĒ ending a **genuine
yes/no question** on a **fall** makes it sound like a **rude demand** or a
statement of fact ("You want coffee.‚Üė" instead of "You want coffee?‚Üó"). Pair
every yes/no question with a rise.

### 2.3 Fall-rise ‚Üė‚Üó ‚ÄĒ "‚Ä¶but" (the implication tone)

The fall-rise says **"there's more to this than I'm saying."** It signals a
**reservation**, a **hedged disagreement**, or a **contrast the speaker leaves
unfinished**. Cambridge Grammar Today: *"We use fall-rise intonation at the end
of statements when we want to say [that something is true but‚Ä¶]"*.

> From `intonation_corpus.md` (corpus ¬ßB3):
>
> - **It's ‚Üėgood‚Üó.** /…™ts ňą…° äd ‚Üė‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ implication: "‚Ä¶but [expensive / I have a
>   concern]." The withheld negative is the whole point.
> - **I ‚Üėdidn't‚Üó.** /a…™ ňąd…™dnt ‚Üė‚Üó/ ‚ÄĒ contrast: "‚Ä¶I did **something else**."

**The Vietnamese trap:** learners usually **flatten this to a simple fall**
("It's good.‚Üė") ‚ÄĒ which to a native ear **removes the reservation** and sounds
like unqualified praise. The fall-rise is *advanced* but it is what separates
"good English" from "native-like English" for hedging.

## 3. Focus / nuclear stress ‚ÄĒ moving the nucleus moves the meaning

The nucleus is **not fixed** to the last word. You can **shift it** to mark which
part of the sentence is the **new information**. Same words, different nucleus ‚Üí
the sentence answers a **different question**. Wells (2006) calls this
**tonicity**.

> From `intonation_corpus.md` (corpus ¬ßC):
>
> - **I saw a ‚ÜėMAN in the garden.** ‚ÄĒ nucleus on *man* ‚Üí answers **"Whom did you
>   see?"** (the man is the new info)
> - **I ‚ÜėSAW a man in the garden.** ‚ÄĒ nucleus on *saw* ‚Üí answers **"Did you hear
>   a man‚Ä¶?"** (the *seeing* is the new info)

The rule of thumb: **put the nucleus on the new/contrastive word.** If the
listener already knows part of the sentence, **de-stress it** (flatten its pitch,
shorten its vowel) and **move the pitch movement** onto the part they don't know.

ūüĒó This is the bridge to [SENTENCE STRESS](./SENTENCE_STRESS.md): sentence stress
decides *which words are strong vs weak*; intonation decides *which strong
syllable carries the pitch movement*. The two work together.

## 5. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
speaker hits on intonation ‚ÄĒ extend, don't replace, the seed rows from the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Vietnamese is a lexical-tone language** ‚ÄĒ pitch lives on *each syllable* and distinguishes *words*; there is no sentence-level intonation system to transfer. | Re-train pitch as a **phrase-level** event: let most of the sentence ride a **steady** pitch, then make **one** movement (‚Üė/‚Üó) on the **nucleus** (the last prominent syllable). |
| **Monotone English** ‚ÄĒ the per-syllable tone habit flattens the sentence melody, so statements/questions sound the same. | Exaggerate the **fall** on statements and the **rise** on yes/no questions until the contrast is obvious; then relax. Record and listen for the pitch movement. |
| **Carry-over of Vietnamese tones** ‚ÄĒ a rising Vietnamese word-tone (e.g. *sŠļĮc/ng√£*) lands on an English syllable and **distorts** the intended contour. | Treat English words as **tone-less** by default; assign tone only at the **phrase** level, on the nucleus. Drill minimal pairs: *"teacher"* said with no word-tone, only the sentence tone. |
| **Statement ends on a rise** ‚Üí sounds unsure / like still asking. | End **statements** and **Wh-questions** on a **fall ‚Üė**. The fall is the "I'm telling you" signal. |
| **Yes/no question ends on a fall** ‚Üí sounds blunt / like a demand ("You want coffee.‚Üė"). | End **yes/no questions** on a **rise ‚Üó**. The rise is the "over to you" signal. |
| **No listing contour** ‚ÄĒ every list item gets the same flat tone ‚Üí list sounds unfinished or ambiguous. | **Rise on each item except the last, fall on the last:** *apples ‚Üó, oranges ‚Üó, and grapes ‚Üė*. The final fall is what says "that's the whole list." |
| **Requests delivered on a fall** ‚Üí sound like commands / rude. | Soften requests with a **rise**: *Could you help me?‚Üó*. A fall on a request reads as an order. |
| **No fall-rise (‚Üė‚Üó) for implication** ‚ÄĒ flattens *"It's good‚Üó‚Ä¶"* to *"It's good.‚Üė"*, removing the "‚Ä¶but" and sounding like full praise. | Learn the **fall-rise** as the "‚Ä¶but" tone: pitch **down then up** on the nucleus (*It's ‚Üėgood‚Üó*). It is the core hedging/disagreement tool. |
| **Focus stress never moves** ‚ÄĒ nucleus always on the last word, so *"I saw a man"* can't distinguish "whom?" from "what happened?". | Practise **shifting the nucleus** to the new information: *I saw a ‚ÜėMAN* (whom?) vs *I ‚ÜėSAW a man* (what did you do?). De-stress everything the listener already knows. |
| **Surprise/echo not marked** ‚ÄĒ echoing *"She's a teacher?"* on a fall sounds like confirmation, not surprise. | An **echo question** takes a **rise** (often with widened pitch): *"She's a ‚ÜėTEACHER?‚Üó"* = "really?!" Tone + focus together. |
