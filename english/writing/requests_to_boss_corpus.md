> managing-up and upward-communication sources (First Round Review "A Tactical
> Guide to Managing Up", Harvard Business Review, MindTools "Managing Up",
> University of Minnesota HR "Managing Up: A Practical Approach"): an upward
> request **frames the benefit to the team/manager**, **offers two options**

> the balance — they swing to either extreme: *too deferential* (never make the
> ask → the request never lands) or *too blunt* (no options, no benefit frame).

## A. Move 1 — FRAME AS BENEFIT (justify the request before making it)

The opener. English upward requests lead with **why this helps the team or the
manager**, not with the requester's personal need. Confirmed across the
managing-up sources (First Round Review: "be a giver to your leader — frame the
ask in terms of what unblocks the work"; MindTools "Managing Up": "present
solutions, not problems"). The Cambridge *help* entry attests the pattern
directly: *"I feel that learning English will help my chances of promotion"* —
`[X] would help [Y]` is the dictionary's own grammar frame.

## B. Move 2 — OFFER OPTIONS (lower the manager's decision cost)

The move that makes an upward request *manageable*. English "managing up"
advises presenting **two concrete options** so the manager chooses, rather than
one take-it-or-leave-it demand (First Round Review: "bring your boss solutions
with options, not open-ended questions"). The Cambridge *either* entry attests
the pattern directly: *"We can either eat now or after the show"* — the
`either… or…` choice frame is the dictionary's own example.

## C. Move 3 — CLEAR ASK (the explicit, confident request)

The move that makes it a *request* and not a hint. English upward requests end
with **one clear, modal-softened ask** ("Would it be possible to…?", "Could I
get your approval to…") — deferential but unambiguous. The Cambridge *possible*
entry attests the pattern directly: *"Would it be possible to turn the music
down a little?"* — the `Would it be possible to…?` frame is the dictionary's
own example sentence.

## D. SOFTEN / DEFER (defer to the manager's authority — the face-saver)

The move that keeps the request *upward* rather than demanding. After a clear
ask, English "managing up" hands the decision back: *"I'd appreciate your
guidance"*, *"What do you think?"*, *"Happy to go with your call"*. This is the
*positive politeness* move that lets a manager save face while still receiving
a concrete request. The Cambridge *appreciate* entry attests the pattern: *"I
would appreciate it if you could let me know"* — `would appreciate` is the
dictionary's own "politely asking" sense. The Cambridge *guidance* entry
attests *"give/offer/provide guidance (to sb)"*.

**L1 interference (Vietnamese → English upward communication):**
- Hofstede cultural dimensions — Vietnam scores high on **power distance**
  (≈70): hierarchical communication norms make direct requests to seniors feel
  face-threatening (*mất thể diện*), biasing Vietnamese L1 writers toward
  over-deference (never making the ask) or, when pushed, bluntness.
- "Contrastive rhetoric" (Kaplan; Connor) — East-Asian L1 preference for
  indirect, context-heavy requests vs English's explicit-ask norm.
- See also `FINAL_CONSONANTS.md` §6 (Vietnamese L1 phonology) — the spoken
  sibling interference, here applied to written register.
