> **The principle (this bundle's thesis):** a collocation is a pairing of words
> that *sound right* together to a native speaker. The words are **NOT freely
> interchangeable** — you cannot swap *make* for *do* or *heavy* for *strong*
> just because the dictionary lists them as near-synonyms. Each chunk below is
> learned **as one fixed unit**, not assembled word-by-word.
>
> **Sources at the bottom of this file.** IPA spot-checks: each transcription was
> confirmed in ≥2 sources (a learner's dictionary + a collocation dictionary or a
> second dictionary).

## A. make vs do — the verb + noun split

The single most common collocation error for L2 learners (Vietnamese included).
**Both verbs mean roughly "perform / produce"**, so a word-by-word translation
collapses them — but English splits them along a fault line:

- **make** = *creating / producing / constructing* something that did not exist
  before (a decision, a plan, money, a mistake).
- **do** = *performing / completing* an activity, a task, a duty (homework,
  business, a favor, research).

There is no rule that predicts every case — that is the whole point of this
bundle. They are learned **as chunks**.

## B. adjective + noun — the "heavy vs strong" split

The second classic error: the learner reaches for a dictionary synonym (e.g.
*strong* = "mạnh") and attaches it to the nearest noun — but the noun **only
accepts one specific adjective**. `heavy rain` is right; `strong rain` is wrong,
even though *heavy* and *strong* are near-synonyms. The pairing is arbitrary and
language-specific.

> **Verification note:** `heavy smoker` has a dedicated Cambridge Dictionary

> Students of English lists `heavy` (not *strong*) under **rain**, **traffic**,
> and **smoker**; and `strong` (not *heavy*) under **wind**, **coffee**, and
> **influence**.
