## A. Ask to repeat / slow down (when the accent or speed beats you)

The first-line defence in any cross-cultural / ELF (English as a Lingua Franca)
call: you did not catch what was said, and the polite move is to signal it
*immediately* rather than smile-and-nod. Cambridge records `slow down` as a
phrasal verb "to move slower, or to cause someone or something to move slower"
and `catch` (sense) as "to hear or understand — *I didn't quite catch what she
said.*" Oxford Learner's glosses *catch* similarly: "to hear or understand
something."

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sorry, could you say that again? | polite request to repeat what was just said | /ˈsɒri kʊd juː seɪ ðæt əˈɡen/ UK · /ˈsɑːri kʊd juː seɪ ðæt əˈɡen/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/again | ≈#400 (of *again*) | US/UK |
| Could you slow down a bit? | ask a fast speaker to speak more slowly | /kʊd juː sləʊ daʊn ə bɪt/ UK · /kʊd juː sloʊ daʊn ə bɪt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/slow-down | ≈#700 (of *slow*) | US/UK |
| I didn't quite catch that. | "I didn't quite hear/understand that" — softer than "what?" | /aɪ ˈdɪdnt kwaɪt kætʃ ðæt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/catch | ≈#400 (of *catch*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `slow down` UK /sləʊ/ · US /sloʊ/ confirmed verbatim in
> the Cambridge phrasal-verb entry ("to move slower, or to cause someone or
> something to move slower"); `catch` /kætʃ/ confirmed in the Cambridge Essential
> English Dictionary and corroborated in the Speech Active consonant reference
> and the Biskra University phonetics lecture (minimal set `catch` /kætʃ/, `caught`
> /kɔːt/); `again` UK /əˈɡen/ · US /əˈɡen/ is the standard Cambridge transcription.
> The *quite* in *I didn't quite catch that* is a politeness hedge — its softening
> function is documented in Cambridge's grammar notes on hedging.

## B. Confirm meaning (check that you understood correctly)

The second move: you *think* you understood, but across accents and cultures it
is safer to paraphrase back. Cambridge records `mean` as "to express or represent
something such as an idea, thought, or fact" with the live examples *"You mean the
entire family?"* and *"Do you mean the one with short blond hair?"* — exactly the
checking construction. *Just to make sure* and *Are you saying* are the
diplomatic confirm frames.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Just to make sure I understood… | frame a confirm-back; "let me check I got this right" | /dʒʌst tə meɪk ʃʊə aɪ ˌʌndəˈstʊd/ UK · /dʒʌst tə meɪk ʃʊr aɪ ˌʌndərˈstʊd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/understand | ≈#120 (of *understand*) | US/UK |
| Do you mean…? | ask the speaker to confirm the intended meaning | /duː juː miːn/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/mean_1 | ≈#60 (of *mean*) | US/UK |
| Are you saying…? | restate the speaker's point as a yes/no confirm | /ɑː juː ˈseɪɪŋ/ UK · /ɑːr juː ˈseɪɪŋ/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/say | ≈#50 (of *say*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `mean` /miːn/ confirmed verbatim in the Cambridge verb
> entry, which itself prints the checking examples *"Do you mean the one with
> short blond hair?"* and *"You mean the entire family?"* — so *Do you mean…?* is
> a dictionary-attested construction, not an invention; `say` /seɪ/ → `saying`
> /ˈseɪɪŋ/ is the standard Cambridge form; `understood` UK /ˌʌndəˈstʊd/ · US
> /ˌʌndərˈstʊd/ confirmed in the Cambridge entry for *understand* (past form).

## C. Admit unfamiliarity politely (you don't know the term)

The third move, and the one Vietnamese learners fear most: admitting you don't
know a word or didn't catch a name. In an ELF setting this is normal and
respectful — the speaker expects accommodation. Cambridge records *be familiar
with* as "to know something or someone well" with the example *"I'm sorry, I'm
not familiar with your poetry."* Oxford records *spell* as "to say or write the
letters of a word in the correct order."

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'm not familiar with that term. | admit you don't know a word/concept — politely | /aɪm nɒt fəˈmɪl.i.ər wɪð ðæt tɜːm/ UK · /aɪm nɑːt fəˈmɪl.i.jɚ wɪθ ðæt tɜːrm/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/familiar | ≈#800 (of *familiar*) | US/UK |
| Could you spell that, please? | ask the speaker to spell a word/name aloud | /kʊd juː spel ðæt pliːz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/spell | ≈#900 (of *spell*) | US/UK |
| Let me read that back to you. | confirm a detail (number/spelling) by repeating it | /let miː riːd ðæt bæk tə juː/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/read | ≈#40 (of *read*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `familiar` UK /fəˈmɪl.i.ər/ · US /fəˈmɪl.i.jɚ/ confirmed
> verbatim in the Cambridge adjective entry, which itself prints *"I'm sorry, I'm
> not familiar with your poetry."* — so *I'm not familiar with…* is a
> dictionary-attested construction; `spell` /spel/ is the standard Cambridge form
> ("to say or write the letters of a word in the correct order"); `read` /riːd/
> is the standard Cambridge form, and *read back* ("to read aloud text that has
> been written") is the Cambridge *read* phrasal entry.
