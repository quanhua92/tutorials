## A. `prefer` — the verb and its three comparison patterns

`prefer` is the workhorse preference verb. The trap is its **complement**: it
takes **`to`** (not *than*) between two nouns/gerunds, **`over`** in informal
American usage, and a **to-infinitive** after `would prefer`. Confirmed in the
Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry for `prefer` (senses + Grammar
panel "Would prefer") and the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry
(`prefer something to something` / `prefer something over something` /
`prefer to do something`).

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I prefer X to Y. | I like X more than Y (the standard comparison) | /aɪ prɪˈfɜːr/ UK · /aɪ prɪˈfɝːr/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/prefer | ≈#600 (of *prefer*) | US/UK |
| I prefer X over Y. | I like X more than Y (esp. US, informal) | /aɪ prɪˈfɜːr ˈoʊvər/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/prefer | ≈#600 (of *prefer*) | US |
| I'd prefer to + infinitive | I would choose to (do something) | /aɪd prɪˈfɜːr tə/ UK · /aɪd prɪˈfɝːr tə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/prefer | ≈#600 (of *prefer*) | US/UK |
| I'd prefer not to… | I would rather not (polite refusal / hedging) | /aɪd prɪˈfɜːr nɒt tə/ UK · /aɪd prɪˈfɝːr nɑːt tə/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/prefer | ≈#600 (of *prefer*) | US/UK |

> **Real attestations (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - *"I prefer red wine to white."* — Cambridge, `prefer` entry.
> - *"I prefer jazz to rock music."* — Oxford, `prefer` → *prefer something to
>   something*.
> - *"Many people prefer streaming over other forms of consuming media."* —
>   Oxford, `prefer` → *prefer something over something*.
> - *"I'd prefer not to discuss this issue."* — Cambridge, `prefer` →
>   `[ + to infinitive ]`.
> - *"Would you prefer to pay by cash, cheque, or credit card?"* — Cambridge,
>   `prefer` example.
> - *"I'd prefer to wait here."* — Oxford, `prefer` → *Express Yourself:
>   Expressing a preference*.

> **Verification note:** `prefer` UK /prɪˈfɜːr/ · US /prɪˈfɝːr/ (-rr-) confirmed
> verbatim on the Cambridge `prefer` pronunciation line (web-fetched) and
> cross-checked against the Oxford `prefer` entry. The `to`-vs-`over`-vs-`than`
> complement rule is stated explicitly in both dictionaries' grammar panels:
> Cambridge "We can use a prepositional phrase with **to** when we compare two
> things"; Oxford lists `prefer something to something` and `prefer something
> over something` as distinct patterns and gives **no** `*prefer than*` pattern.

## B. `rather` — `would rather` (`'d rather`) and `rather than`

`would rather` (contracted `'d rather`) expresses preference with a **bare
infinitive** — never `*to*`. The comparative frame is **`rather … than`**
(not `*rather … to*`). When the preference is about *someone else's* action,
English slips into the past subjunctive: `I'd rather you didn't`. Confirmed in
the Cambridge `rather` entry (senses **rather than** B1 and **would rather** B1
+ the Grammar panel "Rather than: alternatives and preferences") and the Oxford
*Express Yourself* preference set.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'd rather + bare inf | I would prefer to (do something) | /aɪd ˈrɑːðər/ UK · /aɪd ˈræðər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/rather | ≈#120 (of *rather*) | US/UK |
| I'd rather X than Y. | I prefer doing X over Y (comparative frame) | /aɪd ˈrɑːðər … ðæn/ UK · /aɪd ˈræðər … ðæn/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/rather | ≈#120 (of *rather*) | US/UK |
| I'd rather you didn't. | I'd prefer it if you didn't (past subjunctive) | /aɪd ˈrɑːðər juː ˈdɪdnt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/rather | ≈#120 (of *rather*) | US/UK |
| rather than + X | instead of; used when comparing two options | /ˈrɑːðər ðæn/ UK · /ˈræðər ðæn/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/rather | ≈#120 (of *rather*) | US/UK |

> **Real attestations (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - *"I'd rather have a beer."* — Cambridge, `rather` → **would rather**.
> - *"I think I'd like to stay at home this evening rather than go out."* —
>   Cambridge, `rather` → **rather than**.
> - *"I'd rather go to the concert than the play."* — Oxford, `prefer` →
>   *Express Yourself: Expressing a preference*.
> - *"I think I'd rather stay in than go out tonight."* — Oxford, *Express
>   Yourself*.
> - *"Well, actually, I'd rather you didn't."* — Cambridge Advanced Learner's
>   Dictionary (collocated example under the `rather` grammar discussion).

> **Verification note:** `rather` UK /ˈrɑːðər/ · US /ˈræðər/ confirmed verbatim
> on the Cambridge `rather` pronunciation line (web-fetched; the dictionary
> prints UK /ˈrɑː.ðər/ and US /ˈræð.ɚ/). The bare-infinitive rule is stated in
> the Cambridge Grammar panel and corroborated by the Oxford Dictionary of
> English Grammar (`'I'd rather try than do nothing' — bare infinitive`).

## C. Softer / hedged preference — the indirect ways natives say it

Direct `I prefer` can sound blunt or categorical. Natives soften it with
identity framing (`I'm more of a … person`), tendency hedging (`I tend to
prefer`), inclination (`I lean toward`), and the comparative `I like X better
(than Y)`. These are the chunks that mark a learner as fluent the moment they
deploy them instead of the blunt `I want`. Attested in the Oxford `prefer`
collocations panel (adverbs `greatly / much`, `verb + prefer: would / tend to`),
the Oxford *Express Yourself* set, and the Cambridge `prefer` SMART Vocabulary
cluster ("Liking more").

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I'm more of a … person. | I tend to be the kind of person who likes … | /aɪm mɔːr əv ə … ˈpɜːsən/ UK · /aɪm mɔːr əv ə … ˈpɜːrsən/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/person | ≈#100 (of *person*) | US/UK |
| I tend to prefer… | I usually prefer (hedged, less categorical) | /aɪ tend tə prɪˈfɜːr/ UK · /aɪ tend tə prɪˈfɝːr/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/prefer | ≈#600 (of *prefer*) | US/UK |
| I lean toward… | I'm inclined to choose (still deciding) | /aɪ liːn təˈwɔːd/ UK · /aɪ liːn tɔːrd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/lean | ≈#1500 (of *lean*) | US/UK |
| I like X better (than Y). | I prefer X (comparative of *like*) | /aɪ laɪk … ˈbetər/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/better | ≈#70 (of *better*) | US/UK |

> **Real attestations (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - *"Industries still tend to prefer virgin raw materials to recycled ones."* —
>   Oxford, `prefer` → *Extra Examples*.
> - *"I like swimming better than jogging."* — Oxford, `prefer` → *Express
>   Yourself: Expressing a preference*.
> - *"I'm more of a morning person."* — attested usage of the `more of a …
>   person` frame (Cambridge `person` entry sense; widely documented in
>   corpus/YouGlish clips of the fixed phrase).
> - *"I'd rather go to the concert than the play."* — Oxford, *Express Yourself*
>   (the `rather … than` comparative softened by `I'd`).

> **Verification note:** `tend` /tend/, `lean` /liːn/, `better` /ˈbetər/,
> `person` UK /ˈpɜːsən/ · US /ˈpɜːrsən/ are the standard Cambridge
> transcriptions; `tend to prefer` is listed under the Oxford `prefer`
> collocations panel (`verb + prefer: would, tend to, appear to`).

## D. Neutral / no-preference replies — when you genuinely don't care

When there is no strong preference, the fluent move is a **neutral concession**,
not a blank "anything." These tokens signal "I defer; you decide" politely and
are the social-lubricant counterpart to the strong-preference chunks. Attested
in the Oxford *Express Yourself: Expressing a preference* set, which explicitly
labels the regional variants.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Either is fine. / Either way. | both options are OK with me | /ˈaɪðər/ UK · /ˈiːðər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/either | ≈#200 (of *either*) | US/UK |
| I don't mind. | I have no preference (esp. British) | /aɪ dəʊnt maɪnd/ UK · /aɪ doʊnt maɪnd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/mind | ≈#180 (of *mind*) | US/UK |
| (I'm) happy either way. | both are fine (esp. N. American) | /ˈhæpi ˈaɪðər weɪ/ UK · /ˈhæpi ˈiːðər weɪ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/prefer | — | US/UK |
| Whatever you like. | you choose (can read as dismissive — watch tone) | /wɒtˈevər/ UK · /wɑːtˈevər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/whatever | ≈#300 (of *whatever*) | US/UK |

> **Real attestations (verbatim from the sources above):**
> - *"I'm happy either way."* — Oxford, `prefer` → *Express Yourself* (labelled
>   "North American English").
> - *"I don't really mind whether we talk now or later."* — Oxford, *Express
>   Yourself* (labelled "British English").
> - *"It doesn't really matter to me whether we eat here or go out."* — Oxford,
>   *Express Yourself* (labelled "especially North American English").
> - *"I don't really care either way."* — Oxford, *Express Yourself*.

> **Verification note:** `either` UK /ˈaɪðər/ (also /ˈiːðər/) · US /ˈiːðər/
> confirmed on the Cambridge `either` pronunciation line — this is the famous
> transatlantic vowel difference learners must flag. `mind` /maɪnd/,
> `whatever` UK /wɒtˈevər/ · US /wɑːtˈevər/ are the standard Cambridge forms.
