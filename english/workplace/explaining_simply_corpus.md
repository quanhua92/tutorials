## A. Analogy openers (compare the unknown to the known)

The highest-value move when a non-technical colleague is lost: lead with a
concrete comparison, not a definition. Cambridge records *think of* as a phrasal
verb "to consider someone or something in a particular way" with the example
*"I think of him as someone who will always help me"* — and the imperative
analogy frame *Think of it like this…* is the standard native opener for
analogy-first explanation (attested across native speakers, e.g. Andrew Ng on
LinkedIn: *"Think of it like this: — Traditional code: You tell the
computer…"*). Cambridge records *similar* as "looking or being almost, but not
exactly, the same" and *imagine* as "to form or have a mental picture or idea of
something" with the live example *"Imagine Robert Redford when he was young —
that's what John looks like."*

## B. Simplification moves (strip the jargon, give the essence)

When the listener's eyes glaze over, the fix is to **stop the technical sentence
and restate in plain language**. Cambridge records *simply* as "in an easy way"
and prints the dictionary example *"To put it simply, we won't pay until we've
received the goods we ordered"* — a dictionary-attested simplification frame.
Cambridge lists the idiom *"to put it bluntly/simply/briefly, etc."* as a fixed
phrase. *Plain* is glossed as "honest and direct" / "clear," and *gist* as "the
most important pieces of information about something, or general information
without details" with the example *"Here's the gist: the answer is to be in
control."*

## C. Reframing moves (say it a second way when the first failed)

When the listener still hasn't understood, the move is not to repeat louder — it
is to **re-cast the idea in different words**. Cambridge records *in other
words* as the standard reformulation idiom (attested across the Cambridge
English Corpus, e.g. *"In other words, the average correlations … are most
similar"*). *mean* is glossed as "to express or represent something such as an
idea, thought, or fact," and *another* as "one more" — so *to put it another
way* is the canonical reframing frame, paired with Cambridge's idiom family *"to
put it bluntly/simply/briefly, etc."*

## D. Process moves (walk through "how it works" step by step)

When the concept is a mechanism (not a single idea), the move is to narrate the
**sequence**: name the process, then step through it. Cambridge records *work*
as "to do the job or activity that you are intended to do" and *first* as the
sequence marker. *The way it works is…* is the standard native opener for a
mechanism walkthrough; *First…, then…* is the canonical sequencing pair
(Cambridge lists *then* as "at that time" / "as a result").

| First…, then… | sequence a process into ordered steps | /fɜːst ðen/ UK · /fɜːrst ðen/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/first | ≈#50 (of *first*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *work* UK /wɜːk/ · US /wɜːrk/, *way* /weɪ/, *first* UK
> /fɜːst/ · US /fɜːrst/, *then* /ðen/ are the standard Cambridge transcriptions;
> *The way it works is…* is the standard native opener for a mechanism
