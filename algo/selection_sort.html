The minimum-swap sort. Each pass scans the unsorted tail for the smallest element (reads only), then makes one swap to put it at the front. At most n−1 swaps total — the fewest of any comparison sort — but always Θ(n²) comparisons with no best case. ② The invariants — always n(n−1)/2 comparisons, at most n−1 swaps

What to do: drag n. Comparisons grow as n² no matter what; swaps stay capped at n−1. That gap is the whole story: selection sort minimizes writes but maximizes reads.
Even a fully sorted input still pays all n(n−1)/2 comparisons — the scan can't know it's done. Compare with merge sort: Θ(n log n) comparisons on every input.

③ Complexity — best = average = worst = Θ(n²) (no fast path)

There is no best case: the order of the input does not change the comparison count. Only the swap count varies (0 on sorted input, up to n−1 on the worst).
Selection sort's niche: expensive writes (flash/EEPROM) and small n. It is also the conceptual ancestor of heapsort — "selection sort with a heap for the scan" — which turns Θ(n²) into Θ(n log n).
