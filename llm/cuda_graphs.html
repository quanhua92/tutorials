① The eager launch tax — N kernels, each ~10µs of pure CPU bookkeeping

What you're looking at: every red tick is one CPU→GPU kernel launch. Each costs the launch latency in driver overhead — bookkeeping that produces zero FLOPs. Drag N up: the tax grows linearly. At N=72, 10µs it is 720µs per step — over half a small model's decode step, all wasted on talking to the GPU.

② One graph per batch size — pick current batch, see which graph replays

graph_bs = [1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32] (nano-vllm's capture_cudagraph())
A captured graph bakes in its shape — a graph for bs=8 won't run bs=3. So we pre-capture a few sizes and pick the smallest captured graph ≥ current batch. The wasted slots (graph_bs − current_bs) are why the slot_mapping.fill_(−1) padding trick exists.

③ Eager vs graphed timeline — the launch tax collapses (GOLD: 144× cheaper launch)

graph replay ≈ 5µs (single launch)
Two timelines, same compute: the red launch chunk is what the graph deletes. The blue compute is identical in both. The LAUNCH speedup is pinned at 144× for the defaults (72 × 10 = 720µs → 5µs). The TOTAL speedup is smaller because compute is unchanged — and it shrinks as batch grows (panel ④).

④ Batch scaling — graphs help MOST at small batch, least at large batch

Launch overhead is fixed per step (same N kernels regardless of batch). Compute grows ~linearly with batch. So the launch tax's fraction of the step shrinks as batch grows → graphs help most at batch 1 (interactive latency), and barely at batch 64+. This is why serving engines graph the small-batch decode path and fall back to eager past a capture cap (e.g. >512).
