## A. Acknowledge the question (validate before you answer)

The professional reflex when a question lands: **name it as a good one** before
you tackle it. This buys ~2 seconds of thinking time *and* signals respect for
the asker. Confirmed as a taught Q&A move in presentation-skills references and
attested across COCA spoken.

## B. Buy time / defer (you can't answer right now)

When you genuinely don't have the answer on the spot, **defer gracefully** rather
than bluff or freeze. Defer = promise to return with it; it is professional, not
a weakness. Confirmed in business-English usage references (Chatterfox, Sapling)
and attested across COCA spoken / YouGlish.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| I'll get back to you on that | promises a later (often written) follow-up | /aɪl ˌɡet ˈbæk tə ju ɑːn ðæt/ US · /aɪl ˌɡet ˈbæk tə ju ɒn ðæt/ UK | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/get-back | ≈ high | US/UK |

## C. Hedge partial knowledge (you know *some* of it)

When you know part of the answer, **hedge before the part**: mark the limits of
what you're sure of so you're never over-claiming. These are the canonical
evidential/vagueness hedges. Confirmed in Collins (entry for "as far as I know"),
Wiktionary (for "off the top of one's head"), and the Cambridge Core linguistics
paper on evidential fragments.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| As far as I know | "to the extent of my knowledge — I may be wrong" | /əz ˌfɑːr əz aɪ ˈnoʊ/ US · /əz ˌfɑː əz aɪ ˈnəʊ/ UK | https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/as-far-as-i-know | ≈ high | US/UK |
| Off the top of my head | "from memory, without checking — approximate" | /ɑːf ðə ˈtɑːp əv maɪ ˈhed/ US · /ɒf ðə ˈtɒp əv maɪ ˈhed/ UK | https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/off_the_top_of_one%27s_head | ≈ mid | US/UK |
| To be honest, I'm not 100% sure | explicit partial-knowledge hedge; "100%" read "a hundred percent" | /tə bi ˈɑːnəst aɪm ˌnɒt ə ˈhʌndrəd pərˈsent ˈʃʊər/ US · /tə bi ˈɒnɪst aɪm ˌnɒt ə ˈhʌndrəd pəˈsent ˈʃɔː/ UK | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/honest | ≈ mid | US/UK |
| To the best of my knowledge | formal variant of "as far as I know" (written/meetings) | /tə ðə best əv maɪ ˈnɒlɪdʒ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/knowledge | ≈ mid | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `as far as I know` confirmed as a Collins COBUILD entry:
> *"to indicate that you are not absolutely sure of the statement… and you may be
> wrong. [vagueness]"* with the attested example *"It only lasted a couple of
> years, as far as I know."* `off the top of one's head` confirmed in Wiktionary
> with the attested example *"I cannot think of any good examples off the top of
> my head…"*. `honest` /ˈɒnɪst/ UK · /ˈɑːnəst/ US, `sure` /ʃɔː/ UK · /ʃʊr/ US,
> `knowledge` /ˈnɒlɪdʒ/ are the standard Cambridge/Oxford entries. The Cambridge
> Core journal *English Language and Linguistics* article "Last I heard"
> classifies "as far as I know" as an evidential hedge, corroborating its
> pragmatic function.

## D. Check back (confirm the answer landed)

After you answer, **close the loop**: invite the asker to confirm the answer was
enough. This is the Q&A analogue of `speech_acts/checking_understanding` and is
taught as standard presentation-closing behaviour.

> **Verification note:** `answer` /ˈɑːnsə/ UK · /ˈænsər/ US, `make sense` (idiom
