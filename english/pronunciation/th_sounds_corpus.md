## A. The two phonemes — what ⟨th⟩ actually is

English spells one digraph — **⟨th⟩** — for **two different phonemes**, both made
**tongue-between-teeth** (interdental) or tongue-on-back-of-upper-teeth (dental):

- **/θ/** — **voiceless** dental fricative. No vocal-cord buzz; a steady stream
  of air hisses between tongue and teeth. *think, three, bath.*
- **/ð/** — **voiced** dental fricative. Same tongue position, but the vocal
  cords **buzz**. *this, the, brother, with.*

> From the reference: "In English, the digraph ⟨th⟩ usually represents either
> the voiced dental fricative phoneme /ð/ (as in ***th**is*) or the voiceless
> dental fricative phoneme /θ/ (as in ***th**ink*)." — *Pronunciation of English
> ⟨th⟩*, Wikipedia. "Both are pronounced either interdentally, with the blade of
> the tongue resting against the lower part of the back of the upper teeth and
> the tip protruding slightly, or with the tip of the tongue against the back of
> the upper teeth."

These two phonemes are the **#1 set of English sounds that does not exist in
Vietnamese** at all — Vietnamese has no dental fricatives. (See §E + Sources for
the L1-interference research.) The two headwords below are the pinned,
sanity-checkable attestations this corpus MUST contain.

## C. /ð/ — voiced (mostly function words + medial). Real high-frequency words.

/ð/ is rarer as a *type* but far more frequent as a *token*, because the most
common words in English — *the, this, that, there, they, with* — all start or
end with it. Vietnamese substitutes /z/ (*this* → "zis") or /d/ (*this* → "dis").

## E. L1-interference research (Vietnamese → English)

Vietnamese has **no dental fricatives /θ ð/ at all**, so learners fall back on
the nearest permitted sounds. The substitution patterns below are **attested in
peer-reviewed / institutional EFL research on Vietnamese learners**, not
invented:

- **/θ/ → /t/** (often aspirated /tʰ/ or unreleased [t]): *think* → "tink",
  *three* → "tree", *bath* → "bat". Documented in: the SciSpace paper
  *Pronunciation of consonants /ð/ and /θ/ by adult Vietnamese EFL learners*
  ("The most outstanding problem in pronouncing the consonant /θ/ was replacing
  this sound by Vietnamese /t'/"), the Khanh study cited in the ResearchGate
  survey, and the Dalat University (DLU) consonant-difficulty paper.
- **/θ/ → /s/** (less common, register-dependent): *thick* → "sick",
  *thin* → "sin". Attested on the Quora consensus ("English learners often
  substitute /th/ or /s/ for θ").
- **/ð/ → /z/**: *this* → "zis", *they* → "zey". From the ResearchGate survey
  ("replace /ð/ with the voiced alveolar fricative /z/").
- **/ð/ → /d/** (often implosive /ɗ/): *this* → "dis", *brother* → "broder".
  From the Academia.edu + ResearchGate studies ("substitute /ð/ with the
  implosive /ɗ/").

> **Why tongue-between-teeth is unfamiliar:** Vietnamese stops /t/ and /ɗ/ are
> made with the tongue tip on the **alveolar ridge** (just behind the upper
> teeth), never protruding. English /θ ð/ require the tongue tip to go
> **between** or **behind** the upper teeth — a posture Vietnamese speakers
> never use, hence the persistent /t/ /d/ /s/ /z/ fallback. (Sources in §E +
> the Vietnamese-phonology references at the bottom.)
