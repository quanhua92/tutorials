5Writing task

Write a 2-line self-introduction — one casual, one formal. Include the uptake (Nice to meet you). Then reveal the model answer.

Model (match the register; always close with the uptake):

Casual: Hi, I'm Linh. Nice to meet you. /haɪ aɪm… ˌnaɪs tə ˈmiːt juː/ — contraction + uptake

Formal: Good morning. My name is Linh Tran, I'm a data analyst. Pleased to meet you. /ɡʊd ˈmɔːrnɪŋ… ˌpliːzd tə ˈmiːt juː/ — fuller self-ID + formal uptake

Both lines release the /t/ in meet and reduce to → /tə/. Never skip the uptake — it's the obligatory 2nd pair-part of an English introduction.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Pronoun-hierarchy reflex — reaches for anh/chị/em and freezes when English has no slot for itEnglish drops the hierarchy. Use Hi / How's it going? for anyone; the chunk itself signals register.
Literal "Where are you going?" as a greeting ("Anh đi đâu đấy?" is friendly in VN)Intrusive in English, not friendly. Use Hi / How's it going? / What's up?
Answers "How are you?" with a health reportCasual openers are social handshakes. Reply short + positive + bounce back: Good, you? / Not bad.
Skips the uptake Nice to meet you (the VN pronoun swap is the acknowledgement)The uptake is obligatory. Close every first-meeting with Nice to meet you / Pleased to meet you.
Answers How do you do? with "I'm fine, thank you"How do you do? is formulaic, not a question. Reply with the echo: How do you do?
Drops the final /t/ in meet → "Nice_tə_mee_(you)"Release the /t/: /ˌnaɪs tə ˈmiːt juː/. The /t/ is the chunk boundary.
Over-formalizes casual openers — "Hello, how are you today?" to a friendMatch the register. Friend → Hey, what's up? Colleague → Hi, how's it going?
No contraction habit → "I am… My name is…" (full forms)Default to the contraction: I'm… Casual English prefers contractions.
Stresses every word equally (syllable-timed VN rhythm) in How's it going?Stress content words — HOW's it GOing? — reduce the grammar.
