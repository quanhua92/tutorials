## A. BLUF openers — the bottom line up front

The BLUF convention (Bottom Line Up Front): state the key information — the ask,
the decision, the single most important point — in the **first sentence**, then
give context. Wikipedia attests it as "a standard in U.S. military communication
whose aim is to make military messages precise and lucid," originating in Army
Regulation 25-50. The Cambridge Learner's Dictionary attests the civilian form:
*"the bottom line" = "the most important fact in a situation."*

| TL;DR: | ultra-short summary tag ("too long; didn't read") — the whole thing in one line | /ˌtiːˌelˌdiːˈɑːr/ (read letter-by-letter) | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TL;DR | — | US/UK |
| In short,… | concise summary signaler — the quick version | /ɪn ˈʃɔːt/ UK · /ɪn ˈʃɔːrt/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/short | — | US/UK |
| The key takeaway is… | BLUF opener naming the single main message | /ðə kiː ˈteɪkəweɪ ɪz/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/takeaway | — | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** "the bottom line is" is attested verbatim in the
> Cambridge Learner's Dictionary entry for *the bottom line* with the model
> sentence "The bottom line is that if you don't work, you'll fail the test."
> "TL;DR" is attested in the Wikipedia "TL;DR" article ("short for 'too long;
> didn't read'… often used to introduce a summary of an online post or news
> article") and corroborated by Grammarly's usage guide. "In short" is the
> standard Cambridge idiom for "described using only a few words." "key takeaway"
> is attested in the Cambridge Business English entry for *takeaway*: "What's the
> **key takeaway** from this survey?" IPA: *bottom* /ˈbɒtəm/ UK · /ˈbɑːtəm/ US,
> *line* /laɪn/, *short* /ʃɔːt/ UK · /ʃɔːrt/ US, *takeaway* /ˈteɪkəweɪ/ confirmed
> in Cambridge Dictionary.

## C. The recommendation / proposed-action close

An executive brief ends with **one clear recommendation** — what to do, decided.
Cambridge attests the noun (*recommendation*) and the verb (*propose*) in a
business context; the university executive-summary guides attest the structure
(extract the major points → state the recommendation).

> /ˌrek.ə.menˈdeɪ.ʃən/) with the Business-English model sentences "We accept the
