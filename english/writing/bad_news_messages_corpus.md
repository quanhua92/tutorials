## A. Buffer ‚ÄĒ open neutral / positive (the cushion)

The first move of an indirect bad-news message is a **buffer**: a neutral or
mildly positive statement that cushions the news to come without misleading the
reader into expecting good news. Two openers carry the load ‚ÄĒ one names the
effort (gratitude), one names the process (deliberation).

## B. Reason ‚ÄĒ give a brief, blame-free cause

The reason move is one or two sentences ‚ÄĒ never longer than the news. It answers
"why?" once, naming a cause without pointing at a person or spotlighting the
rejection. Two openers carry almost the whole load.

## C. The bad news ‚ÄĒ deliver it clearly, not brutally

The move the whole message exists for. Cambridge's own dictionary examples nail
the full three-register ladder of this move, from soft (*I'm afraid*) to formal
(*We regret to inform you*) to firm (*We're unable to*). The rule, per Cruthers:
break the news **clearly** ‚ÄĒ never bury it ‚ÄĒ but de-emphasise it (subordinate
clause, passive voice once, mid-paragraph) so it does not read as a slap.
