## Why this bundle exists (read this first)

A Vietnamese learner writing an English proposal almost always makes the same
three mistakes, and all three are **rhetorical-culture** mistakes, not grammar
ones.

**Mistake 1 ‚Äî indirectness buries the ask.** Vietnamese persuasive writing is
**high-context and face-preserving** (*th·ªÉ di·ªán*, harmony). The Vietnamese
convention is to circle toward the request ‚Äî context first, reasoning, then a
soft, hedged ask (*"N·∫øu ƒë∆∞·ª£c th√¨‚Ä¶", "Anh/ch·ªã xem gi√∫p‚Ä¶"*). English
proposal culture is the **opposite**: it is **deductive** ‚Äî the recommendation
lands early and the ask is **explicit** ("I propose that we‚Ä¶", "I'm seeking
approval to‚Ä¶"). A proposal whose ask the reader has to *find* reads as weak or
evasive. Purdue OWL states the rule plainly: a proposal must name the problem
and its significance, then make the case.

**Mistake 2 ‚Äî modesty kills the benefits.** Vietnamese culture reads
self-promotion as *ki√™u ng·∫°o* (arrogance), so the writer **under-states the
payoff** (*"N√≥ c√≥ th·ªÉ gi√∫p m·ªôt ch√∫t"*) or lists benefits **without numbers**.
English proposal culture requires a **quantified benefit** ("This would result
in a 20% reduction‚Ä¶", "The expected impact is a 15-point gain‚Ä¶"). A benefit
with no number is read as hand-waving, not modesty.

**Mistake 3 ‚Äî no clear next step.** Vietnamese letters often end on a polite,
open note (*"Mong nh·∫≠n ƒë∆∞·ª£c ph·∫£n h·ªìi"*) that **does not commit anyone to
anything**. English proposals end with **one concrete ask + a next step**
("Could we pilot this for 30 days?", "Next steps: I draft the spec by Friday").
The ask is what turns a memo into a proposal.

This bundle teaches the four-move English skeleton that fixes all three:
**problem ‚Üí solution ‚Üí benefits ‚Üí ask**.

## 1. The mechanism: why English proposals are deductive

Vietnamese and English persuasive writing disagree on **where the main point
goes**:

| | Vietnamese L1 convention | English target |
|---|---|---|
| Thesis placement | **Inductive** ‚Äî context/reasoning first, request last, softly | **Deductive** ‚Äî recommendation up front, ask explicit |
| Tone of the ask | Hedged, indirect ("N·∫øu ƒë∆∞·ª£c th√¨‚Ä¶") | Direct but polite ("I propose that we‚Ä¶", "I'm seeking approval to‚Ä¶") |
| Evidence | Trait-list ("hardworking, careful") | Quantified result ("a 20% reduction") |
| Closing | Open, polite, no commitment | One concrete ask + dated next step |
| Self-presentation | Modest (face, harmony) | Confident, evidence-led |

So when a Vietnamese writer maps the L1 convention onto English, the result is
a proposal where the recommendation is **buried**, the benefit is **unquantified**,
and the ask **does not exist**. The reader finishes and thinks *"so‚Ä¶ what do
you want me to do?"* ‚Äî the exact failure mode.

> |---|---|---|
> | Problem | **The challenge we're facing** | /√∞…ô Ààt É√¶l…™nd í w…™…ô Ààfe…™s…™≈ã/ |
> | Solution | **I propose that we** | /a…™ pr…ôÀàp…ô äz √∞√¶t wi/ (US /pr…ôÀàpo äz/) |

## 2. Move 1 ‚Äî PROBLEM: name it, don't hint it

English proposals open by **naming the problem in one line** ‚Äî not circling it.
The two highest-frequency openers:

- **"The challenge we're facing is‚Ä¶"** ‚Äî formal, frames a difficulty as a
  shared challenge (not blame).
- **"Currently, we struggle with‚Ä¶"** ‚Äî present-tense, anchors the pain in a
  concrete process.

> From `proposals_corpus.md`:
>
> - **The challenge we're facing** /√∞…ô Ààt É√¶l…™nd í w…™…ô Ààfe…™s…™≈ã/ ‚Äî the formal
>   opener naming a current difficulty.
> - **Currently, we struggle with** /Ààk år…ôntli wi Ààstr å…°l w…™√∞/ ‚Äî present-tense
>   framing of a pain point.
> - **The problem we're facing** /√∞…ô Ààpr…íbl…ôm w…™…ô Ààfe…™s…™≈ã/ ‚Äî the standard
>   problem-statement opener.
> - **The issue is** /√∞i Àà…™ ÉuÀê …™z/ ‚Äî a blunt one-line problem statement.

**The Vietnamese trap:** the L1 instinct is to soften ("There might be a small
difficulty‚Ä¶") or to delay the problem to paragraph three. English readers want
the problem in **sentence one**, with a number if possible (*"turnover hit 22%
last quarter"*).

## 3. Move 2 ‚Äî SOLUTION: lead with an active verb

This is the move that makes it a *proposal*. The recommendation uses a
**clear, active verb** ‚Äî `propose`, `recommend`, `should` ‚Äî placed early.

> From `proposals_corpus.md`:
>
> - **I propose that we** /a…™ pr…ôÀàp…ô äz √∞√¶t wi/ (US /pr…ôÀàpo äz/) ‚Äî the canonical
>   proposal-move opener (a subjunctive verb follows: *"I propose that we
>   pilot this"*).
> - **We should** /wi  É…ôd/ ‚Äî the shortest recommendation verb.
> - **My recommendation is** /ma…™ Àårek…ômenÀàde…™ Én …™z/ ‚Äî a noun-headed, confident
>   recommendation.
> - **The solution is to** /√∞…ô s…ôÀàluÀê Én …™z t…ô/ ‚Äî the problem‚Üísolution bridge.

**Pinned real example (sanity-check the attestation):** the Oxford Advanced
Learner's Dictionary entry for **propose** carries the grammar pattern
**`propose that‚Ä¶`** with the real sentence *"She proposed that the book be
banned."* ‚Äî so `I propose that we‚Ä¶` is the first-person-plural instantiation of
a dictionary-attested pattern, not an invented phrase. IPA `/pr…ôÀàp…ô äz/` (UK),
US `/pr…ôÀàpo äz/`, fetched from the Oxford entry on 2026-06-23.

**The Vietnamese trap:** the L1 instinct is to bury the recommendation
(*"C√≥ l·∫Ω ch√∫ng ta c√≥ th·ªÉ c√¢n nh·∫Øc‚Ä¶"* ‚Üí "Maybe we could consider‚Ä¶"). English
readers read a hedged recommendation as **no recommendation**. The fix is not
to be rude ‚Äî it is to let the verb carry the confidence and keep one softener
at most (*"I propose that we pilot this"* ‚Äî confident; *"I was wondering if
maybe we could possibly consider‚Ä¶"* ‚Äî broken).

## 4. Move 3 ‚Äî BENEFITS: quantify, don't assert

The move Vietnamese L1 writers omit most. English proposals **quantify** the
payoff ‚Äî a number, a percentage, a concrete outcome.

> From `proposals_corpus.md`:
>
> - **The benefits of this approach** /√∞…ô Ààben…™f…™ts …ôv √∞…™s …ôÀàpr…ô ät É/ (US
>   /…ôÀàpro ät É/) ‚Äî the standard benefits-statement opener.
> - **This would result in** /√∞…™s w äd r…™Ààz ålt …™n/ ‚Äî the conditional benefit
>   verb (past-tense hedged = "this is a projection, not a promise").
> - **The expected impact is** /√∞i …™kÀàspekt…™d Àà…™mp√¶kt …™z/ ‚Äî the
>   quantified-impact opener.
> - **The ROI** /√∞i …ëÀêr a…™ Àào ä/ ‚Äî the return-on-investment headline.

**Pinned real example (sanity-check):** the Oxford *benefit* entry carries the
real sentence *"the benefits of modern medicine"* and *"This deal will offer
major benefits to industrialists"* ‚Äî the `the benefits of [X]` pattern is
dictionary-attested, and `this approach` is the Oxford *approach* example

**The Vietnamese trap:** the L1 modesty instinct produces *"It might help a
little"* or a benefit with no number. English readers want *"This would result
in a 20% reduction in turnover"* ‚Äî number first. No number = no credibility.

## 5. Move 4 ‚Äî ASK: one clear request + a next step

The move that turns a memo into a proposal. End with **one concrete ask** and
**one dated next step**.

> From `proposals_corpus.md`:
>
> - **I'm seeking approval to** /a…™m ÀàsiÀêk…™≈ã …ôÀàpruÀêvl t…ô/ ‚Äî the formal,
>   confident ask.
> - **I'd like your sign-off on** /a…™d la…™k j…îÀê Ààsa…™n …íf …ín/ ‚Äî a confident
>   but warmer ask.
> - **Could we pilot this** /k äd wi Ààpa…™l…ôt √∞…™s/ ‚Äî a low-risk, option-giving
>   ask (the "pilot" is the single most persuasive proposal move).
> - **Next steps** /nekst steps/ ‚Äî the standard closing header.

**The Vietnamese trap:** the L1 closing is polite and open (*"Mong nh·∫≠n ƒë∆∞·ª£c
ph·∫£n h·ªìi"*). English readers want to know **exactly what you want them to do,
by when**. A proposal that ends "please consider" commits no one to anything.

## 7. Vietnamese ‚Üí English L1 pitfalls table

The "expert payoff." These are the specific interference traps a Vietnamese
writer hits on proposals ‚Äî extend, don't replace, the seed rows from the spec.

| Vietnamese trap (what you do) | English fix (what to do instead) |
|---|---|
| **Inductive structure buries the recommendation** ‚Äî context for 3 paragraphs, ask at the very end, softly | **Deductive**: put the recommendation in the first or second sentence. BLUF ‚Äî *"I propose that we pilot remote work"* ‚Äî then justify. üîó See [EMAIL ANATOMY](./EMAIL_ANATOMY.md). |
| **Hedges the ask away** ‚Äî *"N·∫øu ƒë∆∞·ª£c th√¨‚Ä¶"* ‚Üí "If possible, maybe you could consider‚Ä¶" | Make **one explicit ask**: *"I'm seeking approval to‚Ä¶"* / *"Could we pilot this‚Ä¶"*. One softener max; let the verb carry it. |
| **Modesty ‚Üí under-quantifies benefits** ‚Äî *"n√≥ gi√∫p m·ªôt ch√∫t"* / lists traits not numbers | Quantify the payoff: *"This would result in a 20% reduction"* / *"The expected impact is +15 points"*. No number = no credibility. |
| **No concrete next step** ‚Äî ends "mong nh·∫≠n ƒë∆∞·ª£c ph·∫£n h·ªìi" (please reply) | End with **one dated next step**: *"Next steps: I draft the spec by Friday."* The ask is what makes it a proposal. |
| **Face-saving softens the problem** ‚Äî won't name the difficulty plainly | Name it in sentence one: *"The challenge we're facing is‚Ä¶"* / *"Currently, we struggle with‚Ä¶"*. Framing it as a shared *challenge* (not blame) keeps face while staying direct. |
| **Generic trait-list, no evidence** ‚Äî "hardworking, responsible, careful" | Replace traits with **one quantified result**: *"cut churn by 18%"*. Evidence > adjectives. üîó See [CV / R√âSUM√â BULLETS](./CV_BULLETS.md). |
| **Translates L1 formal filler** ‚Äî "K√≠nh g·ª≠i‚Ä¶ Tr√¢n tr·ªçng‚Ä¶" word-for-word into stiff English | Use the **proposal register**, not ceremonial filler: *"I propose‚Ä¶ The benefits‚Ä¶ I'm seeking approval‚Ä¶"*. Formal ‚âÝ ornate. |
| **Over-hedges with "I think / maybe"** ‚Äî *"I think maybe it could possibly help"* | Calibrate to **one hedge**: *"This would likely result in‚Ä¶"*. Three hedges in one clause reads as no confidence. üîó See [EDITING: HEDGING](./EDITING_HEDGING.md) (#61). |
| **Copula / article / plural slips carry into writing** ‚Äî "it good idea", "we should do pilot program" | Proofread the proposal for **dropped articles, missing plurals, no copula** ‚Äî the spoken L1 traps (üîó [FINAL CONSONANTS](../pronunciation/FINAL_CONSONANTS.md) ¬ß6) leak into writing too. |

## How to practise this bundle (the daily 20 min)

1. **READ** (5 min) ‚Äî this guide, ¬ß1‚Äì¬ß5.
2. **SHADOW** (7 min) ‚Äî open `proposals.html`, drill the 8 flip cards + the
   role-play **aloud**; exaggerate the stress on *propose*, *recommendation*,
   *benefits*, *approval*.
3. **PRODUCE** (8 min) ‚Äî the writing task: write a **mini-proposal** (problem
   + solution + benefits + ask), one sentence per move, with at least **one
   number** in the benefits. Then reveal the model answer and self-score
   against the 4-move skeleton.
