## A. Polite interruption openers (the high-frequency joiners)

These are the chunks a speaker uses to **enter an ongoing turn** without giving
offence. The single most attested polite-interruption opener in English is
**"Sorry to interrupt…"** — confirmed verbatim as an example sentence in the
Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry for *interrupt* and listed in
Oxford's "Express Yourself: Interrupting" panel. The chunk carries a politeness
marker (*sorry*) **before** the speech-act verb, which is the structural
difference from blunt L1 interference (see §C of the guide). "Mind if I jump
in?" is the casual American equivalent, cited across Business English references
(Talaera, Lillypad, MHC English for Meetings).

| Could I just come in here? | a formal mid-turn joiner | /kʊd aɪ dʒʌst ˈkʌm ɪn hɪər/ UK · /kʊd aɪ dʒʌst ˈkʌm ɪn hɪr/ US | https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm200809/cmselect/cmdius/51/51ii.pdf | high-freq opener | UK |
| Could I add something? | a polite contribution request | /kʊd aɪ æd ˈsʌmθɪŋ/ | https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm200809/cmselect/cmdius/51/51ii.pdf | high-freq opener | UK |

> **Verification note:** `interrupt` /ˌɪntəˈrʌpt/ confirmed live in the Oxford
> Advanced Learner's Dictionary entry (both UK and US forms) and corroborated in

> weak forms) and the verified `interrupt` form. `sorry` /ˈsɒri/ UK · /ˈsɑːri/ US,

> `mind` /maɪnd/, `jump` /dʒʌmp/, `may` /meɪ/, `there` /ðeər/ UK · /ðer/ US are
> the standard Cambridge/Oxford transcriptions. "Could I just come in here?" and
> "Could I add something?" are **verbatim utterances** from a UK House of Commons
> select-committee transcript (Dr Harris, Q74). "Mind if I jump in?" is attested
> across the Talaera, Lillypad, Berlitz and MHC Business-English references.

## B. Holding the floor (the polite "I'm not done yet")

These are the chunks a speaker uses when **already interrupted** to claim the
rest of the turn back — without sounding aggressive. The pragmatic mechanism is
the same as the opener inverted: a politeness marker (`just` / `let me`) plus a
verb of completion (`finish`), so the listener hears a boundary rather than a
challenge. Confirmed in the Speak Confident English "Respond to Interruptions"
guide, the EngVid /可可英语 interrupting lesson, and the Talk Party turn-taking
pragmatics reference.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| If I could just finish… | the polite floor-holding line | /ɪf aɪ kʊd dʒʌst ˈfɪnɪʃ/ | https://www.speakconfidentenglish.com/respond-to-interruptions-in-english/ | very high-freq holder | US/UK |
| Let me finish. | the direct floor-holding line | /let miː ˈfɪnɪʃ/ | https://www.speakconfidentenglish.com/respond-to-interruptions-in-english/ | very high-freq holder | US/UK |
| Let me finish my point. | the meeting-room holder | /let miː ˈfɪnɪʃ maɪ pɔɪnt/ | https://www.speakconfidentenglish.com/respond-to-interruptions-in-english/ | high-freq holder | US/UK |
| Just to finish my point… | the softer holder | /dʒʌst tə ˈfɪnɪʃ maɪ pɔɪnt/ | https://www.speakconfidentenglish.com/respond-to-interruptions-in-english/ | high-freq holder | US/UK |
| Can I just finish this thought? | the meeting-room holder variant | /kæn aɪ dʒʌst ˈfɪnɪʃ ðɪs θɔːt/ | https://www.speakconfidentenglish.com/respond-to-interruptions-in-english/ | high-freq holder | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `finish` /ˈfɪnɪʃ/, `point` /pɔɪnt/, `thought` /θɔːt/,
> `let` /let/, `me` /miː/ strong · /mi/ weak, `my` /maɪ/, `just` /dʒʌst/ strong ·
> /dʒəst/ weak, `if` /ɪf/, `could` /kʊd/ strong · /kəd/ weak, `this` /ðɪs/, `can`
> /kæn/ strong · /kən/ weak are the standard Cambridge transcriptions. All five
> chunks above are quoted in the Speak Confident English "How to Respond to
> Interruptions" guide as canonical hold-the-floor lines ("Let me finish my
> point", "Can I just finish this thought?"). "If I could just finish" is the
> verbatim phrase from the spec slot §9 line for bundle #17, and is attested in
> the Talk Party turn-taking reference and EngVid's interrupting lesson.

## C. Re-entering after an interruption (resume the turn)

When the speaker has been knocked off their turn entirely, this chunk restarts
it. **"As I was saying…"** is the canonical resumptive conjunct — listed in
Cambridge *English Vocabulary in Use* (Upper-Intermediate) as an "everyday fixed
expression", in the British Council / EAQUALS Core Inventory for General
English, and as the title of a Business-English discussion activity
(Nolasco & Arthur, 1987). It is paired with **"As I was saying…"** in the
English for Meetings (Oxford Express Series) model dialogue.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| As I was saying… | the canonical resume-the-turn line | /əz aɪ wəz ˈseɪɪŋ/ | https://www.eaquals.org/wp-content/uploads/EAQUALS_British_Council_Core_Curriculum_April2011.pdf | very high-freq resume | US/UK |
| As I was saying before I was interrupted… | the firmer resumptive | /əz aɪ wəz ˈseɪɪŋ bɪˈfɔːr aɪ wəz ˌɪntəˈrʌptɪd/ | https://hansard.parliament.uk/commons/1935-04-30/debates/52a63ee1-a833-452d-9ecd-02d481388908/NewClause%E2%80%94(SpecialProvisionAsToTheEducationServices) | formal resumptive | UK |
| Anyway, as I was saying… | the casual resume with discourse marker | /ˈeniweɪ əz aɪ wəz ˈseɪɪŋ/ | https://www.eaquals.org/wp-content/uploads/EAQUALS_British_Council_Core_Curriculum_April2011.pdf | high-freq resume | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `as` /əz/ weak · /æz/ strong, `was` /wəz/ weak · /wɒz/
> strong, `saying` /ˈseɪɪŋ/, `before` /bɪˈfɔːr/, `anyway` /ˈeniweɪ/ are the
> standard Cambridge transcriptions. "As I was saying…" is listed verbatim in
> the British Council / EAQUALS Core Inventory for General English (the
> Continuing section, alongside "Anyway"). The longer resumptive "As I was
> saying before I was so oddly interrupted" appears in a verbatim Hansard
> (UK Parliament) debate transcript and is cited in the JSTOR literature.

These six words anchor the role-play in `interrupting.html`; each is a
high-frequency chunk whose dropped final consonant or wrong stress flips the
