## A. STEP 1 — Notice + acknowledge the misunderstanding

The repair begins the moment you **sense** the other person has the wrong end of
the stick. The native move is to **take responsibility for the clarity** ("I
didn't make myself clear"), not to blame the listener. These three are the
high-frequency openers.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| That's not quite what I meant | polite correction: "you understood me slightly wrong" | /ðæts nɒt kwaɪt wɒt aɪ ment/ UK · /ðæts nɑːt kwaɪt wɑːt aɪ ment/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/meant | ≈#1200 (of *mean*) | US/UK |
| Sorry, I didn't make myself clear | I accept that I was unclear (not your fault) | /ˈsɒri aɪ ˈdɪdnt meɪk maɪˈself klɪə/ UK · /ˈsɑːri aɪ ˈdɪdənt meɪk maɪˈself klɪr/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/clear | ≈#200 (of *clear*) | US/UK |
| I don't think that came across | my message didn't reach you the way I intended | /aɪ dəʊnt θɪŋk ðæt keɪm əˈkrɒs/ UK · /aɪ doʊnt θɪŋk ðæt keɪm əˈkrɔːs/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/come-across | ≈#400 (of *come across*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *mean* → *meant* /ment/ is the standard Cambridge past
> form; *clear* /klɪə/ UK · /klɪr/ US is the standard Cambridge transcription;
> the phrasal verb *come across* /ˌkʌm əˈkrɒs/ UK · /ˌkʌm əˈkrɔːs/ US is
> attested in the Cambridge entry cited. The pragmatic function (self-blame
> opener) is documented in communication-repair references (see Sources) and in
> the Oxford English Grammar Course *Advanced* gambits chapter.

## B. STEP 2 — Rephrase (don't repeat louder)  ⭐ PINNED ANCHORS

The **discipline of repair**: never repeat the same words louder — **say it in
different words**. These five are the workhorses. The two **pinned** chunks
(bolded ⭐) are the ones the corpus must contain verbatim so the attestation is
sanity-checkable.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ⭐ **Let me put it another way** | I'll express this differently | /let mi pʊt ɪt əˈnʌðə weɪ/ UK · /let mi pʊt ɪt əˈnʌðər weɪ/ US | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/another | ≈#250 (of *another*) | US/UK |
| Let me rephrase that | let me say it again in clearer words | /let mi ˌriːˈfreɪz ðæt/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/pronunciation/english/rephrase | ≈#4000 (of *rephrase*) | US/UK |
| ⭐ **What I'm trying to say is…** | the point I want to make is… | /wɒt aɪm ˈtraɪɪŋ tə seɪ ɪz/ UK · /wɑːt aɪm ˈtraɪɪŋ tə seɪ ɪz/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/try | ≈#150 (of *try*) | US/UK |
| To clarify… | to make this clearer (slightly formal) | /tə ˈklærəfaɪ/ | https://www.oxfordlearnersdictionaries.com/definition/english/clarify | ≈#3000 (of *clarify*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note (pinned anchors — sanity check these are real):**
> - ⭐ *Let me put it another way* — attested as a **rephrasing gambit** in the
>   *Oxford English Grammar Course — Advanced* (Swan) gambits chapter ("Let me
>   put it another way"), mirrored in the University of Babylon humanities
>   gambits reference and the ResearchGate *Gambits in a New Light* paper.
> - ⭐ *What I'm trying to say is…* — attested in the same University of Babylon
>   gambits list (paired with *Let me put it another way*) and in the
>   communication-repair references (English with Kim; Judy Ringer, *Being
>   Heard*).
> - *rephrase* /ˌriːˈfreɪz/ — confirmed in Cambridge (US/ˌriːˈfreɪz/,
>   UK/ˌriːˈfreɪz/), Oxford, Collins, and Britannica (≥4 sources).
> - *clarify* /ˈklærəfaɪ/ — confirmed in Oxford (with the live example
>   *"She asked him to clarify what he meant"*); Cambridge mirrors the form.
> - Connected-speech reductions: *Let me* → /lemi/ ("lemme"), *to* → /tə/,
>   *trying to* → /ˈtraɪɪŋ tə/. These are standard documented reductions
>   (Cambridge connected-speech notes); the citation form above is the
>   retrievable form the learner practises first.

## C. STEP 3 — Check understanding (close the repair loop)

A repair isn't finished until you **hand back** to the listener and confirm the
new version landed. These three are the high-frequency closers.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Does that make more sense? | is the new version clearer than before? | /dʌz ðæt meɪk mɔː sens/ UK · /dʌz ðæt meɪk mɔːr sens/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sense | ≈#300 (of *sense*) | US/UK |
| Is that clearer? | is the point clearer now? | /ɪz ðæt ˈklɪərə/ UK · /ɪz ðæt ˈklɪrər/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/clear | ≈#200 (of *clear*) | US/UK |
| Does that make sense now? | do you follow me at this point? | /dʌz ðæt meɪk sens naʊ/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/make-sense | ≈#300 (of *make sense*) | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** *make sense* /meɪk sens/ and *sense* /sens/ are the
> standard Cambridge forms; *clear* comparative *clearer* /ˈklɪərə/ UK ·
> /ˈklɪrər/ US is the standard Cambridge transcription. The pragmatic function
> (comprehension check that closes a repair) is documented in the
> communication-repair references in Sources.
