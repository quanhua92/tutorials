5Writing task

Rewrite one sentence at 3 formality levels (formal / neutral / casual). Keep every content word on the right rung. Then reveal the model.

Model — same intent, three rungs (keep each line on one rung):

FORMAL (Latinate, full forms, to a client):
I would like to request the quarterly report at your convenience.

NEUTRAL (modal + contraction, default workplace):
Could you send it over when you have a moment?

CASUAL (Germanic verbs, to a close teammate):
Hey — can you get me that report?

Every content word stays on its rung: request/send/get, would/can, convenience/moment/that.

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Maps Hán-Việt ↔ English unevenly — stacks a Latinate word on a casual frame: "I endeavor to obtain a coffee."English formal words are marked formal. For coffee with a friend, use the Germanic twin: "I'm trying to get a coffee." Pick the rung by audience, not by which word you studied first.
Mixed register within one sentence — Germanic verb + Latinate adjective: "The manager get the sufficient funds."Keep the whole utterable on one rung. Casual → get + enough; formal → obtains + sufficient.
Over-formal in casual speech — schools teach Latinate words first, so you say "I purchased a snack" to a friend.Default to the Germanic twin in spoken casual English (buy, get, help, start, enough, ask). Reserve Latinate twins for clients, seniors, writing.
Under-formal in formal contexts — defaults to the Germanic twin and writes "We need to end the contract" in a legal document.In writing / to seniors, climb to the Latinate twin: "The contract shall terminate." / "We require…"
Sprinkles Latinate words to sound "smart" — "utilize," "facilitate," "endeavor" in casual chat.Register is about fit, not prestige. The fluent speaker uses buy 95% of the time and purchase only when the setting climbs.
Skips the dictionary's [formal] tag — treats commence/sufficient/terminate as plain synonyms of start/enough/end.Read the register tag. A tagged word is the Latinate twin — formal rung only. The untagged twin is the everyday default.
Translates "xưng hô" pronoun-respect (anh/chị/em) onto English by over-formalizing everyone.English marks respect with vocabulary + full forms, not pronouns. To a senior: could you / would you + Latinate twin. The pronoun is always you.
No awareness of the 1066 history — doublets look arbitrary, so you memorise synonyms not register pairs.Learn pairs as twins: buy↔purchase, get↔obtain, help↔assist, start↔commence. The history is the mnemonic.
