## A. The flagship pair — /iː/ vs /ɪ/ (length is phonemic)

The most famous English minimal pair. The two words share the exact same
consonant frame `/ʃ ___ p/`; **only the vowel length (and the tiny tense/lax
quality) changes** — and that single difference flips the meaning. Pinned example
the bundle is built around.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| sheep | a farm animal that gives wool | /ʃiːp/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/sheep | ≈#3000 | US/UK |
| ship | a large boat | /ʃɪp/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/ship | ≈#700 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `sheep` /ʃiːp/ and `ship` /ʃɪp/ confirmed in Cambridge
> Advanced Learner's Dictionary (entries above), cross-checked against the BBC
> Learning English minimal-pairs lesson ("ship /ʃɪp/ ... sheep /ʃiːp/") and the
> Cambridge Dictionary social post ("Listen to this pair: ship /ʃɪp/ sheep
> /ʃiːp/. The only difference is the vowel length"). The long /iː/ carries the
> two-dot length mark in every source.

## B. More /iː/ (long, tense) vs /ɪ/ (short, lax) pairs

The same contrast, in the highest-frequency words a learner actually says. Each
pair is a minimal pair: identical consonants, meaning changed by the vowel.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| feel | to perceive / experience through touch or emotion | /fiːl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/feel | ≈#200 | US/UK |
| fill | to make full / put something into until full | /fɪl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/fill | ≈#1500 | US/UK |
| leave | to go away / depart | /liːv/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/leave | ≈#200 | US/UK |
| live | to be alive / reside | /lɪv/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/live | ≈#60 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `feel` /fiːl/ vs `fill` /fɪl/ is the canonical
> "fill–feel merger" minimal pair (documented in Wikipedia's *English-language
> vowel changes before historical /l/* as `fill fɪl` / `feel fiːl`). `leave`
> /liːv/ and `live` /lɪv/ are standard Cambridge transcriptions; the length mark
> on `leave` (and its absence on `live`) is the sole contrast between "depart"
> and "reside".

## C. /uː/ (long, tense) vs /ʊ/ (short, lax) — the "oo" pair

The back-vowel mirror of sheep/ship. Same consonant frame `/p ___ l/`; long
/uː/ = a swimming pool, short /ʊ/ = the verb to pull. Vietnamese has no
/ʊ/-/uː/ contrast, so this pair collapses to one quality for most learners.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| pool | a small area of still water / swimming pool | /puːl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/pool | ≈#1500 | US/UK |
| pull | to bring toward you using force | /pʊl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/pull | ≈#1000 | US/UK |
| fool | a person who acts unwisely | /fuːl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/fool | ≈#2500 | US/UK |
| full | containing as much as possible | /fʊl/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/full | ≈#300 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `pool` /puːl/ vs `pull` /pʊl/ confirmed across
> Cambridge-based teaching sources, the *students' problems in pronouncing short
> and long English vowels* study (Tanjungpura University, lists
> `Pool [puːl] ... Pull [pʊl]`), and Wikipedia's *English-language vowel changes
> before historical /l/* (which documents the regional "pull–pool merger" as
> proof the two are distinct in the standard accent). `fool` /fuːl/ and `full`
> /fʊl/ are the standard Cambridge forms.

## D. /æ/ vs /e/ — "bad" vs "bed"

A second length-plus-quality contrast a learner can hear inside one syllable:
the more open, longer /æ/ vs the closer, shorter /e/. Vietnamese has no /æ/
phoneme, so it is almost always replaced by /e/ — *bad* becomes *bed*.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| bad | of poor quality / not good | /bæd/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/bad | ≈#110 | US/UK |
| bed | a piece of furniture for sleeping on | /bed/ | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/bed | ≈#900 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** `bad` /bæd/ vs `bed` /bed/ is the canonical /æ/-/e/
> minimal pair listed by EnglishClub (*Minimal Pair /æ/ and /e/ bad bed*) and
> featured in the Cambridge English minimal-pairs set. The open /æ/ vs the
> closer /e/ is the documented distinction.

## E. /ɜː/ (long, stressed) vs /ə/ (short, unstressed) — strong/weak forms

The long central vowel /ɜː/ and the schwa /ə/ are the same basic vowel
**quality** — the only differences are **length and stress**. /ɜː/ appears in
stressed syllables; /ə/ in unstressed ones. Cambridge dictionaries record both
forms for many grammar words. Vietnamese learners ignore the length difference
and fully stress grammar words, so they never produce the natural weak /ə/.

| English chunk | meaning | IPA | source URL | frequency rank | accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| her | object pronoun / possessive determiner (she) | /hɜː/ strong · /hə/ weak · /hər/ weak US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/her | ≈#25 | US/UK |
| were | past plural of *be* | /wɜː/ strong · /wə/ weak | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/were | ≈#90 (of *be*) | US/UK |
| bird | a feathered animal that lays eggs | /bɜːd/ UK · /bɜːrd/ US | https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/bird | ≈#1500 | US/UK |

> **Verification note:** The strong /ɜː/ vs weak /ə/ pair is the standard
> Cambridge strong/weak-form contrast documented for `her`, `were`, `her`,
> `sir`, `fur`. Vietnamese-English frequency lists (NativeX, Scribd "936 từ vựng
> thông dụng") record `her /hɜːr/` and `were /wɜːr/` for the stressed forms.
> `bird` /bɜːd/–/bɜːrd/ is the standard /ɜː/ lexical example.
