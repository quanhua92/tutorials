5Writing task

Write a debate position: one claim + evidence + one rebuttal of the opposing view. Then reveal the model answer.

Model (claim → evidence → rebut the other side):

Claim: I'd argue that a four-day work week improves output, not reduces it.

Evidence: The evidence suggests that rested teams make fewer errors. For example, the Iceland trials showed no drop in productivity.

Rebut the other side: I see the objection that client coverage would shrink, however staggered shifts solve that without losing the benefit.

Note: strong stance verb for the claim (argue), hedged verb for the evidence (suggests, never proves).

6L1 pitfalls & cheat sheet

Vietnamese → English pitfalls

Vietnamese trap (what you do)English fix (what to do instead)
Avoids debate to keep harmony (giữ hòa khí) OR escalates emotionally / makes it personalUse the four-move arc (claim → evidence → rebut → counter). The structure is the calm. Attack the idea, never the person.
States opinion with no evidence — "I think it's bad, full stop"Attach a because: claim + "The evidence suggests…" / "For example…" in the same turn.
Leads the rebuttal with "No" / "But you're wrong" — skips the concessionAcknowledge-then-rebut: lead with "I see your point, however…" or "That's true, but…".
Over-direct disagreement — "You are wrong", "That's stupid", bare "I disagree"Soften with the pivot frame or "Let me push back a little…". Bare "I disagree" sounds aggressive.
Takes disagreement personally — reads "however" as a rejection of youReframe: in English "however" attacks the argument, not the speaker. Receive it neutrally.
Argues only from one locked position — never steelmans / never uses "devil's advocate"Use "Let me play devil's advocate…" to test the opposite without committing. Signals intellectual honesty.
Hedges the claim but over-claims the evidence — "I think X" then "research proves"Invert: strong verb for the claim (I'd argue), hedged verb for evidence (suggests, never proves).
Drops finals on load-bearing words — "poin" for point, "eviden" for evidence, "rebutta-luh" for rebuttalRelease every final consonant — the /t/ in point, /s/ in evidence, /tl/ in rebuttal. A dropped final on the pivot word collapses the move.
Pro-drop / missing subject — "Think is good"Supply the subject + stance verb: "I'd argue that it's good." The debate needs an explicit I owning the claim.
Rushes past the comma before however — concession vanishesHonour the comma pause: I see your point [beat] however…. The pause is the audible "I heard you".
